## 2. Partnership Structure

### 2.1 Founding Partners and Roles

| Partner | Title | Domain |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Gbitse Barrow | Co-Founder & Chief Content and Country Officer | Content design and development; Nigerian country operations; B2B business development; market access; country authority; primary public face in Nigeria |
| Sanju Goswami | Co-Founder & Chief Technology Officer | Platform and app design and development; AI build and integration; infrastructure maintenance and upgrade |
| Mark Otis | Co-Founder & Chief Partnership Officer | Governance; US strategic educational partnerships; capital raising; investors; DFI relationships |

### 2.2 Equity Split

- **30% Gbitse Barrow**
- **30% Sanju Goswami**
- **30% Mark Otis**
- **10% option pool** reserved at formation

Equity represents the long-term structural stake: governance, decision rights, and claim at a sale or exit. It is explicitly NOT the mechanism for compensating unequal short-term contribution -- that asymmetry is carried entirely by role fees.

### 2.3 Vesting

- **Schedule:** 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff, monthly thereafter (all three founders, identical terms)
- **Acceleration:** Double-trigger on a sale of the company
- **IP assignment:** Founder IP created for or contributed to the JV assigns at signing, regardless of vesting status
- **Pre-existing IP:** Unrelated pre-existing IP stays with each founder; contributed pre-existing assets (including Efiko content) enter on separately agreed terms (Section 5.10)
- **Contribution schedules:** Each founder's expected contribution is documented in a contribution schedule attached to the founders' agreement: named deliverables, a minimum time commitment, and review checkpoints aligned to the 90-day sprint gates

### 2.4 Governance

**Board:** Three seats, one per founding partner.

**Decision Rights Matrix:**

| Decision Domain | Owner | Notes |
|----------------|-------|-------|
| Content decisions | Gbitse Barrow | Curriculum, course calendar, SME validation standards, content QA |
| Platform and technical decisions | Sanju Goswami | Architecture, AI tooling, infrastructure, security, NDPA platform compliance |
| Country-level operations | Gbitse Barrow | Staffing, local contracts, B2B sales in Nigeria, government relationships; Nigeria-First Covenant authority |
| Pricing and revenue model | Joint (all three) | Requires consensus; directly impacts mission alignment and learner access |
| Credential partnerships | Gbitse (Nigerian institutions) / Mark (global bodies, agreements) + joint approval | Nigerian institutional outreach led by Gbitse under Nigeria-First Covenant; global certification bodies and partnership agreement structure led by Mark; terms require joint approval |
| Capital raises and investor relationships | Mark Otis + joint approval | Mark leads; material raises are a reserved matter |
| Geographic expansion | Supermajority (2 of 3) | Formal vote before commitment or expenditure |
| Learner data monetization | Unanimous (all three) | No unilateral data commercialization |
| New hires and option grants | Joint (all three) | Option grants require joint approval |

**Reserved Matters -- Supermajority (2 of 3):**
- Any Geographic Expansion Decision
- Capital raises consistent with the mission framework
- Annual budget above the agreed cap
- Market sub-partner, reseller, and channel deals

**Reserved Matters -- Unanimous (all 3):**
- Monetization or transfer of learner data to third parties
- Sale or dissolution of the JV; relocation or restructuring of the Nigerian operating subsidiary
- Amendment of the founders' agreement or mission clauses (Section 5.11)
- Any equity issuance outside the option pool
- Any action diluting a founding partner below a **20% floor**

### 2.5 Deadlock Mechanics

1. **Status quo prevails** -- domain owners decide inside their lanes
2. Deadlocked joint matters get a **30-day cooling period** and one structured mediation session
3. A deadlocked reserved matter is **not acted on** -- nobody can force an expansion, a raise, or a data decision
4. **Terminal deadlock** (continued operation impossible) triggers a buy-sell:
   - Valuation by independent appraiser, jointly appointed
   - Failing agreement: each side appoints one appraiser; those two appoint a third whose determination binds
   - Price payable over **12-24 months** (consistent with departure mechanics)
   - Process completes within **120 days** of trigger
   - **No shotgun mechanism** (expressly avoided; favors whichever founder has the deepest pockets)

### 2.6 Mission Governance (Section 5.11)

SABIficate is explicitly not a venture-scale growth company. The founders' agreement includes mission-protection clauses governing:
- Which capital can be accepted and on what mission-compatibility conditions
- NGN pricing floors that must be maintained to preserve learner access
- Decision thresholds that trigger a mission-alignment review

## 3. Entity Structure

### 3.1 Dual-Entity Arrangement

**US Holding Entity:**
- **Type:** Delaware LLC with a pre-agreed conversion trigger to a C-Corp at an institutional priced round or DFI requirement
- **Holds:** Platform IP, AI tooling, content IP
- **Functions:** Primary interface for impact investors and DFIs; manages capital structure and governance; all three founding partners hold equity at this level

**Nigerian Operating Subsidiary:**
- **Type:** CAC-registered Ltd (Corporate Affairs Commission)
- **Holds:** Local contracts, employer relationships, NGN transactions, NDPA compliance, data domicile
- **Functions:** Employs the Nigerian SME content team; handles NGN-denominated transactions; primary legal entity for NDPA compliance and data domicile; the public face of the platform in Nigeria with country authority under the Nigeria-First Covenant
- **Ownership:** Shares held by the US holding entity (cap-table decision, not a primacy decision). If Nigerian practice or counsel requires a second shareholder of record, that holding is a documented nominee arrangement for the US holding entity, not an economic stake.

### 3.2 Platform License

The platform license from the US entity to the Nigerian subsidiary must be priced at arm's length, with tax counsel on transfer pricing in both jurisdictions before signing.

### 3.3 Dissolution Protections

If the partnership dissolves:
- The Nigerian operating subsidiary retains a **perpetual, royalty-free license** to operate the platform in Nigeria on the version existing at dissolution
- Learner data remains with the Nigerian operating subsidiary

### 3.4 Registration Path

Counsel to confirm the foreign-participation path: NIPC (Nigerian Investment Promotion Commission), business permit, minimum capital, CCI (Certificate of Capital Importation).

## 4. Economics

### 4.1 Role and Operating Fees

- Fees attach to **roles**, not individuals or nationality
- Three defined roles: country general management, platform maintenance and development, governance and capital functions
- **No fees accrue pre-revenue** -- fees activate when revenue or grant funding covers them
- Fees **rank ahead of profit distributions**; profit distributions follow equal equity (equal thirds)
- Amounts set **annually by joint approval**
- **NGN-denominated** for Nigerian-subsidiary roles; **USD** for US-entity roles
- Scheduled FX review included

### 4.2 Bridge Capital

- SABIficate runs lean: personal time, AI-accelerated production, and goodwill relationships substitute for cash wherever possible
- Monthly cash burn estimated at the founding working session
- Founders should budget **12-18 months** of cash burn before first grant or revenue receipt
- Bridge funding contributed in **equal thirds** (matching equal ownership)
- **In-kind contributions** credited at rates pre-agreed in the founders' agreement
- **Contribution default** (cash or in-kind, measured against contribution schedules) cures through **formula-based dilution** of the defaulting founder, **not expulsion**

### 4.3 Capital Importation Note

Foreign capital into the Nigerian subsidiary needs a Certificate of Capital Importation (CCI) at the time of inflow. Without it, repatriating dividends or the arm's-length platform license fee through official channels is severely constrained. This affects how bridge capital (Section 5.4) is routed.

### 4.4 Cap Table Discipline

The cap table stays tight. Non-founder contributors are paid in cash, fees, royalties, commission, or revenue share -- not equity:

| Role / Contribution | Payment Method | Equity? |
|---------------------|---------------|---------|
| Founding partners (3) | Equity plus role fees; vesting with cliff | Yes |
| Regional / sub-partners, NGO channels, resellers | Revenue share or commission with term, cap, or renegotiation trigger | No |
| Content / curriculum contributors | Fixed fees or per-course royalties | No |
| Advisors who move the needle | Small vesting grant ~0.1-0.5% from the option pool | Tiny |
| Rare strategic long-term addition | Equity from the pool, only with all three founders agreeing | Yes, rare |

### 4.5 Option Pool and Secondary Equity

- **10% option pool** reserved at formation; dilution pre-agreed and shared evenly across three founders
- **Advisor equity:** From pool only, **5% aggregate sub-cap**
- **Earn-in requirement:** Equity earned against named, signed institutional agreements within **12 months** of JV formation. A "closed deal" means a signed agreement with an identifiable institutional partner -- not an introduction or letter of intent
- **Unearned equity** at the end of the earn-in window **reverts to the pool**
- **Institutional credential partners:** Revenue share or per-credential royalty with a defined term and cap, **not equity**. Many professional bodies and universities cannot hold equity. An equity exception for an institution requires all three founders.
- **Capital contributors:** Enter through a SAFE or convertible note converting at a future priced round; stay off the cap table until a real valuation exists

### 4.6 Capital Strategy

- Investment thesis positioned for **impact investors and development finance institutions** (not traditional VC)
- Priority targets: IFC, African Development Bank, Omidyar Network, Mastercard Foundation, regional impact funds active in Nigerian ed-tech and workforce development
- Revenue-funded growth is the operating default
- Impact capital accelerates platform investment and geographic expansion; does not gate them
- Grant programs in African-language AI and workforce development to be tracked for future cycles

### 4.7 Unit Economics (to be determined at session)

- NGN-denominated ACV per seat
- Gross margin
- CAC
- Payback period
- All on a single page; Gbitse drafts, pricing decisions remain joint

### 4.8 Payment Rails (default, to be confirmed)

- **Individual payments:** Paystack
- **B2B contracts:** Direct invoicing
- **Multi-year NGN contracts:** FX review clause required
- Alternatives mentioned: Flutterwave, telco carrier billing

## 5. IP and Data Ownership

### 5.1 What the JV Owns

1. **Platform architecture and codebase** -- owned by the US holding entity, licensed to the Nigerian subsidiary for delivery. From the first commit, all code resides in a JV-owned repository and runs on JV-controlled infrastructure accounts; no founder holds sole credentials to repositories, hosting, or domains.
2. **AI-assisted content production workflow** -- including process, tooling, and institutional knowledge for rapid curriculum production using AI with SME oversight.
3. **Learner performance data** -- legally owned by the Nigerian operating subsidiary as data controller; domiciled in Nigeria in compliance with NDPA 2023; US holding entity holds defined usage rights under an intercompany data processing agreement. No partner may license, monetize, or transfer learner data to third parties without unanimous consent. If the JV dissolves, learner data remains with the Nigerian subsidiary.
4. **Credentialing partnerships** -- owned by the JV and governed jointly.

### 5.2 Efiko Content

### 5.3 Founder IP Assignment

- Assignment covers IP created for or contributed to the JV
- Unrelated pre-existing IP stays with each founder
- Contributed assets (Efiko content) enter on Section 5.10 terms
- NDA and IP assignment must be signed before content work begins
- Content produced by the Nigerian SME team under the JV belongs to the JV entity

### 5.4 Platform IP

- Platform IP, AI tooling, and content production workflow owned by the US holding entity
- Licensed to the Nigerian subsidiary at arm's length with transfer-pricing counsel
- **Perpetual, royalty-free Nigeria license survives any dissolution**

### 5.5 Interim Contributor Letter (Pre-Formation IP)

- All work product created for the Project during the pre-formation period (code, curriculum, course materials, brand/design assets, documents, data models) is assigned to the Company upon formation
- Until formation, the contributor holds Project Work Product in trust and grants founding partners a non-exclusive license for Project purposes
- Pre-existing materials excluded; contributed pre-existing materials (including Efiko content) enter only on separately agreed terms
- **Withdrawal:** Any signatory may withdraw before formation by written notice, without financial or commercial liability. On withdrawal, the assignment lapses except for material already integrated into shared deliverables (remains licensed for Project use).
- **Sunset:** If founders' agreement not signed within **6 months**, the letter terminates and assignment lapses for everyone; each contributor retains ownership of their own work. Founding partners may extend by unanimous written consent.
- **Governing law:** State of Delaware

## 6. Nigeria-First Covenant (Full Terms)

The Nigeria-First Covenant is a non-economic recognition mechanism. Its complete terms:

1. **Nigeria is the founding market.** The Nigerian operating subsidiary is the legal owner of learner data and customer contracts and is the public face of the platform in Nigeria.

2. **Country lead authority.** The country lead (Gbitse Barrow) holds decision authority over anything touching:
   - Nigerian government relations
   - Regulatory standing
   - Cultural and political adaptation
   - Public representation in Nigeria

3. **Sign-off requirement.** No founder makes commitments affecting the Nigerian market without country-lead sign-off.

4. **Cultural test.** Product, content, and go-to-market decisions are tested against Nigerian cultural, political, and market realities before commitment.

5. **Compensation mechanism.** Compensation for the operating burden of running the Nigerian market flows through role fees (Section 5.3), applied by role rather than nationality. **No market premium or preferential distribution attaches to any founder.**

6. **Credential outreach.** Nigerian institutional outreach for credentialing is led by Gbitse Barrow under this Covenant. Outreach to Nigerian credential institutions is led by Gbitse; global certification bodies and partnership agreement structure are led by Mark.

7. **Geographic expansion.** Any extension beyond Nigeria constitutes a Geographic Expansion Decision requiring supermajority approval (2 of 3). When expansion into a new country is approved, the governance model for that country mirrors the Nigerian structure: a local operating partner with defined economics, decision rights over content and country operations, and a founding-partner-equivalent authority in that market.

8. **Drift safeguard.** The Covenant is explicitly guarded against drifting into informal preferential economics. All compensation asymmetry runs through role fees, reviewed annually by joint approval.

## 7. Exit and Departure Mechanics

### 7.1 Good Leaver

**Triggers:** Death, disability, removal without cause.
- **Keeps** vested shares
- Company holds an **option to repurchase at fair value**

### 7.2 Bad Leaver

**Triggers:** Cause, material breach, or abandonment.
- **Abandonment defined as:** 90 days of material nonperformance measured against the founder's contribution schedule, after written notice and a 30-day cure opportunity
- **Forfeits** unvested equity
- **Sells** vested equity at the **lower of cost or fair value**

### 7.3 Buyback Terms

- Payable over **12-24 months** so a departure never forces a cash crisis
- Right of first refusal to company, then founders pro rata

### 7.4 No-Liability Exit Principle

**Departure consequences are carried entirely in equity treatment.** No founder owes damages, repayment, or any personal financial obligation for choosing to leave, at any stage, absent:
- Fraud
- IP misappropriation
- Breach of confidentiality

This structure exists to prevent future misunderstanding, not to lock anyone in. Participation is in good faith and exit is always priced in equity, never in liability.

### 7.5 Contribution Default (Distinct from Departure)

A contribution default (cash or in-kind, measured against contribution schedules) cures through **formula-based dilution** of the defaulting founder, **not expulsion**.

(See Section 2.5 above for full mechanics.)

## 8. Legal Concerns Flagged

### 8.1 Resolved by Founders (Confirm in Drafting)

1. **Learner data ownership:** Nigerian subsidiary is sole legal owner and NDPA data controller; US holding entity holds defined usage rights under intercompany DPA. Counsel drafts the DPA and confirms NDPA cross-border transfer mechanics.
2. **LLC equity mechanics:** Delaware LLC stands. Counsel translates option pool, vesting, repurchase, and SAFE provisions into membership-unit and profits-interest equivalents. C-Corp conversion trigger unchanged.
3. **20% dilution floor:** Operates as a per-founder consent right on any raise large enough to breach it. Draft deliberately, not as boilerplate anti-dilution.
4. **Nigerian subsidiary ownership:** Wholly owned by holding entity; any second shareholder of record is a documented nominee. Counsel confirms foreign-participation path (NIPC, business permit, minimum capital, CCI) at formation.

### 8.2 Entity, Tax, and Cross-Border Questions for Counsel

1. **Capital importation and repatriation:** Foreign capital into the Nigerian subsidiary needs a CCI at time of inflow. Without it, repatriating dividends or the arm's-length platform license fee through official channels is severely constrained.
2. **No US-Nigeria tax treaty:** A pass-through Delaware LLC with a Nigerian-resident member creates US filing and withholding obligations and double-taxation exposure. Confirm member-level treatment alongside transfer-pricing work.
3. **Role fee characterization:** Are founders employees or contractors, of which entity, under which payroll regime (PAYE in Nigeria; self-employment in US)? Assess permanent-establishment risk from US entity's activities in Nigeria.

### 8.3 Missing Protections

1. **Restrictive covenants:** Confidentiality, non-solicitation, and a scoped non-compete for founders. Enforceability standards differ between Delaware and Nigeria; draft per jurisdiction.
2. **Anti-corruption compliance:** Government agencies and state programs are named target customers; DFIs will require it. FCPA and Nigerian law anti-bribery policy and sanctions screening should exist before first B2G outreach, not at diligence.
3. **Efiko temporary contracting:** Any contract signed under the existing Efiko entity "to shorten time to contract" needs assignability clauses, or novation costs land later. Define which contract types may use the temporary entity.
4. **Intercompany data agreement:** Cross-border transfer of learner data from Nigeria to the US entity (analytics, AI tooling) requires NDPA-compliant safeguards. The DPIA budget exists but the intercompany data processing agreement is unmentioned.

### 8.4 Arbitration

- **Default:** UNCITRAL rules, sole arbitrator, seated in London, English language
- **Rationale:** New York Convention venue, enforceable in both Nigeria and the US
- **Subject to counsel confirmation** -- counsel may propose Lagos (LACIAC) or LCIA if better suited
- **Nigerian-subsidiary contracts** governed by Nigerian law

### 8.5 Parallel Items (Not for Counsel, But Before Signing)

1. **Legal budget as its own line item:** Formation across two jurisdictions, transfer pricing, and founders' agreement carry real cost even where goodwill/pro bono covers part. Treat as its own budget line, not absorbed in operating burn.
2. **Operating mechanics:** Banking and signature authority, expense approval thresholds, information and audit rights among founders, D&O/cyber insurance once revenue contracts exist.
3. **DPO designation:** Designate the NDPA Data Protection Officer by name and start the DPIA clock. Both are launch gates; the 90-day sprint timeline consumes float quickly.

## 9. Brand/Trademark Analysis

### 9.1 Current Status
- `.com` and `.org` domains are secured
- No USPTO registration found
- No visible conflicting registration surfaced in searches

### 9.2 Verdict
**Conditionally viable.** Strong strategic asset for a Nigeria-first B2B skills platform, but viability depends on resolving two issues before launch: trademark registrability and a partially occupied social/handle namespace.

### 9.3 Strengths

1. **Name is the value proposition.** "Sabificate" (sabi + certificate) already circulates in Nigerian discourse as shorthand for demonstrated competence over paper qualification, with blog and media usage dating to at least 2015.
2. **Cultural authenticity and memorability.** Pidgin-rooted, instantly pronounceable to the Nigerian workforce, unlike generic edtech naming. Hard for foreign entrants to copy credibly.
3. **Existing concept gravity.** "Certificate vs sabificate" is a live debate in Nigerian career and entrepreneurship content. SEO and content marketing can ride an existing conversation.
4. **B2B framing advantage.** "We issue sabificates, not certificates" maps directly to analytics and time-to-productivity pitch.

### 9.4 Risks

1. **Crowded "Sabi" namespace.** Sabi (sabi.am) is a ~$300M-valuation Nigerian B2B platform (took layoffs, pivoted in 2025). SabiTeach, Sabi Learn, SabiMONI all operate in Nigerian education/training. Risk of confusion and inheriting negative press association at the prefix level.
2. **Prior users of the exact name.** Instagram account @sabificate ("the sabificate Institute"), SABIFICATE.ng Facebook page (Abuja), Skill For All's SABIFICATE advocacy campaign. None appears to be a scaled commercial operation, but key social handles are taken and prior local use could support a passing-off claim or opposition during Nigerian trademark prosecution.
3. **Genericness risk.** The word functions as a category term in Nigerian usage (like "know-how"). Trademark may be deemed descriptive and weak. Exclusivity hard to enforce; competitors can use "sabificate" descriptively.
4. **Limited portability.** Outside anglophone West Africa the name carries no meaning and invites spelling errors. Expansion to East or Francophone Africa or international NGO procurement would need a sub-brand or explanation.
5. **Register perception.** Pidgin-derived branding may read as informal to some government and compliance-oriented buyers (mitigable with visual identity and credential rigor).

### 9.5 Recommended Actions

1. Commission a Nigerian Trademarks Registry search through a local agent (registry not reliably searchable online) and **file in Classes 9, 41, and 42 immediately** (Nigeria is effectively first-to-file).
2. Contact or monitor @sabificate Instagram and SABIFICATE.ng accounts; acquire handles or settle on a consistent variant (e.g., @getsabificate, @sabificateHQ) before public launch.
3. Consider positioning "Sabificate" as both company and credential name ("earn your Sabificate"), converting the genericness weakness into product language (every credential issued = a brand impression).
4. Reserve a neutral umbrella name for non-Nigerian expansion now, while it is cheap.

## Appendix B: Risks and Safeguards

| Risk | Safeguard |
|------|-----------|
| Founder misalignment or deadlock among three equal owners | Status-quo default, mediation step, and terminal buy-sell mechanism defined before signing |
| Nigeria-First Covenant drifting into informal preferential economics | Keep recognition non-economic; all compensation asymmetry runs through role fees, reviewed annually by joint approval |
| Vesting forcing a founder out and costing unvested equity unfairly | Good-leaver, bad-leaver, no-cause exit, and acceleration on sale defined in the agreement |
| Revenue share to outside partners eating the lean margin | Model lifetime cost vs. one-time grants; cap or time-limit every deal |
| The word "partner" creating a legal partnership unintentionally | Call revenue-share collaborators contractors, affiliates, or resellers in contracts |
| IP leakage from contributors or founders | Explicit IP assignment in every agreement, founders included, executed at signing |
| Cross-border tax, currency, and enforceability risk | Delaware governing law; UNCITRAL arbitration seated in London; tax counsel on transfer pricing before signing |
| Mission and profit expectations diverging later | Mission-protection clauses (Section 5.11) agreed in writing |

## Appendix E: NDPA Compliance Requirements

- Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) required before deploying AI systems that process Nigerian resident data through automated decision-making or large-scale profiling
- Data Protection Officer (DPO) must be designated before launch
- Learners must be able to access the full educational platform without opting into behavioral profiling
- Any profiling with significant effects on individuals requires human intervention rights
- Budget for upfront legal counsel, DPIA, and DPO designation set at working session
- Cross-border transfer of learner data from Nigeria to US entity requires NDPA-compliant safeguards
