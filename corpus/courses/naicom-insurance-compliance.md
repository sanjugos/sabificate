## Corpus Summary

This corpus covers the full regulatory compliance landscape for Nigeria's insurance industry, anchored in the landmark Nigerian Insurance Industry Reform Act (NIIRA) 2025 signed into law on 5 August 2025. The corpus addresses the NAICOM recapitalization exercise with its July 30, 2026 deadline (requiring life insurers to hold N10B, non-life N15B, reinsurance N35B minimum capital), the expansion of compulsory insurance classes from 6 to 11, the 60-day claims settlement mandate with compound interest penalties, the new Insurance Policyholders' Protection Fund (0.25% of net premiums), the Road Safety and Accident Victims Compensation Fund (N2M medical compensation for uninsured accident victims), NAICOM's corporate governance guidelines (7-15 board members, six mandatory committees, 75% attendance requirement), risk-based supervision and capital frameworks, NAICOM's insurtech guidelines effective August 2025, AML/KYC requirements including mandatory NIN-linking of all policies by April 2026, and the prohibition of composite insurance operations (5-year restructuring window). The corpus includes professional body requirements from CIIN and NCRIB, 7 Nigerian case studies (Niger Insurance liquidation, Standard Alliance license revocation, 12 firms in recapitalization difficulty), 8 textbooks and reference works, 7 existing courses/syllabi, and a proposed 3-module/12-lesson structure mapping to all sources.

## 1. Primary Regulatory Sources

### 1.1 Nigerian Insurance Industry Reform Act (NIIRA) 2025
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (signed by President Bola Ahmed Tinubu)
- **Date:** 2025-08-05
- **Relevance:** Landmark legislation repealing and consolidating the Insurance Act 2003, Marine Insurance Act, Motor Vehicles (Third-Party Insurance) Act, and other prior insurance statutes into a single comprehensive framework. Introduces sweeping reforms across capital requirements, consumer protection, compulsory insurance expansion, and regulatory alignment with international standards.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Reclassifies insurance from "life and general" to "life and non-life"
  - Prohibits composite insurance operations (5-year compliance window for existing composite insurers)
  - Introduces risk-based capital (RBC) requirements: minimum capital or RBC, whichever is higher
  - Minimum share capital: Life N10B, Non-life N15B, Composite N25B, Reinsurance N35B
  - 12-month transition window for capital compliance (deadline: July 30, 2026)
  - Expands compulsory insurance classes from 6 to 11
  - 60-day claims settlement deadline with compound interest penalties at prevailing bank rate
  - Establishes Insurance Policyholders' Protection Fund (0.25% of gross premiums)
  - Establishes Road Safety and Accident Victims Compensation Fund (0.5% of motor underwriting profit)
  - Creates new insurance classes: energy insurance (oil, gas, power), agricultural insurance, annuity
  - Penalties: N25M fine / 2 years imprisonment for unlicensed individual operations; N50M for companies
  - Requires NIN linking for all insurance policies (Section 64(4))
  - Integrates ECOWAS Brown Card Scheme for cross-border motor accident compensation
  - Requires fully audited financial statements (replacing balance sheets/P&L accounts)
  - 14-day statutory timeline for regulatory approval of financial statements (deemed approved if no response)
  - Annual audited financial statements due by June 30; quarterly returns within 10 days of quarter-end
  - Publication of financials in minimum two national newspapers
- **Reference:** https://naicom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NIIRA-2025.pdf
- **Secondary sources:**
  - https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/insurance-laws-and-products/1745894/some-key-provisions-in-the-nigeria-insurance-industry-reform-act-2025
  - https://www.forvismazars.com/ng/en/insights/publications/local-insights/niira-2025-reforms
  - https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/insurance-laws-and-products/1670184/regulatory-update-the-nigerian-insurance-industry-reform-act-2025-navigating-compliance

### 1.2 Insurance Act, 2003 (No. 1 of 2003) [REPEALED by NIIRA 2025]
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2003
- **Relevance:** Former primary insurance legislation, now repealed by NIIRA 2025. Understanding this Act remains important for historical context, transition provisions, and interpreting legacy insurance contracts. Governed licensing, operations, capital requirements, and the six original compulsory insurance classes.
- **Key Provisions (Historical):**
  - Divided insurance into Life and General classes (Section 2(1))
  - Capital requirements: Life N150M, General N200M, Composite N350M (substantially increased in 2007)
  - Section 79: Professional Code of Conduct requirement for all registered insurers, reinsurers, agents, brokers, and loss adjusters
  - Six compulsory insurance classes established
  - Regulatory framework superseded by NIIRA 2025
- **Reference:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373797587_Insurance_Regulation_in_Nigeria_A_Review_of_Differences_in_Provisions_between_the_1997_Insurance_Decree_and_the_2003_Insurance_Act

### 1.3 National Insurance Commission (NAICOM) Act, 1997 (Cap. N53 LFN 2004)
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (Decree No. 1 of 1997)
- **Date:** 1997
- **Relevance:** Establishing legislation for NAICOM as Nigeria's insurance regulatory authority. Provides the foundational mandate for insurance supervision.
- **Key Functions:**
  - Effective administration, supervision, regulation and control of insurance business in Nigeria
  - Establishing standards for the conduct of insurance business
  - Approving rates of insurance premiums for all classes
  - Ensuring adequate protection of strategic Government assets
  - Regulating transactions between insurers and reinsurers (domestic and cross-border)
  - Advising the Federal Government on insurance-related matters
  - Approving standards, conditions and warranties for all insurance classes
  - Protecting insurance policyholders, beneficiaries and third parties
- **Reference:** https://naicom.gov.ng/about-naicom/

### 1.4 NAICOM Corporate Governance Guidelines for Insurance and Reinsurance Companies (CGGIRC), 2021
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2021
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive corporate governance framework aligned with the Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance (NCCG) 2018. Mandates governance standards for all insurance and reinsurance companies operating in Nigeria.
- **Key Requirements:**
  - Board composition: 7-15 members; no more than 40% in executive capacity
  - Board meeting attendance: minimum 75% annually per director
  - At least one annual board training session required
  - Board evaluation results reported to NAICOM by end of Q1 each year
  - Six mandatory board committees: Audit, Risk Management, Nomination & Governance, Remuneration, Compliance, Finance/Investment/General Purpose
  - Annual reports must disclose compliance/deviations from NCCG 2018 and CGGIRC 2021
  - Directors' meeting attendance records must be disclosed
  - Guideline 11: Whistleblowing framework — operators must report imprudent practices to NAICOM within 5 working days; anti-retaliation protections for whistleblowers
- **Reference:** https://www.corpgovnigeria.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/NAICOM-CORPORATE-GOVERNANCE-GUIDELINES.pdf

### 1.5 NAICOM Recapitalization Directives (2025-2026)
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2025-08 through 2026-07
- **Relevance:** Implementation framework for NIIRA 2025 capital requirements. Defines the structured timeline, verification process, and consequences of non-compliance.
- **Key Details:**
  - Minimum capital requirements: Life N10B, Non-life N15B, Composite N25B, Reinsurance N35B
  - Previous requirements: Life N2B, General N3B, Composite N5B, Reinsurance N10B
  - Increases: Life 400%, General 400%, Composite 400%, Reinsurance 250%
  - Industry-wide capital shortfall estimated at N132.5 billion
  - Timeline: Recapitalization plans due September 30, 2025; capital verification November 2025 - June 2026; final deadline July 30, 2026
  - 18 insurance companies reported ready for capital verification as of November 2025
  - 12 insurance firms identified as being "in tight corner" as of April 2026
  - Non-compliance consequences: license withdrawal, forced consolidation, liquidation
  - NAICOM Commissioner Olusegun Omosehin confirmed no extension or relaxation of deadline
  - Risk-Based Capital (RBC) framework considers: insurance risk, market risk, credit risk, operational risk
  - "Paid-up share capital" terminology replaced with "minimum share capital"
- **References:**
  - https://nairametrics.com/2025/11/27/naicom-18-insurance-companies-now-ready-for-capital-verification/
  - https://businessday.ng/insurance/article/insurers-scramble-to-meet-july-recapitalisation-deadline/
  - https://brandcom.ng/2026/05/18/insurance-brands-race-against-%E2%82%A6132bn-as-july-31-recapitalization-deadline-looms/

### 1.6 NAICOM Prudential Guidelines for Insurance Institutions in Nigeria (Revised October 2022)
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2022-10 (revised)
- **Relevance:** Establishes minimum prudential standards for underwriting, reinsurance, investments, reserving, outsourcing, and intermediary services. Critical for compliance officers managing investment portfolios and financial reserves.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Policyholders' funds shall not be invested offshore
  - Permitted investments include: supranational bonds, Sukuk, infrastructure funds registered with SEC (minimum N5B value), investment properties (capped at 25% of minimum capital requirement)
  - Minimum prudential standards for underwriting practices
  - Reinsurance arrangement guidelines
  - Reserving requirements for outstanding claims
  - Outsourcing risk management framework
- **Reference:** https://naicom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/4_REVISED-PRUDENTIAL-GUIDELINES-FOR-INS-INSTIT-IN-NIG-OCT-2022.pdf

- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** Various (2021-2025)
- **Relevance:** Establishes market conduct standards for insurance operators, including Takaful/Retakaful operators and Insurtech firms. Promotes effective risk management and internal control frameworks.
- **Key Guidelines:**
  - Market Conduct Guidelines for Takaful and Retakaful Insurance Operators
  - Enterprise Risk Management Framework for Takaful and Retakaful operators

### 1.8 NAICOM Guidelines for Insurtech Operations in Nigeria (August 2025)
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2025-08-01 (effective)
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive regulatory framework for technology-driven insurance operations. Establishes licensing, operational boundaries, and compliance requirements for Insurtech firms.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Two categories: Partnering Insurtechs (collaborate with licensed insurers) and Standalone Insurtechs (operate independently)
  - Standalone Insurtechs restricted from underwriting: oil & gas, marine/aviation, retirement life annuities, government asset insurance
  - Registration process: Application stage, Verification stage, Licensing stage
  - License validity: 4 years (unless cancelled or suspended)
  - Regulatory sandbox testing may be required
  - 30-day compliance window for existing operators from effective date
  - First Insurtech licence issued by NAICOM on May 29, 2026
- **Reference:** https://naicom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Guidelines-for-Insurtech-Operations-in-Nigeria.pdf

### 1.9 NAICOM Risk-Based Supervision Framework
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2025 (formalized under NIIRA 2025)
- **Relevance:** Represents a paradigm shift from traditional compliance-focused regulation to risk-proportionate supervision. Allows NAICOM to assess insurers based on their specific risk profiles rather than uniform compliance requirements.
- **Key Features:**
  - Risk-based capital replaces fixed capital requirements (higher of fixed sum or RBC)
  - RBC considers: capital for insurance risk, market risk, credit risk, operational risk
  - Enables early identification of vulnerabilities
  - Allows allocation of supervisory resources based on risk profiles
  - Proactive measures to address emerging risks before they threaten financial stability
  - EY Consulting appointed as NAICOM's consulting actuary for transition
- **Reference:** https://www.nigeriahousingmarket.com/news/naicom-advances-transition-to-risk-based-supervision-appoints-ey-consulting-actuary

### 1.10 NIIRA 2025 Compulsory Insurance Classes (Expanded from 6 to 11)
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria / NAICOM
- **Date:** 2025-08-05
- **Relevance:** Major expansion of mandatory insurance coverage aimed at increasing insurance penetration and protecting vulnerable populations. Each class carries specific penalties for non-compliance.
- **The 11 Compulsory Classes:**
  1. **Motor Third-Party Insurance** (passenger coverage) — All fee-paying passengers insured against death/bodily injury
  2. **Group Life Insurance** — Employer-provided life coverage for employees
  3. **Public Building Insurance** — Multi-storey buildings, hostels, tenements; covers fire, collapse, earthquake, storm, flood, legal liabilities; Penalty: minimum N1M fine or 12 months imprisonment or both; insurers pay 0.25% of net premiums quarterly to Fire Services Maintenance Fund
  4. **Construction Risk Insurance** — Required before construction begins on multi-storey buildings; covers bodily injury, death, loss to workers and public; Penalty: N5M fine or 12 months imprisonment or both
  5. **Employer's Liability Insurance** — Coverage for workplace injuries and occupational hazards
  6. **Healthcare Provider Professional Indemnity Insurance** — All healthcare facilities must maintain coverage; certificate displayed in office
  7. **Aviation Industry Insurance** — Carriers, aerodrome operators, fuel suppliers, ground handling providers; third-party liability and compensation
  8. **Petroleum & Gas Station Third-Party Insurance** — All refilling stations and fuel transport vehicles; covers third-party losses from accidental fire/explosion; Penalty: N1M fine or 2 years imprisonment or both; certificate must be displayed conspicuously
  9. **Credit Life Insurance** — Borrowers obtaining loans exceeding N10M must secure coverage for remaining loan balance upon death/permanent disability; costs transparently disclosed to borrower
  10. **Government Assets & Employee Insurance** — Federal government and agencies must insure all assets and employees against determined hazards
  11. **Marine Cargo Insurance** — Coverage for goods in transit by sea
- **Enforcement Challenge:** Despite existence of compulsory classes since 2003, weak enforcement has historically hindered the industry's potential
- **Reference:** https://punchng.com/key-facts-on-niira-2025-compulsory-insurances/

### 1.11 NAICOM AML/KYC and NIN-Linking Requirements
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2026-04-01 (effective)
- **Relevance:** Enhanced know-your-customer requirements under Section 64(4) of NIIRA 2025 and powers conferred by NAICOM Act 1997. Aligns insurance sector with global AML/CFT standards.
- **Key Requirements:**
  - No insurance cover shall be provided or incepted without obtaining the National Identification Number (NIN) of individual clients
  - Corporate clients must provide CAC (Corporate Affairs Commission) documents
  - Applies to both individual and group insurance policies
  - Every insurance policy must be directly linked to the policyholder's NIN
  - Implementation deadline: April 30, 2026
- **Reference:** https://gazettengr.com/naicom-directs-insurers-to-link-policies-with-nin/

### 1.12 Nigerian Reinsurance Corporation Act / Reinsurance Cession Requirements
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 1977 (original); updated provisions in NIIRA 2025
- **Relevance:** Establishes compulsory reinsurance cession requirements for all Nigerian insurers.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - 5% compulsory cession to African Reinsurance Corporation (Africa Re)
  - 20% compulsory cession to Nigerian Reinsurance Corporation (as of January 1, 1978)
  - Africa Re deals directly with insurance/reinsurance companies operating in Nigeria for the 5% cession
  - NIIRA 2025 reinsurance licensing: minimum capital N35B
- **Reference:** https://www.placng.org/lawsofnigeria/print.php?sn=387

### 1.13 NAICOM Insurance Policyholders' Protection Fund (IPPF) Guidelines
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** 2026-04 (operationalized); established under NIIRA 2025
- **Relevance:** First-ever policyholder protection scheme in Nigeria's insurance sector. Provides a financial safety net for policyholders when insurers become insolvent.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - All insurers and reinsurers must contribute 0.25% of net premium income annually
  - Additional contribution: 0.25% from prior year Security and Insurance Development Fund balances
  - Fund used exclusively for resolving insolvency cases and settling admitted but unpaid claims
  - NAICOM expects to raise over N2B from initial contributions
  - Non-compliance attracts suspension or cancellation of operating licences
  - Insurance Policyholders' Protection Fund Committee inaugurated May 18, 2026
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2026/04/10/naicom-rolls-out-mandatory-policyholders-protection-fund-tightens-insurance-compliance/

### 1.14 NAICOM Complaints and Enforcement Mechanism
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date:** Ongoing
- **Relevance:** NAICOM's complaints resolution and enforcement framework for policyholder protection.
- **Key Data:**
  - 1,571 policyholder complaints reported (as of March 2025 report)
  - Industrial and General Insurance (IGI) Plc recorded highest complaints: 327 cases
  - Complaints span motor insurance, group life, education policies, bonds, annuities, all-risk policies
  - Under NIIRA 2025: insurer with 5+ verified complaints of failure to pay claims promptly risks license cancellation
  - Penalty for non-payment: up to 10 times the amount payable
  - Persistent non-compliance grounds for cancellation of registration
  - N500,000 penalty for late payment beyond 60-day deadline, plus monthly compound interest at prevailing bank rate
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2025/03/13/igi-plc-leads-as-naicom-reports-1571-policyholder-complaints-against-insurers/

### 1.15 NIIRA 2025 Road Safety and Accident Victims Compensation Fund
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria / NAICOM
- **Date:** 2025-08-05 (established under NIIRA 2025)
- **Relevance:** Novel consumer protection mechanism for motor accident victims involving uninsured or unidentified vehicles.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Insurers contribute 0.5% of underwriting profit on motor business to the fund pool
  - Road Safety and Accident Victims Compensation Committee (RSAVCC) established to oversee fund management
  - Up to N2M medical compensation for victims of uninsured vehicle accidents (Section 99)
  - Compensation amount may be reviewed by NAICOM
  - Covers hospital expenses for persons involved in motor accidents by uninsured/unidentified vehicles
  - NAICOM, FRSC, and NHIA forged alliance to boost road safety and insurance compliance under NIIRA 2025
- **Reference:** https://leadership.ng/new-law-offers-n2m-medical-compensation-for-uninsured-accident-victims/

## 4. Nigerian Case Studies

### 4.1 Niger Insurance Plc — Liquidation (2022)
- **Date:** License cancelled June 21, 2022
- **Background:** Niger Insurance Plc was one of Nigeria's oldest insurance companies. The company was struggling to pay policyholders' claims, staff salaries, and carry out other obligations.
- **NAICOM Action:** Cancelled certificate of registration; appointed Sanya Ogunkuade Esq as Receiver/Liquidator
- **Impact:** Approximately 300 staff nationwide were let go. Three years after liquidation, policyholders were still yet to receive compensation for their claims, leaving many frustrated and financially stranded.
- **Lesson for Course:** Demonstrates consequences of capital inadequacy and poor claims management; underscores urgency of NIIRA 2025 recapitalization and the need for the new Policyholders' Protection Fund
- **Reference:** https://www.thecable.ng/investment-gone-wrong-niger-insurance-subscribers-in-limbo-three-years-after-companys-liquidation/

### 4.2 Standard Alliance Insurance Plc — License Revocation and Court Challenge (2022)
- **Date:** License cancelled June 21, 2022
- **Background:** Standard Alliance Insurance Plc had its operating licence revoked simultaneously with Niger Insurance.
- **NAICOM Action:** Cancelled certificate of registration; appointed Kehinde Aina Esq of Aina Blankson LP as Receiver/Liquidator
- **Legal Challenge:** Standard Alliance dragged NAICOM to Federal High Court Lagos in August 2022. Justice Akintayo Aluko ruled the revocation null and void and awarded costs in favor of the plaintiffs.
- **Lesson for Course:** Illustrates the tension between regulatory enforcement powers and judicial review; raises questions about due process in insurance regulation. Demonstrates that NIIRA 2025's strengthened enforcement provisions aim to address these procedural challenges.
- **Reference:** https://punchng.com/liquidation-standard-alliance-drags-naicom-to-court/

### 4.3 The 2006 Insurance Recapitalization — Historical Precedent
- **Date:** 2005-2007
- **Background:** NAICOM imposed massive capital increases: Life from N150M to N2.5B (1,233% increase), General from N200M to N3B (1,400% increase), Reinsurance from N350M to N10B (2,750% increase).
- **Outcome:** Number of insurance companies reduced from 114 to 71 through mergers and consolidation. Notable example: Sovereign Trust Insurance completed a four-way merger absorbing Coral Insurance, Confidence Insurance, and Prime-trust Insurance.
- **Controversy:** Insurance Commissioner Emmanuel Chukwulozie was forced to resign after disputes over the list of recapitalized companies. His successor Fola Daniels completed the exercise amid legal disputes and political interventions.
- **Achievement:** Zero-liquidation consolidation — all companies either merged or recapitalized; none were liquidated.
- **Lesson for Course:** Directly relevant to the current NIIRA 2025 recapitalization cycle. Demonstrates that Nigeria has successfully navigated insurance recapitalization before, but the current exercise involves much larger absolute amounts and a tighter timeline.
- **Reference:** https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/15265940810916111/full/html

### 4.4 12 Insurance Firms in Recapitalization Difficulty (2026)
- **Date:** April 2026
- **Background:** As the July 30, 2026 deadline approaches, 12 insurance firms were identified as being "in tight corner" regarding meeting the new capital requirements.
- **Industry Context:** Industry-wide capital shortfall estimated at N132.5 billion. NAICOM Commissioner Omosehin confirmed no extension of deadline. NAICOM has identified financially weak operators and is engaging them through structured interventions including restructuring, mergers, and acquisitions.
- **Lesson for Course:** Real-time case study of recapitalization challenges; demonstrates the practical compliance pressures facing the industry

### 4.5 NAICOM Crackdown on Fake Motor Insurance (2025-2026)
- **Date:** August 2025 onwards
- **Background:** Widespread sale of fraudulent motor third-party insurance certificates, particularly in the motor insurance segment, eroding public confidence.
- **NAICOM Action:** Partnered with Nigeria Police Force for enforcement of NIIRA 2025. Motor Third-Party Insurance Enforcement Committee conducted raids in Abuja targeting counterfeit policy sellers. ARIAN (Association of Registered Insurance Agents of Nigeria) joined enforcement efforts.
- **Notable Warning:** ARIAN issued public warning against National Cooperative Insurance Society (NCIS) — an unlicensed entity selling insurance products.
- **Penalties Under NIIRA 2025:** Unlicensed agents face up to N500,000 fine, 6 months imprisonment, or both. Insurers transacting with unlicensed agents face penalty of 5 times the premium collected.
- **Lesson for Course:** Demonstrates NIIRA 2025's strengthened enforcement provisions and the importance of dealing only with licensed operators
- **Reference:** https://businessday.ng/news/article/naicom-nigeria-police-begins-crackdown-on-sellers-of-fake-motor-insurance-policies/

### 4.6 IGI Plc — Highest Policyholder Complaints (2025)
- **Date:** March 2025 report
- **Background:** NAICOM released data showing 1,571 policyholder complaints across the industry. Industrial and General Insurance (IGI) Plc led with 327 complaints.
- **Complaint Categories:** Motor insurance, group life, education policies, bonds, annuities, all-risk policies
- **NAICOM Response:** Warned insurers will be held financially responsible for all resolved complaints and must promptly settle claims once disputes are adjudicated.
- **Lesson for Course:** Demonstrates the importance of claims management processes, customer service standards, and the reputational risk of poor complaint handling
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2025/03/13/igi-plc-leads-as-naicom-reports-1571-policyholder-complaints-against-insurers/

### 4.7 NAICOM Warnings of Further Liquidations (2022)
- **Date:** July-August 2022
- **Background:** Following the Niger Insurance and Standard Alliance revocations, NAICOM warned that more insurance companies may be liquidated to "sanitise" the industry. NAICOM also moved against erring firms for unpaid claims.
- **Regulatory Position:** NAICOM explicitly stated it would sanction companies through liquidation where necessary to strengthen the industry, protect policyholders, and prevent the broader contagion effects of weak insurers.
- **Lesson for Course:** Establishes NAICOM's willingness to use its most severe enforcement powers; demonstrates the regulatory environment that led to NIIRA 2025's enhanced consumer protection provisions
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2022/07/29/naicom-warns-more-insurance-companies-may-be-liquidated-to-sanitise-the-industry/

### 4.8 Mergers and Acquisitions Wave (2025-2026)
- **Date:** 2025-2026
- **Background:** The NIIRA 2025 recapitalization deadline has triggered a wave of M&A activity as weaker firms seek lifelines from stronger players. Companies face the stark choice of raising new capital, merging, or facing forced liquidation.
- **Legal Considerations:** Federal Competition and Consumer Protection Commission (FCCPC) approval required for mergers; SEC notification for listed entities; NAICOM holds exclusive approval authority for insurance amalgamations; court intervention limited to cases where NAICOM refuses (with 6-month appeal window).
- **Lesson for Course:** Demonstrates the practical implications of recapitalization requirements and the regulatory processes governing industry consolidation
- **Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1675208/mergers-acquisitions-in-nigeria-in-view-of-recapitalization-in-the-banking-and-insurance-sectors-key-considerations

## 6. Professional Body Requirements

### 6.1 CIIN (Chartered Insurance Institute of Nigeria)
- **Status:** Mandatory membership for all insurance practitioners in Nigeria
- **Established:** By Act of the National Assembly
- **Certification Levels:**

| Level | Credits Required | Designation | Subjects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Certificate | 45 credits | Cert. CIIN | 2 compulsory + 1 optional |
| Diploma | 110 credits | Dip. CIIN | 2 compulsory + optional from Diploma/Advanced Diploma |
| Advanced Diploma | 290 credits | AIIN (Associate) | 3 compulsory + 3 electives |

- **Examination Schedule:** Two diets per year — April and October
- **Credit System:** Diploma units worth 20-25 credits; Advanced Diploma units worth 30-35 credits
- **Entry Requirements:** WASC/GCE O-Level with credits in 4 subjects including English and Mathematics (maximum two sittings). Exemptions available for holders of HND, professional qualifications, and recognized university degrees.
- **CPD Requirements:** Ongoing continuing professional development required for membership maintenance (specific credit requirements set by CIIN Council)
- **Financial Members:** Schedule of financial members maintained and updated (latest published as at November 30, 2025)
- **College of Insurance:** CIFM established as CIIN's training subsidiary, offering mandatory training, diploma courses, and short-term training
- **Contact:** 27 Lagos Street, Ebute-Metta, Lagos; info@ciinigeria.org; 08172040922
- **Reference:** https://ciinigeria.org/

### 6.2 NCRIB (Nigerian Council of Registered Insurance Brokers)
- **Status:** Regulatory body for insurance brokers in Nigeria
- **Individual Membership Requirements:**
  - Certification by Area Committee Chairman
  - 3,000-3,500 word dissertation on insurance broking topic
  - 3,000-3,500 word report on major project involvement
  - Original academic qualifications and professional certifications
  - Updated CV, passport photographs, valid ID
- **Company Registration Requirements:**
  - Must be limited liability company incorporated in Nigeria
  - CEO must hold AIIN, ACII, or equivalent associateship diploma in insurance
- **Training:** Regular workshops and training programs to keep pace with global trends and regulatory requirements
- **Regulatory Role:** Works with NAICOM on broker regulation and enforcement
- **Reference:** https://ncrib.net/

### 6.3 NIA (Nigerian Insurers Association)
- **Status:** Umbrella organization for all insurance companies in Nigeria
- **Current Chairman:** Kunle Ahmed
- **Key Functions:**
  - Industry representation and advocacy
  - Nigerian Insurance Industry Database (NIID) — verification system for all compulsory insurance policies
  - Market development and stakeholder engagement
  - Data compilation and publication
  - Collaboration with regulators on NIIRA 2025 implementation
- **Reference:** https://nigeriainsurers.org/

### 6.4 ARIAN (Association of Registered Insurance Agents of Nigeria)
- **Status:** Professional body for registered insurance agents
- **Key Activities:**
  - Partnering with NAICOM on enforcement within NIIRA 2025 framework
