## 1. Primary Regulatory Sources

### 1.1 Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (signed into law August 7, 2020)
**Full Title:** Companies and Allied Matters Act, 2020 (Act No. 3)
**Full Text:** https://www.cac.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/CAMA-NOTE-BOOK-FULL-VERSION.pdf

**Key Sections on Directors' Duties:**

- **Section 265(6):** Prohibits the same person from holding the offices of CEO and Chairman of a public company.
- **Section 271:** Small companies may have a single director (exception to the two-director minimum).
- **Section 275:** Every public company must have at least three independent directors.
- **Section 284(2):** Director attendance records must be made available to members at general meetings and considered during re-election.
- **Section 305 (Duties of Directors):** Directors owe a fiduciary relationship to the company. Must act in good faith, in the best interests of the company, to preserve assets, further business, and promote corporate purposes.
- **Section 306 (Conflicts of Duties and Interests):** Prohibits conflict of personal interest with official duties. Requires disclosure to the general meeting for a director to escape liability.
- **Section 307 (Multiple Directorships):** Directors must disclose multiple directorships. A person shall not be director in more than five public companies.
- **Section 308 (Duty of Care and Skill):** Directors must act with honesty and care, exercising skills comparable to a reasonably prudent director in similar circumstances. Absence from board deliberations does not relieve responsibility unless justified.
- **Section 330:** Appointment of company secretary optional for small companies.

**Course Relevance:** Foundational for the modules on fiduciary duties, conflicts of interest, and corporate governance obligations. CAMA 2020 codifies directors' ethical obligations and provides the legal basis for personal liability when those obligations are breached.

### 1.2 Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Act 2000 (ICPC Act)

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria / Independent Corrupt Practices and Other Related Offences Commission (ICPC)
**Date:** Enacted June 13, 2000; Commission inaugurated September 29, 2000
**Full Text:** https://www.nfiu.gov.ng/images/Downloads/downloads/ICPC-Act-2000.pdf
**ICPC Overview:** https://icpc.gov.ng/the-icpc-act-at-20-overview-of-the-act/

**Key Provisions:**
- Establishes the ICPC with mandate to receive and investigate reports of corruption, prosecute offenders, examine and review corruption-prone systems and procedures of public bodies, and educate the public against corruption.
- Defines "Public Officer" broadly to include persons employed in public service of Federation/State/LGC, public corporations, or private companies wholly or jointly floated by government or its agency.
- Section 45: Power of ICPC Chairman to place restrictions on bank accounts of suspects.
- All offences under the Act are bailable.

**Course Relevance:** Essential for lessons on anti-corruption frameworks, public sector ethics, and the consequences of corrupt practices. The broad definition of "public officer" means many private sector professionals who work with government contracts fall within scope.

### 1.3 Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (Establishment) Act 2004

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
**Date:** Originally enacted 2002, re-enacted 2004
**Full Text:** https://www.efcc.gov.ng/assets/EFCC_establishment_act-D6hK95Tu.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- EFCC empowered to prevent, investigate, prosecute, and penalize economic and financial crimes.
- Enforces multiple related statutes: Money Laundering Act 1995, Money Laundering (Prohibition) Act 2004, Advance Fee Fraud Act 1995, Failed Banks Act 1994, BOFIA 1991.
- Covers offences relating to financial malpractice, terrorism financing, public officers' misconduct, retention of proceeds of criminal conduct.
- Powers extend to both private and public sectors.
- Forfeiture provisions: assets can be forfeited after conviction, including passport seizure.

**Course Relevance:** Central to modules on consequences of unethical conduct, financial crimes, and enforcement. EFCC is the most active anti-corruption enforcement body in Nigeria with 4,111 convictions in 2024 alone.

### 1.4 Money Laundering (Prevention and Prohibition) Act 2022

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
**Date:** Signed May 17, 2022 (repeals the 2011 Act)
**Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/money-laundering/1233474/key-provisions-in-the-money-laundering-prevention-and-prohibition-act-2022

**Key Provisions:**
- Section 18 expressly prohibits money laundering, defining various constituent offences.
- Prohibits numbered or anonymous bank accounts.
- Mandatory reporting of cross-border fund/security transfers exceeding US$10,000 to NFIU, CBN, and/or SEC within one day.
- Penalties for individuals: imprisonment 4-14 years, or fine of not less than 5x the value of crime proceeds, or both.
- Designated non-financial businesses failing customer identification/reporting face daily fines of N250,000 and potential license revocation.

**Course Relevance:** Relevant to modules on compliance obligations, financial integrity, and the personal liability professionals face for facilitating or ignoring money laundering.

### 1.5 Investment and Securities Act (ISA) 2007 -- Insider Dealing Provisions

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria / Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)
**Full Text:** https://home.sec.gov.ng/documents/6/THE-INVESTMENTS-AND-SECURITIES-ACT-2007_NIGERIA.pdf

**Key Sections:**
- **Section 111:** Prohibition of dealing in securities by insiders -- an insider cannot buy, sell, or deal in company securities if possessing unpublished price-sensitive information.
- **Section 112:** Abuse of information obtained in official capacity.
- **Section 113:** Exemptions -- actions not prohibited by Sections 111 and 112.
- **Section 114:** Transactions in contravention are voidable at the instance of the SEC.
- **Section 115:** Penalties -- individuals face fines of at least N500,000 or double the profit gained/loss avoided, or imprisonment up to 7 years. Corporate entities face fines of at least N1,000,000 or twice the profit.

**Course Relevance:** Core content for modules on insider trading/dealing, confidential information handling, and securities market ethics.

### 1.6 Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance (NCCG) 2018

**Issuing Body:** Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria (FRCN)
**Date:** Published January 15, 2019 (effective immediately)
**Full Text:** https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/advisory/2018-Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance.pdf

**Structure:** 28 principles divided into 7 parts:
- 16 principles on Board of Directors and Officers
- 4 principles on risk management, whistleblowing, and audit
- 3 principles on relationship with shareholders
- 2 principles on ethical conduct of business

**Ethics Provisions:**
- Boards must formulate a Code of Business Conduct and Ethics modeling top-down commitment to ethical standards from Board/management to employees, contractors, and suppliers.
- Ethical policies must monitor conflicts of interest, insider trading, related party transactions, and corrupt activities.
- **Principle 19 (Whistleblowing):** Boards must ensure a reliable, accessible whistleblowing mechanism guaranteeing anonymity. All disclosures treated confidentially. Whistleblowers can disclose violations of law or internal policy. Policy must detail internal/external disclosure recipients.
- **Principle 27 (Stakeholder Communication):** Board must keep stakeholders informed to enable informed decisions.

**Authority:** Sections 11(c) and 41(c) of the Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria Act, 2011.

**Course Relevance:** Framework for corporate governance modules. Replaces all prior sectorial governance codes and provides the national standard for ethical corporate behavior across all sectors.

### 1.7 CBN Corporate Governance Guidelines 2023

**Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
**Date:** Circular issued July 13, 2023; effective August 1, 2023
**Full Text:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/Out/2023/FPRD/Circular%20and%20Guidelines%20for%20Corporate%20Governance.pdf
**Analysis:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1437172/key-highlights-of-the-cbns-code-of-corporate-governance-guidelines-for-banks-and-financial-holding-companies-in-nigeria

**Key Provisions:**
- Applies to Commercial, Merchant, Non-Interest, and Payment Service Banks plus Financial Holding Companies.
- Requires banks to create a Code of Business Conduct and Ethics covering legal obligations, stakeholder expectations, and individual accountability.
- INED requirement increased from 2 to 3 for major banks.
- At least 2 Non-Executive Directors (1 INED) must have technology/cybersecurity expertise.
- Three primary objectives: supplement NCCG 2018 with banking-specific guidance, define governance standards for banks, promote high ethical standards.

**Course Relevance:** Banking-sector-specific governance and ethics requirements. Critical for professionals in financial services.

### 1.8 Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act (BOFIA) 2020

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
**Date:** Signed November 12, 2020

**Key Ethics Provisions:**
- Section 5(6): Personal liability on directors, managers, and officers for failure to comply with banking license conditions.
- Tightens incentive structures for compliance and penalizes bank office-holders more severely for regulatory breaches.
- Requires compliance with prudential ratios and enhanced disclosure.
- Addressed gaps exploited for insider abuse and unethical conduct that increased NPLs.

**Course Relevance:** Important for understanding personal liability of banking professionals and the regulatory consequences of ethics failures.

### 1.9 Code of Conduct for Public Officers (Fifth Schedule, 1999 Constitution)

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (Constitution)
**Enabling Legislation:** Code of Conduct Bureau and Tribunal Act (Chapter C15, Laws of Federation 2004)
**Reference:** https://nigerian-constitution.com/fifth-schedule-part-1-code-of-conduct-for-public-officers-general/

**Key Provisions:**
- Section 5: "A Public Officer shall not put himself in a position where his personal interest conflicts with his duties and responsibilities."
- Prohibits giving and receiving bribes, abuse of public office, operation of private foreign accounts.
- Asset declaration within 3 months of taking office and every 4 years thereafter.
- Gifts given to public officials in the course of duties are strictly prohibited.
- Code of Conduct Bureau receives and investigates breach allegations.
- Code of Conduct Tribunal adjudicates cases.

**Course Relevance:** Foundational for public sector ethics, conflict of interest, and gift/gratification modules. The constitutional anchoring gives these provisions supreme legal force.

### 1.10 Federal Government Whistleblowing Policy (2016)

**Issuing Body:** Federal Ministry of Finance
**Date:** Launched December 21, 2016
**Status:** Policy only (no statutory backing); Whistleblower Protection Bill still pending in National Assembly as of 2026.

**Key Features:**
- Encourages voluntary disclosure of information about fraud, bribery, looted funds, financial misconduct, government assets, and corruption.
- Reward: 2.5-5% of recovered amount (percentage decreases as amounts increase).
- Reporting channels: online portal, telephone hotlines, SMS, email.
- No statutory whistleblower protection law exists -- the Whistleblower Protection Bills of 2008, 2011, 2015, 2019, and 2022 have all stalled.
- ICPC continues to urge passage of protection legislation as of 2025.

**Course Relevance:** Critical case study in the gap between policy aspiration and legal protection. The absence of whistleblower legislation is a significant ethics infrastructure gap in Nigeria.

### 1.11 Proceeds of Crime (Recovery and Management) Act 2022

**Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
**Date:** May 2022

**Key Provisions:**
- Non-conviction-based asset recovery framework.
- All law enforcement/anti-corruption agencies must establish Proceeds of Crime Management Directorates.
- Seized property transferred to Directorate within 30 days.
- Confiscated/forfeited funds directed to Confiscated and Forfeited Properties Account for national development.
- Special courts designated to hear proceeds of crime cases.

**Course Relevance:** Demonstrates the expanding legal consequences of financial crimes. Relevant to understanding that proceeds of unethical conduct can be recovered even without criminal conviction.

### 1.12 SEC Code of Corporate Governance for Public Companies 2011

**Issuing Body:** Securities and Exchange Commission of Nigeria
**Date:** 2011 (supplementing the NCCG 2018 with capital-markets-specific guidance)
**Full Text:** https://www.sec.gov.ng/files/CODE%20OF%20CORPORATE%20GOVERNANCE%20FOR%20PUBLIC%20COMPANIES.pdf

**Course Relevance:** Provides listed-company-specific governance requirements. While superseded by NCCG 2018 in general application, SEC has issued additional recommended practices largely from this code.

### 1.13 CBN Nigerian Foreign Exchange Code 2024

**Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
**Date:** Effective October 14, 2024

**Key Features:**
- Principles covering Ethics, Governance, Execution, Information Sharing, Risk Management and Compliance, Confirmation and Settlement.
- Applies to all FX market participants.
- Aligns Nigerian FX trading with global standards and international best practices.

**Course Relevance:** Sector-specific ethics code demonstrating how ethical principles apply to specialized financial market activities.

## 2. Professional Body Ethics Codes & CPD Requirements

### 2.1 Chartered Institute of Bankers of Nigeria (CIBN)

**Establishing Act:** CIBN Act 5 of 2007 (originally founded 1963 as Nigerian Institute of Bankers; chartered 1990)
**Website:** https://cibng.org/
**Code of Conduct:** https://www.cibng.org/files/resourceDownloads/codeOfConduct.pdf

**Code of Conduct Scope:** Applies to ALL Directors, Managers, Officers, Supervisors, and Employees (full-time, part-time, temporary, casual, in-sourced) of all banks/discount houses in Nigeria.

**Ethics Governance Structure:**
- Sub-Committee on Ethics and Professionalism (established December 19, 2000)
- Composition: CBN representatives, NDIC personnel, seven bank MDs (4-year terms), CIBN as secretariat
- Filing fee: N100,000 (non-refundable)
- Banks must respond to complaints within 2 weeks
- Penalties include mandatory refunds (14 days), interest calculations, 1% non-compliance surcharges
- Jurisdictional limits: declines cases older than 6 years, below N5 million, pending in court, or involving criminal allegations

**Examination Requirement (Ethics):**
- Subject D4: "Ethics, Corporate Governance and Professionalism" at the Diploma level
- Part of the banking professional examination structure (three stages: Diploma, Intermediate Professional, Chartered Banker)
- All staff must complete Attestation Form per CBN Circular BSD/BCS/GEN/DBS/05/095

**MCPD:** Mandatory Continuing Professional Development required; specific hour requirements not publicly detailed on website.

### 2.2 Institute of Chartered Accountants of Nigeria (ICAN)

**Establishing Act:** ICAN Act 1965
**Website:** https://icanig.org/
**IFAC Member:** https://www.ifac.org/about-ifac/membership/members/institute-chartered-accountants-nigeria

**Ethics Framework:**
- Code of Professional Conduct and Guide for Members
- Based on the International Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants (IESBA Code) adopted through IFAC membership
- Core values: Integrity, Objectivity, Professional Competence and Due Care, Confidentiality, Professional Behavior

**Disciplinary Framework:**
- Accountants' Investigating Panel (AIP) -- receives and investigates complaints
- Accountants' Disciplinary Tribunal (ADT) -- adjudicates and imposes sanctions
- Sanctions: suspension, withdrawal of practicing rights, removal from membership

**Examination Requirement (Ethics):**
- **Skills Level:** Management, Governance and Ethics (MGE) -- dedicated ethics subject
- **Professional Level:** Corporate Strategic Management and Ethics (CSME) -- covers corporate governance, strategic management, and ethics including bribery, money laundering, embezzlement, theft, fraud, extortion, blackmail
- Ethics embedded across all 15 ACA examination subjects
- Applicable codes/laws: CAMA, NCCG 2018, International Code of Ethics 2018, ICAN Professional Code of Conduct

**Mandatory Professional Development (MPD):**
- Effective January 1, 2024: increased from 90 to 120 credit hours over three years
- Training includes NOCLAR (Non-Compliance with Laws and Regulations) leadership and governance
- Example session: February 2025 MCPD Training (Consultancy Practice Sector), N25,000 fee, 15 MPD credits, conducted via Zoom

### 2.3 Chartered Institute of Personnel Management of Nigeria (CIPM)

**Establishing Act:** CIPM Act (recognized as Nigeria's only chartered HR regulatory body, reaffirmed May 2026)
**Website:** https://cipmnigeria.org/
**Code of Conduct & Ethics Handbook:** https://cipmnigeria.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/CIPM-Professional-Code-of-Conduct-Ethics-Handbook-V5.pdf (October 2023, Version 5)

**Code Scope:** Applies to ALL CIPM members regardless of role, level, sector, industry, or geographical location.

**Six Core Values:** Service, Creativity, Respect, Integrity, Professionalism, Teamwork

**Ethical Obligations:**
- Comply with laws, regulations, and CIPM standards
- Foster workplace cultures emphasizing ethics and compliance
- Promote fairness, diversity, and inclusion
- Avoid conflicts of interest and maintain confidentiality
- Handle data responsibly and ethically
- Treat stakeholders with respect and dignity

**Disciplinary Framework:**
- Minor Misconduct: failure to follow work standards, unprofessional conduct, poor data handling, minor confidentiality breaches
- Gross Misconduct: discrimination, harassment, dishonesty, bribery, health/safety violations, fraud, actions damaging the profession
- Sanctions: formal reprimand (minor) to deregistration/license withdrawal (serious)
- Relisting possible after meeting conditions and demonstrating remorse
- Anonymous whistleblowing channel; strict prohibition on retaliation

**CPD Requirements:**
- HR Practitioners' Licence requires minimum 60 credit points
- As of May 2026, mandatory professional certification for officers in HR roles within MDAs
- CIPM offers CPD programs, seminars, and workshops on HR trends and ethics

### 2.4 Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria (FRCN)

**Establishing Act:** Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria Act, 2011
**Website:** https://frcnigeria.gov.ng/

**Mandate:**
- Protect investors and stakeholder interests
- Provide guidance on financial reporting and corporate governance
- Ensure good governance practices, accuracy, and reliability of financial reports
- Promote highest standards among auditors and financial reporting professionals
- Publishes ethical codes based on IFAC Codes of Ethics
- Issues the NCCG 2018 (see Section 1.6)

**Course Relevance:** The FRCN is the overarching governance regulator across all sectors. Its codes and standards form the backdrop against which all professional body ethics codes operate.

## 5. Nigerian Case Studies

### 5.1 Cadbury Nigeria Plc Accounting Scandal (2006)

**Year:** 2006
**Organizations:** Cadbury Nigeria Plc, Cadbury Schweppes UK, Akintola Williams Deloitte
**Source:** https://www.thecasecentre.org/products/view?id=118820 (academic case); multiple media reports

**Summary:** Cadbury Nigeria overstated its accounts by N13 billion (approximately $85 million) over the 2002-2005 reporting period. Irregularities involved inflated sales figures via trade loading (shipping excess products to distributors near year-end to boost sales artificially) and issuance of false suppliers' stock certificates to defer cost recognition.

**Consequences:**
- CEO Bunmi Oni and Finance Director Akadiri terminated by UK parent company
- Both banned from operating in Nigerian capital market, from financial services employment, and from directorship in any Nigerian public company
- Auditors Akintola Williams Deloitte fined $130,000
- Share price collapsed from N57 to N34
- Often called "Nigeria's Enron"

**Teaching Points:**
- Creative accounting and earnings management as ethical failures
- Weak internal controls enabling management collusion
- Role and failure of external auditors
- Consequences of governance failures: personal liability, career destruction, market value erosion
- Importance of tone at the top

### 5.2 Cecilia Ibru / Oceanic Bank Fraud (2009-2010)

**Year:** 2009-2010
**Organizations:** Oceanic Bank, EFCC, Ecobank (acquirer)
**Source:** https://saharareporters.com/2010/10/08/former-md-oceanic-bank-cecilia-ibru-convicted-bank-fraud; Al Jazeera; Vanguard

**Summary:** Cecilia Ibru, Nigeria's first female bank CEO, was convicted of corporate fraud for negligent and reckless grant of credit facilities running into billions of dollars, including N16 billion to a company with no collateral, and mismanagement of depositors' funds.

**Consequences:**
- Convicted on 25 counts of corporate fraud (October 8, 2010)
- Sentenced to 6 months imprisonment (plea bargain)
- Ordered to reimburse $1.2 billion in cash and assets
- Forfeited shares in 298 listed and unlisted companies (Guinness Nigeria, Zenith Bank, UBA, Diamond Bank, First Bank, Nestle Nigeria)
- Over 100 properties confiscated in Nigeria, Dubai, and the United States
- Oceanic Bank placed in administration and acquired by Ecobank in 2011

**Teaching Points:**
- Abuse of executive power and fiduciary duty
- Reckless lending without proper due diligence
- Asset forfeiture as a consequence of financial crimes
- Gender dimension of corporate leadership ethics
- Impact of individual misconduct on entire institutions

### 5.3 Erastus Akingbola / Intercontinental Bank Fraud (2009-ongoing)

**Year:** 2009-present
**Organizations:** Intercontinental Bank Plc, EFCC

## 7. Ethical Decision-Making Frameworks

### 7.1 Rushworth Kidder's Ethical Decision-Making Model

**Source:** Institute for Global Ethics (founded by Kidder)

**Steps:**
1. Identify relevant facts (detail and context)
2. Recognize and identify relevant moral issues
3. Determine the moral agents
4. Investigate the moral trilemma (right vs. wrong vs. right)
5. Test for right-versus-wrong issues
6. Test for right-versus-right issues using four paradigms:
   - Truth vs. Loyalty
   - Individual vs. Community
   - Short-term vs. Long-term
   - Justice vs. Mercy
7. Apply resolution principles and justify the decision

**Nigerian Relevance:** The truth vs. loyalty paradigm is particularly relevant in Nigerian workplace culture where personal/ethnic/family loyalty often conflicts with institutional duties and professional obligations.

### 7.2 Thomas Jones's Moral Intensity Framework

**Source:** Jones, T.M. (1991), "Ethical Decision Making by Individuals in Organizations"

**Six Factors:**
1. Magnitude of Consequences
2. Social Consensus
3. Probability of Effect
4. Temporal Immediacy
5. Proximity
6. Concentration of Effect

**Nigerian Relevance:** Helps professionals calibrate ethical responses based on the severity and scope of potential harm -- essential for prioritizing compliance efforts in resource-constrained Nigerian organizations.

### 7.3 Josephson's Six-Step Model

**Source:** Josephson Institute of Ethics

**Steps:**
1. Stop and think
2. Clarify goals
3. Determine facts
4. Develop options
5. Consider consequences
6. Choose, monitor, and modify

**Nigerian Relevance:** Simple, actionable framework suitable for microlearning format. Can be applied to common Nigerian workplace ethical dilemmas.
