## Corpus Summary

This corpus covers credit risk management for Nigerian banking professionals, anchored in CBN Prudential Guidelines for Deposit Money Banks, BOFIA 2020 lending provisions, IFRS 9 expected credit loss implementation, CBN credit concentration risk guidelines (2019), CBN stress testing guidelines, CBN CRMS mandatory reporting, credit bureau regulations (CRC, FirstCentral, CreditRegistry), the 2024 bank recapitalisation programme, and the post-forbearance NPL reclassification crisis of 2025. The corpus includes 14 primary regulatory sources, 7 textbooks and reference works, 7 existing courses and training programmes, 9 Nigerian case studies spanning the 2009 banking crisis through the 2024 Heritage Bank revocation, 14 key data points on Nigerian banking credit metrics, professional body certification requirements from CIBN/GARP/PRMIA/CRMING, 7 identified gaps requiring SME input, and a proposed 3-module/12-lesson structure.

## Primary Regulatory Sources

### 1. CBN Prudential Guidelines for Deposit Money Banks (Revised 2019, Updated 2023)
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2019 (exposure draft August 2019, updated provisions through 2023)
- **Relevance:** Primary regulatory framework governing loan classification, provisioning, and credit risk management for DMBs. Defines the five-category loan classification system: Performing (1% provision), Watchlist (5%), Substandard (20%), Doubtful (50%), Lost (100%). Establishes 90-day past-due threshold for non-performing status. Covers credit portfolio disclosure requirements, insider lending limits, and collateral valuation standards.
- **Key Sections:** Section 3 (Risk Management), Section 15 (Loan Loss Provisioning), credit portfolio management, specialised loan classification
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2019/fprd/exposure_dmbs%20%20august%202019.pdf

### 2. Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act (BOFIA) 2020
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (signed by President Buhari, November 12, 2020)
- **Date:** 2020-11-12
- **Relevance:** Primary banking legislation replacing BOFIA 1991. Prohibits unsecured lending except per CBN regulations. Establishes Credit Tribunal (Sections 102-132) for enforcement and recovery of eligible loans. Grants CBN expanded powers for licence revocation (Section 12). Requires enhanced public disclosure of financial information by banks.
- **Key Sections:** S.12 (Licence Revocation), S.102-132 (Credit Tribunal), unsecured credit restrictions, collateralisation requirements, enhanced disclosure obligations
- **Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/financial-services/1073908/banks-and-other-financial-institutions-act-2020-highlight-of-key-changes

### 3. CBN Guidelines on Management of Credit Concentration Risk (2019)
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria, Banking Supervision Department
- **Date:** 2019
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive guidelines on managing single-name and sectoral credit concentration risk. Requires banks to adopt Standard Industry Classification (SIC) for sectoral limits. Mandates quarterly stress testing of credit concentration exposures. Defines single obligor limit at 20% of shareholders' fund unimpaired by losses.
- **Key Sections:** Single-name concentration limits, sectoral portfolio concentration limits, quarterly stress testing requirements, credit concentration policy framework, off-balance-sheet engagement calculation (50%)
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2019/bsd/guideline%20on%20the%20management%20for%20credit%20concentration%20risk.pdf

### 4. CBN Revised Guidance Notes on Credit Risk
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria, Banking Supervision Department
- **Date:** 2015 (revised)
- **Relevance:** Detailed guidance on credit risk measurement, mitigation, and management. Requires banks to adopt qualitative and quantitative techniques for portfolio risk measurement. Covers credit risk mitigation techniques and residual risk capital requirements under Pillar 2. Emphasises holistic credit risk management covering strategy formulation, origination, administration, measurement, and control.
- **Key Sections:** Credit risk measurement techniques, credit risk mitigation, residual risk requirements, credit administration procedures
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2015/bsd/2.revised%20guidance%20notes%20on%20credit%20risk.pdf

### 5. CBN Guideline on Stress Testing for Nigerian Banks
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2019 (updated directive March 2026)
- **Relevance:** Framework for bank stress testing with focus on credit risk. Requires regulatory capital projection for minimum 3-year forward period. 2026 update introduces staged migration assumptions requiring banks to model severe asset quality deterioration, a 10% provisioning floor on deteriorated sectors, and treatment of all insider credits as fully in default. Stress test results become each bank's official individual capital requirement.
- **Key Sections:** Stress scenario design, credit portfolio deterioration assumptions, capital adequacy impact analysis, sectoral sensitivity analysis, insider credit treatment, 12-month progressive deterioration period
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2019/bsd/guideline%20on%20stress%20testing%20%20for%20nigerian%20banks.pdf

### 6. CBN Guidance Notes on Regulatory Capital
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2015 (revised)
- **Relevance:** Defines Tier 1 and Tier 2 capital composition. Tier 2 capital limited to one-third (33.33%) of Tier 1 capital. CAR minimums: 15% for international-licence and SIBs, 10% for other banks. Establishes risk-weighted asset calculation methodology.
- **Key Sections:** Capital composition, Tier 1/Tier 2 definitions, CAR calculation, risk-weight categories, deductions for goodwill and intangibles
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2015/bsd/1.revised%20guidance%20notes%20on%20regulatory%20capital.pdf

### 7. CBN Banking Sector Recapitalisation Programme (March 2024)
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2024-03
- **Relevance:** Landmark recapitalisation requiring all banks to significantly increase minimum paid-in capital by March 31, 2026. International commercial banks: N50B to N500B. National commercial banks: N25B to N200B. Regional commercial banks: N10B to N50B. National merchant banks: N15B to N50B. By April 2026, 33 of 36 banks met requirements. N4.65 trillion raised collectively.
- **Key Sections:** Capital requirement tiers by bank category, 24-month compliance window, capital-raising pathways
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/Out/2024/CCD/Recapitalization_MARCH_2024.pdf

### 8. CBN Credit Risk Management System (CRMS)
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** Established under CBN Act No. 24 of 1991 (Sections 28 and 52)
- **Relevance:** Mandatory credit reporting system requiring all financial institutions to report customers with aggregate outstanding debit balances of N1 million and above. Web-enabled system for filing returns and conducting borrower inquiries. Enables tracking of global debt profiles across multiple institutions. 2026 update restricts further credit to large-ticket obligors with NPL status recorded in CRMS.
- **Key Sections:** Monthly reporting requirements, borrower data elements (names, directors, credit limits, outstanding amounts, status, collateral), penalty provisions for non-compliance
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/supervision/crms.html

### 9. CBN Guidelines for Licensing, Operation and Regulation of Credit Bureaus in Nigeria
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2008 (revised November 2013)
- **Relevance:** Licensing framework for credit bureaus. Three licensed bureaus: CRC Credit Bureau (largest, 200M+ credit facilities processed), FirstCentral Credit Bureau (licensed May 2009), CreditRegistry. Financial institutions must report to at least two of three bureaus. FICO scoring model (300-850) used by CRC and FirstCentral; CreditRegistry uses proprietary scale (100-999).
- **Key Sections:** Bureau licensing requirements, mandatory reporting by financial institutions, data subject consent requirements, credit scoring models
- **Reference:** https://docplayer.net/13396210-Guidelines-for-the-licensing-operations-and-regulation-of-credit-bureaus-in-nigeria.html

### 10. Credit Reporting Act 2017
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2017
- **Relevance:** Legal framework for credit reporting, signed by Vice President Osinbajo. Establishes mandatory credit checks on a need-to-know basis with data subject consent. Requires organisations to include credit check consent in contract forms.
- **Key Sections:** Credit reporting obligations, data subject rights, consent requirements, enforcement provisions
- **Reference:** https://srjlegal.com/lenders-liabilities-to-existing-lenders-under-nigerias-credit-reporting-act/

### 11. AMCON Act 2010 (as amended)
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2010 (amended 2015, 2019)
- **Relevance:** Established the Asset Management Corporation of Nigeria to resolve NPLs from the 2009 banking crisis. AMCON has repaid N3.6 trillion to CBN since inception. Banks pay mandatory AMCON levy (N283.85B paid by 10 banks in Q1 2025 alone, up 28% YoY). AMCON recovered N156B in 2024, targets N215B in 2025.
- **Key Sections:** NPL acquisition mandate, sinking fund contributions, recovery powers, bank levy structure, sunset provisions
- **Reference:** https://amcon.com.ng/

### 12. CBN Anchor Borrowers' Programme Guidelines
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2015 (revised October 2021)
- **Relevance:** Agricultural lending framework linking smallholder farmers to anchor companies. CBN bore 50% credit risk on defaults. Programme disbursed N1.12 trillion, with only N546 billion repaid (49% recovery rate). Now discontinued with recovery ongoing. Critical case study in agricultural credit risk failure. House of Representatives ordered probe in July 2024.
- **Key Sections:** Credit risk sharing model, NIRSAL guarantee deployment, commodity linkage framework, repayment mechanisms
- **Reference:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2021/ccd/abp%20guidelines%20october%2013%202021%20-%20final%20(002).pdf

### 13. NIRSAL Credit Risk Guarantee Framework
- **Issuing Body:** Nigeria Incentive-Based Risk Sharing System for Agricultural Lending (NIRSAL Plc)
- **Date:** Ongoing
- **Relevance:** US$500 million CBN-owned non-bank institution providing credit risk guarantees of up to 75% on agricultural loans. Five components: Risk Sharing Facility, Insurance Component, Technical Assistance, Bank Incentive Mechanisms, Agricultural Bank Rating System. Achieved record N100 billion in approved credit guarantees in 2025.
- **Key Sections:** CRG instrument deployment, risk-sharing ratios, partner financial institution framework, agricultural value chain coverage
- **Reference:** https://nirsal.com/credit-risk-guarantee/

### 14. CBN Post-Recapitalisation Supervisory Framework (2026)
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2026-04
- **Relevance:** New supervisory framework requiring banks to model capital adequacy against adverse scenarios including currency depreciation, commodity price shocks, credit deterioration events, and systemic stresses. Mandates ongoing capital buffer maintenance. Represents shift from fixed capital requirement to risk-based capital requirement.
- **Key Sections:** Stress testing across defined scenarios, capital buffer requirements, risk-based capital adequacy, supervisory review and evaluation
- **Reference:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2026/04/cbns-post-recapitalisation-supervisory-framework-sets-new-standard-for-nigerian-banking/

## Textbooks and Reference Works

### 1. The Essentials of Risk Management (3rd Edition)
- **Authors:** Michel Crouhy, Dan Galai, Robert Mark
- **Publisher:** McGraw-Hill, 2023
- **ISBN:** 9781264258864
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive risk management reference covering credit risk measurement and transfer, ERM approach, credit risk modelling. Third edition includes machine learning, AI, and distributed ledger technology applications in risk management. Core GARP FRM and PRMIA reading material.

### 2. Financial Institutions Management: A Risk Management Approach (11th Edition)
- **Authors:** Anthony Saunders, Marcia Cornett, Otgo Erhemjamts
- **Publisher:** McGraw-Hill, 2024
- **ISBN:** (11th Edition)
- **Relevance:** Leading textbook on financial institution risk management. Covers credit risk measurement, off-balance-sheet risk, liquidity risk, and market risk. Provides quantitative frameworks for credit VaR, KMV model, CreditMetrics, and portfolio risk management.

### 3. Credit Risk Measurement In and Out of the Financial Crisis (3rd Edition)
- **Authors:** Anthony Saunders, Linda Allen
- **Publisher:** John Wiley & Sons, 2010
- **ISBN:** 9780470478349
- **Relevance:** Specialised text on credit risk measurement methodologies including VaR approaches, structural models, reduced-form models, and credit derivatives valuation. Covers lessons from the 2008 financial crisis.

### 4. GARP FRM Study Materials (2025 Curriculum)
- **Publisher:** Global Association of Risk Professionals (GARP)
- **Relevance:** Official study materials for the FRM (Financial Risk Manager) certification. Part I covers quantitative analysis, risk management concepts, financial markets and products, valuation and risk models. Part II focuses on application: market risk, credit risk, operational risk. 97,000+ certified professionals globally across 190+ countries. Exam format: 100 MCQs per part, 4 hours each.

### 5. PRMIA Credit and Counterparty Risk Manager (CCRM) Study Guide
- **Publisher:** Professional Risk Managers' International Association (PRMIA)
- **Relevance:** Specialised study guide for the CCRM Certificate covering practical credit risk analysis frameworks, credit lifecycle, counterparty risk developments, and key modelling techniques. Exam: 60 MCQs, 2 hours, 60% pass rate. Relevant for credit risk staff, financial controllers, and compliance officers.

### 6. IFRS 9 Financial Instruments -- Implementation Guidance
- **Publisher:** International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) / Deloitte / PwC / KPMG
- **Relevance:** Implementation guides for IFRS 9 expected credit loss model. Covers the three-stage impairment model (Stage 1: 12-month ECL; Stage 2: lifetime ECL for SICR; Stage 3: lifetime ECL for credit-impaired), probability of default modelling, loss-given-default estimation, and forward-looking macroeconomic scenarios. Essential for Nigerian banks post-2018 adoption.

### 7. World Bank Policy Note: IFRS 9 in Emerging Markets and Developing Economies
- **Publisher:** World Bank Group, 2021
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive policy recommendations for ECL framework implementation in developing economies. Addresses data availability challenges, modelling risk and overreliance on managerial judgement, and multi-department coordination burden. Directly applicable to Nigerian banking context.
- **Reference:** https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/700511616757978211/pdf/Accounting-Provisioning-Under-the-Expected-Credit-Loss-Framework-IFRS-9-in-Emerging-Markets-and-Developing-Economies-A-Set-of-Policy-Recommendations.pdf

## Nigerian Case Studies

### 1. The 2009 Nigerian Banking Crisis and CBN Intervention
- **Period:** 2008-2011
- **Key Facts:** CBN injected $2.6 billion (N620 billion) into five banks: Afribank, Finbank, Intercontinental Bank, Oceanic Bank, and Union Bank. Joint CBN/NDIC audit revealed massive NPLs and insider abuses. Bank CEOs replaced and subsequently prosecuted. Banks had concentrated exposure to oil/gas and capital markets during commodity boom. Crisis led to creation of AMCON in 2010 to absorb over N5 trillion in toxic assets.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Concentration risk in oil/gas and capital markets; insider lending abuse; inadequate stress testing; failure of risk governance; need for independent credit review functions; importance of sector diversification limits
- **Reference:** https://businesselitesafrica.com/why-these-7-nigerian-banks-failed-and-the-lessons-financial-institutions-can-learn/

### 2. Skye Bank Failure and Polaris Bank Bridge (2016-2018)
- **Period:** 2016-2018
- **Key Facts:** Formed in 2005 from merger of five banks. Acquired Mainstreet Bank in 2014, adding integration risk. CBN dissolved board in July 2016 due to severe liquidity and governance concerns. Share capital fell into negative territory due to NPLs. Licence revoked September 2018; Polaris Bank established as bridge bank. Polaris subsequently lost N26 billion in loans granted to 6 ex-directors without collateral.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Post-merger integration challenges amplify credit risk; insider lending without collateral; weak asset quality governance; rising funding costs create feedback loops; importance of early regulatory intervention frameworks
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2018/11/13/revealed-how-bad-management-played-a-part-in-skye-banks-failure/

### 3. Heritage Bank Licence Revocation (June 2024)
- **Period:** 2024
- **Key Facts:** CBN revoked Heritage Bank's licence on June 3, 2024 under Section 12 of BOFIA 2020, citing persistent financial instability and breach of regulatory requirements. CBN had engaged with the bank and prescribed supervisory steps that failed to stem decline. NDIC appointed liquidator. Maximum deposit insurance coverage of N5 million per depositor applied (increased from N500,000 in April 2024). Most recent bank failure in Nigeria.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Regulatory forbearance has limits; importance of capital adequacy maintenance; BOFIA 2020 licence revocation powers are real; depositor protection framework in action; reputational contagion risk to sector
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2024/06/03/cbn-revokes-heritage-banks-licence-due-to-poor-financial-performance/

### 4. CBN Anchor Borrowers' Programme -- Agricultural Credit Risk Failure
- **Period:** 2015-2024
- **Key Facts:** CBN disbursed N1.12 trillion to smallholder farmers via ABP. Only N546 billion repaid (49% recovery rate). CBN bore 50% credit risk on defaults. Programme now discontinued. House of Representatives ordered probe in July 2024. Challenges included high interest rates, short repayment periods, lack of collateral security, inadequate credit amounts, and poor monitoring infrastructure.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Government-directed lending programmes face acute information asymmetry; agricultural lending requires specialised risk assessment; collateral challenges in rural lending; moral hazard in guaranteed lending; inadequacy of traditional credit scoring for agricultural borrowers
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2024/07/03/house-of-reps-orders-probe-into-n1-12-trillion-anchor-borrowers-program/

### 5. Oil and Gas Sector Credit Concentration Crisis (2024-2025)
- **Period:** 2024-2025
- **Key Facts:** Nine banks' oil/gas exposure rose to N15.6 trillion in 2024 (94.4% increase from N10.17T in 2023). Oil/gas sector received 35.7% of total private sector lending ($136.6B). First Bank carries approximately 35% of net loan book in oil/gas. Post-forbearance reclassification moved legacy exposures into Stage 3 impairment categories, particularly upstream and oil services sub-sectors. Currency depreciation creates feedback loop: FX shortages strain borrower repayment capacity.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Single-sector concentration risk; oil price volatility transmission to credit quality; FX-denominated lending risk; forbearance mask real asset quality; staged migration under IFRS 9 reveals hidden NPLs
- **Reference:** https://allafrica.com/stories/202505120371.html

### 6. Post-Forbearance NPL Reclassification Crisis (2025)
- **Period:** 2025
- **Key Facts:** CBN unwound COVID-era forbearance that allowed banks to restructure exposures and delay NPL classification. NPL ratio spiked to 5.62% in April 2025 (exceeding 5% prudential threshold), then to 7.0% by mid-2025. At least 11 banks breached the 5% NPL regulatory limit. Banking industry CAR declined to 12% (from 15.2% in Q4 2024). CBN prohibited affected banks from paying dividends and bonuses. Impairment charges surged to N3.2 trillion (39% increase from N2.3T in 2024).
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Regulatory forbearance creates cliff-edge risk; true asset quality assessment requires transparent classification; IFRS 9 Stage 3 migration amplifies provisioning burden; dividend restrictions as supervisory tool; importance of forward-looking provisioning even during relief periods
- **Reference:** https://businessday.ng/companies/article/nigerian-banks-impairment-charges-rise-to-n3-2trn-as-forbearance-ends/

### 7. Naira Devaluation and FX Credit Risk (2023-2025)
- **Period:** 2023-2025
- **Key Facts:** Naira devalued from N461.5/$ (January 2023) to N907.11/$ (December 2023) to N1,535.8/$ (2024 average) following Tinubu administration's FX unification. Leading Nigerian companies incurred N1.7 trillion in FX losses in 2023. Dollar-denominated borrowers face 3-4x higher naira repayment obligations. CBN mandated Net Open Position limits. Tier-1 bank earnings fell from N4.12 trillion (2024) to N3.2 trillion (2025) as devaluation gains faded and impairment charges rose.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** FX risk directly translates to credit risk; importance of currency mismatch analysis in loan underwriting; NOP limits as prudential tools; devaluation gains are non-recurring; stress testing must model severe currency scenarios
- **Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2024/04/25/leading-nigerian-companies-incur-a-staggering-n1-7-trillion-in-fx-losses-in-2023/

### 8. SME and Microfinance Lending Risk Challenges
- **Period:** 2024-2025
- **Key Facts:** Nigeria's MSMEs face estimated $236 billion financing gap. Only 4% of 40 million small businesses access formal bank loans. 27% cite high interest rates as biggest barrier (PwC 2024 survey). Moniepoint disbursed over N1 trillion to SMEs but now grapples with N7 billion in NPLs. 51% of informal businesses have never taken a loan and do not intend to (up from 30% prior year). World Bank approved $500 million in 2025 to expand SME finance.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Information asymmetry in SME lending; alternative credit scoring necessity; scaling credit rapidly tests risk frameworks; collateral challenges for informal businesses; importance of cash-flow-based lending models
- **Reference:** https://hallmarknews.com/moniepoint-grapples-with-n7bn-in-non-performing-loans-after-aggressive-sme-lending-push/

### 9. Digital Lending and Fintech Credit Risk Evolution
- **Period:** 2024-2025
- **Key Facts:** 37.5% of Nigerian fintechs use AI for credit scoring (CBN 2025 Fintech Report). Only 2.6% of population has formal credit system access; 90% of consumers use alternative lending. Digital platform lending elevates NPL ratios per academic studies. Bank losses from criminal activity increased 277% (Q3-Q4 2024), with 94.35% from fraudulently issued loans. FCCPC DEON Regulations (2025) require digital lender registration.
- **Credit Risk Lessons:** Alternative data (utility payments, mobile metadata, spending patterns) expands but complicates scoring; algorithmic bias risks; fraud risk in digital lending; regulatory framework emerging but incomplete; speed-vs-quality trade-off in automated credit decisions
- **Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/fintech/1772572/beyond-credit-scores-the-rise-of-ai-driven-lending-in-nigeria

## Professional Body Requirements

- **Relevance:** Primary banking professional body in Nigeria. Three-stage examination pathway: Diploma, Intermediate Professional, and Chartered Banker. Credit risk management and lending are core syllabus components. Partnership with RIMAN for specialised CRM induction programmes. Membership often required or preferred for credit officer and risk management roles in Nigerian banks.

- **Relevance:** Globally recognised risk management credential. Part II covers credit risk measurement and management including probability of default, loss-given-default, expected/unexpected loss, credit VaR, credit derivatives. Increasingly required by international-licence Nigerian banks for senior risk roles. 97,000+ certified professionals globally.
- **Website:** https://www.garp.org/frm

### 3. PRMIA Professional Risk Manager (PRM) and CCRM Certificate
- **Relevance:** PRM covers enterprise risk management including credit risk. CCRM provides specialised credit and counterparty risk certification (60 MCQs, 2 hours). Directly relevant for credit risk analysts and portfolio managers. Covers credit risk analysis frameworks, credit lifecycle, and modelling techniques.
- **Website:** https://prmia.org/

### 4. Chartered Risk Management Institute of Nigeria (CRMING)
- **Relevance:** Nigerian-focused risk management professional body. Offers certification and continuing education programmes covering enterprise, operational, credit, and market risk with specific Nigerian regulatory context.
- **Website:** https://crming.org/

### 5. Chartered Institute of Loan and Risk Management of Nigeria (CILRMN)
- **Relevance:** Specialised Nigerian professional body focused on loan administration and risk management. Offers chartered membership pathway and continuing professional development.
- **Website:** https://cilrmng.org/

### 6. National Institute of Credit Administration (NICA)
- **Relevance:** Nigerian institution focused on credit administration education and certification.
- **Website:** https://www.icanigeria.net/

## Gaps Requiring SME Input

### 1. CBN Internal Credit Risk Examination Methodology
- **Gap Description:** The specific methodology CBN examiners use during risk-based supervision examinations of bank credit portfolios is not publicly documented in detail. SME input needed from former CBN examiners or compliance officers who have been through recent examinations to explain: examination triggers, documentation expectations, common findings, remediation timelines, and penalty escalation.
- **Importance:** HIGH -- practitioners need to know what examiners actually look for beyond published guidelines.

### 2. IFRS 9 ECL Model Implementation Details for Nigerian Banks
- **Gap Description:** While the IFRS 9 framework is well-documented, the specific implementation choices made by Nigerian banks (PD model selection, macroeconomic scenario design, management overlay practices, SICR thresholds) are proprietary. SME input needed from bank risk officers or Big 4 auditors who have audited Nigerian bank ECL models to explain: common PD model approaches used, typical macroeconomic variables incorporated, management overlay justification practices, and calibration challenges specific to Nigeria's data environment.
- **Importance:** HIGH -- bridge between theoretical ECL framework and Nigerian bank practice.

### 3. Collateral Valuation Challenges in Nigerian Lending
- **Gap Description:** Published guidelines mention forced sale value for mortgages and market value for securities, but practical challenges (land title disputes, multiple governors' consent requirements, Lagos Land Use Charge impacts, property market illiquidity outside Lagos/Abuja, valuation fraud) require practitioner insight. SME input needed on: common collateral types accepted, valuation methodology standards, perfection timelines, and recovery rates by collateral type.
- **Importance:** HIGH -- collateral is the primary credit risk mitigant but its practical effectiveness varies enormously in Nigeria.

### 4. Internal Credit Rating Models Used by Nigerian Banks
- **Gap Description:** How Nigerian banks develop and validate internal credit rating models is not publicly documented. SME input needed on: common rating methodologies (expert judgement vs. statistical models), number of rating grades, rating migration tracking, model validation approaches, and CBN expectations for internal ratings.
- **Importance:** MEDIUM -- important for practitioners building or maintaining credit scoring systems.

### 5. Sector-Specific Underwriting Standards
- **Gap Description:** Sector-specific credit assessment approaches (e.g., oil/gas reserve-based lending, agricultural value-chain financing, real estate development finance, telecoms tower financing) are developed internally by banks. SME input needed from sector-specialist credit officers on: key risk factors by sector, typical deal structures, tenor expectations, documentation requirements, and common default triggers.
- **Importance:** HIGH -- sectoral expertise is what differentiates effective credit officers.

### 6. AMCON Debt Recovery Practical Experience
- **Gap Description:** AMCON's actual recovery processes, negotiation approaches, and litigation strategies are not well-documented publicly. SME input needed from AMCON staff or lawyers involved in recovery to explain: typical recovery timelines, negotiation tactics, use of Credit Tribunal under BOFIA 2020, asset realisation challenges, and factors affecting recovery rates.
- **Importance:** MEDIUM -- relevant for understanding the full credit lifecycle including workout and recovery.

### 7. Agricultural and SME Credit Scoring Innovation
- **Gap Description:** Emerging approaches to credit scoring for agricultural borrowers and MSMEs using alternative data (mobile money transaction history, social media, psychometric testing, satellite imagery for crop assessment) are being piloted by Nigerian fintechs and some banks. SME input needed on: which alternative data sources show predictive power in Nigeria, integration challenges with traditional scoring, regulatory acceptance, and validation methodologies.
- **Importance:** MEDIUM -- growing importance as digital lending scales and CBN pushes financial inclusion.

## Proposed Module Structure

### Module 1: Foundations of Credit Risk in Nigerian Banking (Lessons 1-4)

**Lesson 1: The Nigerian Credit Risk Landscape**
- Overview of credit risk in Nigerian banking system
- Key statistics: NPL ratios, credit volumes, sector exposures
- The role of CBN as regulator and supervisor
- Historical context: 2004 consolidation, 2009 crisis, 2024 recapitalisation
- *Sources:* CBN Prudential Guidelines, BOFIA 2020, industry statistics

**Lesson 2: CBN Regulatory Framework for Credit Risk**
- CBN Prudential Guidelines: loan classification system (Performing, Watchlist, Substandard, Doubtful, Lost)
- Provisioning requirements (1%, 5%, 20%, 50%, 100%)
- 90-day past-due threshold for non-performing status
- Single obligor limit (20% of shareholders' fund)
- CBN CRMS mandatory reporting (N1 million threshold)
- CBN credit concentration risk guidelines and sectoral limits
- *Sources:* CBN Prudential Guidelines, CBN Credit Concentration Guidelines, CBN CRMS, BOFIA 2020

**Lesson 3: Credit Bureaus and Credit Information Infrastructure**
- Three licensed credit bureaus: CRC, FirstCentral, CreditRegistry
- Mandatory dual-bureau reporting by financial institutions
- Credit scoring models (FICO 300-850 and CreditRegistry 100-999)
- Credit Reporting Act 2017 requirements
- CBN CRMS: global borrower debt profile tracking
- *Sources:* Credit Reporting Act 2017, CBN Credit Bureau Guidelines, CBN CRMS

**Lesson 4: Capital Adequacy and Its Relationship to Credit Risk**
- Tier 1 and Tier 2 capital composition
- Minimum CAR requirements (15% for international/SIBs, 10% for others)
- Risk-weighted asset calculation for credit exposures
- 2024 recapitalisation programme and its impact on lending capacity
- Loan-to-deposit ratio (50%) and Cash Reserve Ratio (45%)
- *Sources:* CBN Guidance Notes on Regulatory Capital, CBN Recapitalisation Programme

### Module 2: Credit Risk Assessment, Measurement, and IFRS 9 (Lessons 5-8)

**Lesson 5: Credit Analysis and Underwriting Fundamentals**
- The 5 Cs of Credit in Nigerian context (Character, Capacity, Capital, Collateral, Conditions)
- Financial statement analysis for credit decisions
- Cash flow analysis and debt service coverage
- Sector-specific risk assessment (oil/gas, manufacturing, agriculture, services)
- Collateral valuation: forced sale value, market value, perfection requirements
- *Sources:* Banking practice texts (Saunders & Cornett), industry practice, [SME INPUT: Sector-specific underwriting standards]

**Lesson 6: Credit Scoring and Internal Rating Models**
- Traditional credit scoring methodologies for Nigerian banking
- Internal credit rating model development and validation
- Alternative credit scoring: mobile data, psychometric testing, AI/ML models
- CBN 2025 Fintech Report: 37.5% of fintechs use AI for scoring
- Digital lending platforms: speed vs. quality trade-offs
- FCCPC DEON Regulations 2025 for digital lenders
- *Sources:* Credit bureau data, CBN Fintech Report, digital lending research, [SME INPUT: Internal rating models, alternative scoring validation]

**Lesson 7: IFRS 9 Expected Credit Loss Model**
- Transition from IAS 39 incurred loss to IFRS 9 expected credit loss (Nigeria adopted January 2018)
- The three-stage impairment model: Stage 1 (12-month ECL), Stage 2 (lifetime ECL for SICR), Stage 3 (lifetime ECL for credit-impaired)
- Significant Increase in Credit Risk (SICR) definition and triggers
- Probability of Default (PD), Loss Given Default (LGD), Exposure at Default (EAD) estimation
- Forward-looking macroeconomic scenario incorporation
- Management overlays: justification and governance
- CBN regulatory provisioning vs. IFRS 9 ECL: understanding the two frameworks
- *Sources:* IFRS 9 standard, World Bank IFRS 9 guidance for EMDEs, Nigerian bank implementation experience, [SME INPUT: ECL model implementation details]

**Lesson 8: Credit Portfolio Risk Management**
- Portfolio diversification and concentration risk management
- CBN sectoral concentration limits and Standard Industry Classification
- Oil and gas concentration: 35.7% of total lending -- systemic risk
- Credit portfolio stress testing: CBN methodology and scenarios
- Credit risk mitigation techniques: guarantees, collateral, credit derivatives
- FX credit risk: currency mismatch analysis and NOP limits
- *Sources:* CBN Credit Concentration Guidelines, CBN Stress Testing Guidelines, Crouhy/Galai/Mark text, portfolio risk literature

### Module 3: NPL Management, Recovery, and Emerging Risks (Lessons 9-12)

**Lesson 9: NPL Management and Workout Strategies**
- CBN NPL classification criteria and provisioning cascade
- Early warning systems for credit deterioration
- Loan restructuring: principles, documentation, and CBN treatment
- BOFIA 2020 Credit Tribunal: jurisdiction and procedures (Sections 102-132)
- Write-off policies and regulatory requirements
- Case study: Post-forbearance NPL reclassification crisis (2025) -- 11 banks breaching 5% threshold
- *Sources:* CBN Prudential Guidelines, BOFIA 2020, post-forbearance case study, [SME INPUT: AMCON recovery experience]

**Lesson 10: AMCON, Recovery, and the Nigerian Bank Failure Cycle**
- AMCON: establishment (2010), mandate, and operations
- AMCON levy structure and bank contributions (N283.85B from 10 banks, Q1 2025)
- N3.6 trillion repaid to CBN since inception
- Case studies: 2009 banking crisis (Afribank, Intercontinental, Oceanic), Skye Bank/Polaris (2018), Heritage Bank (2024)
- NDIC deposit insurance framework (N5 million coverage since April 2024)
- Lessons learned: concentration risk, insider lending, governance failures
- *Sources:* AMCON Act, BOFIA 2020, CBN supervision history, Heritage Bank revocation, Skye Bank case study

**Lesson 11: Agricultural and SME Lending Risk**
- Agricultural credit risk: CBN Anchor Borrowers' Programme lessons (N1.12T disbursed, 49% recovery)
- NIRSAL Credit Risk Guarantee mechanism (up to 75% guarantee coverage)
- NIRSAL five-component framework: RSF, IC, TAF, BIM, ABRS
- SME financing gap ($236 billion) and credit access challenges
- CREDICORP (Nigerian Consumer Credit Corporation): wholesale financing and risk-sharing model
- Alternative lending and fintech credit risk: Moniepoint experience
- *Sources:* CBN ABP Guidelines, NIRSAL CRG framework, CREDICORP operations, SME lending research, [SME INPUT: Agricultural and SME scoring innovation]

**Lesson 12: Emerging Credit Risks and the Future of Credit Risk Management in Nigeria**
- Post-recapitalisation supervisory framework: risk-based capital requirements
- CBN stress testing evolution: 2026 directive and new capital adequacy calculation
- Naira devaluation and FX credit risk: from N461.5/$ (2023) to N1,535.8/$ (2024)
- Digital lending risks: algorithmic bias, fraud (94.35% of bank losses from fraudulent loans in Q4 2024), data quality
- Climate risk and credit: emerging CBN guidance
- AI and machine learning in credit risk: opportunities and regulatory considerations
- Nigeria's evolving credit culture: CREDICORP, credit scoring infrastructure, financial inclusion imperative
- *Sources:* CBN Post-Recapitalisation Framework, CBN Stress Testing Directive 2026, FX market data, digital lending research, Crouhy/Galai/Mark 3rd Edition
