## 1. PRIMARY REGULATORY SOURCES

### 1.1 CBN Succession Planning Directive for DSIBs (September 2025)

- **Full Title:** Circular on Succession Planning for Managing Directors/Chief Executive Officers of Domestic Systemically Important Banks
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria, Financial Policy and Regulation Department
- **Signed By:** Dr. Rita Sike, Director, Financial Policy and Regulation Department
- **Date Issued:** September 16, 2025
- **Effective Date:** Immediately upon issuance
- **Applies To:** Domestic Systemically Important Banks (DSIBs)
- **References:** Nairametrics: https://nairametrics.com/2025/09/16/cbn-orders-banks-to-secure-regulatory-approval-for-md-successor-six-months-early/ | BusinessDay: https://businessday.ng/banking/article/big-banks-must-name-new-ceos-six-months-early-cbn/ | The Guardian: https://guardian.ng/business-services/money/banks-must-get-ceo-succession-approval-6-months-before-incumbent-exits-cbn/

**Key Requirements:**
- Banks must obtain CBN regulatory approval for successor MD/CEO no later than **6 months** before the incumbent's tenure expires
- Public disclosure of the successor appointment must occur no later than **3 months** before the outgoing MD/CEO exits
- Purpose: "minimize disruptions at the top management level, enable top management appointees to prepare adequately for their new roles, and generally mitigate risks associated with abrupt changes in leadership"
- Builds on Section 2.14 of the CBN's 2023 Corporate Governance Guidelines

**Teaching Relevance:** This is the most directly succession-focused CBN directive. It establishes Nigeria as one of the few jurisdictions globally with mandatory advance CEO succession disclosure for systemically important banks. Critical case study material for the course.

### 1.2 CBN Corporate Governance Guidelines 2023 -- Section 2.14 (Succession Planning)

- **Full Title:** Corporate Governance Guidelines for Commercial, Merchant, Non-Interest, Payment Services Banks and Financial Holding Companies in Nigeria
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria
- **Date Issued:** July 13, 2023
- **Effective Date:** August 1, 2023
- **Official PDF:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/Out/2023/FPRD/Circular%20and%20Guidelines%20for%20Corporate%20Governance.pdf
- **PwC Analysis:** https://www.pwc.com/ng/en/assets/pdf/2023-cbn-governance-guidelines.pdf

**Section 2.14 -- Succession Planning:**
- Board must approve succession plans for MD/CEO, Executive Directors, and senior management staff
- Objectives: minimise disruptions at top management level, enable appointees to prepare adequately, mitigate risks from abrupt leadership changes
- Board Nomination and Governance Committee oversees succession planning process

**Principle 3.0, Sections 3.2-3.5 -- Tenure Limits:**
- MD/CEO: maximum **12 years** (increased from 10 under 2014 Code)
- DMD/ED promoted to MD/CEO in same bank: cumulative maximum **24 years** (prior DMD/ED service NOT counted toward 12-year MD/CEO limit)
- NEDs: maximum **12 years** (3 terms of 4 years)
- INEDs: maximum **8 years** (2 terms of 4 years)
- Cumulative board tenure cap (same bank): **24 years** across all director roles
- Financial Holding Company MD/CEO: maximum **10 years**
- Cumulative tenure across entire banking industry: **20 years** (all banks combined)

**Principle 7.0 -- Cooling-Off Periods:**
- ED to NED (same bank): **2 years**
- NED to executive role (same bank): **2 years**
- ED to Financial Holding Company board: **2 years**

**Principle 6.0 -- Mandatory Board Committees:**
- Board Nomination and Governance Committee (BNGC) -- chaired by INED, responsible for director nominations, governance oversight, and succession planning
- Board Audit Committee (NEDs only)
- Board Risk Management Committee
- Board Remuneration Committee
- Board Credit Committee (for commercial/merchant/non-interest banks)
- ICT & Cybersecurity Committee (for PSBs)
- All committees must meet at least quarterly

**Board Composition:** Minimum 7, maximum 15 directors; NEDs must outnumber EDs; minimum 3 INEDs for large banks, 2 for smaller banks; at least 2 NEDs (one INED) with IT/fintech/cybersecurity expertise; maximum 2 members of same extended family on board

**Director Resignation:** 90 days written notice required; replacement within 90 days if INED/NED minimums are breached

**Replaces:** CBN Code of Corporate Governance for Banks and Discount Houses 2014

**Teaching Relevance:** Foundation regulatory document for the entire course. Section 2.14 is the primary succession planning mandate for Nigerian banks.

### 1.3 Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance 2018 (NCCG 2018)

- **Full Title:** Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance 2018
- **Issuing Body:** Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria (FRCN)
- **Date Published:** January 15, 2019
- **Effective Date:** January 1, 2020
- **Official Source:** https://frcnigeria.gov.ng/publications/
- **ICSAN Mirror:** https://icsan.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance-2018.pdf
- **KPMG Analysis:** https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/advisory/2018-Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance.pdf
- **Templars Highlights:** https://www.templars-law.com/app/uploads/2019/02/Highlights-of-the-Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance-2018.pdf

**Succession-Relevant Principles:**
- **Principle 1.5:** "The Board shall ensure the establishment and implementation of a succession plan for the Board and senior management of the company." -- the foundational national succession planning mandate
- **Principle 2 (Board Composition):** Boards must have appropriate mix of skills, experience, independence, and diversity; majority NEDs, most independent; CEO-Chairman separation mandated (Principle 2.7)
- **Principle 3.3:** Three-year cooling-off period for former executives becoming Chairman
- **Principle 7.2.9:** INED tenure limited to 3 terms of 3 years (maximum 9 years) to enable periodic board refreshing; ED tenure left to board discretion
- **Principle 11 (Board Committees):**
  - Principle 11.2.5: Assigns ownership of board succession planning to the Board Chairperson and the Nomination/Governance Committee
  - **Principle 11.2.5.7:** "A succession plan should be in place for the Chairman of the Board, Managing Director/Chief Executive Officer, Executive Directors, Non-Executive Directors and senior management positions to ensure leadership continuity. Provision should be made for succession in emergency situations as well as long-term vacancies."
- **Principle 12:** Formal, transparent criteria and processes for board appointments and re-appointments
- **Principle 14 (Board Evaluation):** Annual evaluations must assess whether adequate succession plans are in place; external independent evaluation recommended every 3 years
- **Compliance Approach:** "Apply and Explain" -- all entities must explain how each principle is applied
- **Scope:** All public listed entities, private holding companies of public entities, regulated private companies, concessioned/privatized companies

**Teaching Relevance:** Cross-sector governance code that applies beyond financial services. Provides the overarching succession planning governance framework for all Nigerian entities.

### 1.4 SEC Corporate Governance Directives on Director Tenure and Board Succession (2025)

- **Full Title:** Circular to All Public Companies and Capital Market Operators on the Transmutation of Independent Non-Executive Directors and Tenure of Directors
- **Issuing Body:** Securities and Exchange Commission, Nigeria
- **Date Issued:** June 19, 2025
- **Guidance Note:** July 1, 2025
- **Official Source:** https://sec.gov.ng/for-investors/keep-track-of-circulars/circular-to-all-public-companies-and-capital-market-operators-on-the-transmutation-of-independent-non-executive-directors-and-tenure-of-directors/
- **Mondaq Analysis:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1643034/regulatory-update-sec-issues-new-corporate-governance-directives-to-curb-boardroom-rotations-and-preserve-independence

**Key Provisions:**
- Directors: maximum **10 consecutive years** in a single company; **12 years** within the same corporate group
- INEDs prohibited from being appointed as EDs (including CEO) within the same company/group
- Former CEOs/EDs: **3-year cool-off period** before eligible for Chairman appointment; then maximum **4-year** chairmanship
- Companies must update board succession plans in line with the 10/12-year limits and cool-off periods
- Companies must maintain records and notify the Commission where significant governance changes are being planned
- Years already served by affected appointees count toward computing exit dates

**Objectives:**
- Support orderly and transparent board succession planning
- Ensure continuity and independence in oversight
- Promote effective corporate governance aligned with global expectations
- Reinforce public and investor confidence in leadership

**Teaching Relevance:** Most recent regulatory development directly impacting succession planning for all listed companies. Creates mandatory succession planning triggers through tenure limits.

### 1.5 Companies and Allied Matters Act 2020 (CAMA 2020) -- Director Provisions

- **Full Title:** Companies and Allied Matters Act 2020
- **Enacted:** August 7, 2020
- **Signed by:** President Muhammadu Buhari

**Succession-Relevant Sections:**
| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Section 271 | Minimum number of directors |
| Section 272 | Appointment of first directors (by subscribers of memorandum) |
| Section 273 | Subsequent directors appointed by members at AGM |
| Section 274 | **Casual vacancy** -- board may appoint to fill vacancy from death, resignation, retirement, or removal, subject to AGM approval |
| Section 275 | Minimum 2 directors for all companies except small companies; public companies must have at least 3 INEDs |
| Section 281 | Director for life permitted, but still removable under Section 288 |
| Section 283 | Disqualification grounds (infants, unsound mind, suspended/removed directors, bankruptcy) |
| Section 285 | **Rotation of directors** -- all directors retire at first AGM; at each subsequent AGM, one-third retire (longest-serving first) |
| Section 286 | Chairman of public company cannot also serve as CEO (separation of roles) |
| Section 287 | Voting procedures for director appointments |
| Section 288(1) | Members' statutory right to remove a director by ordinary resolution (non-excludable) |
| Section 288(2) | Special notice (28 days before AGM) required; director entitled to 21 days notice and written representations |
| Section 307 | Directors restricted to maximum 5 boards of public entities simultaneously |
| Section 404 | Statutory Audit Committee requirements |

**Note:** CAMA 2020 does not prescribe specific tenure limits (even allowing life appointments under Section 281), leaving those to sectoral codes, but its rotation (Section 285) and removal (Section 288) mechanisms create the legal infrastructure for orderly board renewal.

**Teaching Relevance:** Foundation statute for director appointment, removal, and board composition in Nigeria. Section 285 (mandatory rotation of one-third of directors annually) and Section 288 (removal by ordinary resolution) are critical for understanding how succession is legally structured and how succession crises can be triggered or resolved through shareholder action.

### 1.6 NAICOM Corporate Governance Guidelines for Insurance and Reinsurance Companies 2021

- **Full Title:** Corporate Governance Guidelines for Insurance and Reinsurance Companies (CGGIRC) 2021
- **Issuing Body:** National Insurance Commission (NAICOM)
- **Date Issued:** March 17, 2021
- **Effective Date:** June 1, 2021
- **Mondaq Analysis:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/reinsurance/1064422/naicoms-corporate-governance-guidelines-for-insurance-and-reinsurance-companies-2021-cggirc

**Succession-Relevant Provisions:**
- **Executive Director (Technical) -- Built-in Successor:** Every insurance/reinsurance company must appoint an ED-Technical with the same qualifications and experience as the MD, **explicitly designated as next in line to succeed the MD**. This is a distinctive mandatory succession pipeline provision unique to the insurance sector.
- **CEO Tenure:** Maximum **10 years** (two terms of 5 years each)
- **ED Transitioning to CEO:** Cumulative maximum **15 years** for an ED who becomes CEO in the same company
- **Board Size:** Minimum 7, maximum 15 members; maximum 40% in executive capacity; at least 1 INED
- **Six Mandatory Committees:** Nomination and Governance, Remuneration, Audit, Risk Management, Finance/Investment/General Purpose, Compliance
- **Board Attendance:** Minimum 75% of annual meetings
- **Tenure Limits Effective:** January 1, 2023 (implementation began January 1, 2024)
- Must comply with both NCCG 2018 and NAICOM Guidelines simultaneously

**Impact:** As of 2024, approximately 15 CEOs and Executive Directors across 9+ insurance firms were expected to exit due to tenure limits, creating a major succession planning challenge industry-wide.

**Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/reinsurance/1262132/examining-naicoms-circular-on-tenure-limit-for-executive-directors-and-its-implications-for-the-insurance-industry

**Teaching Relevance:** Insurance sector case study for how regulatory tenure limits force succession planning. The mass departure of 15+ CEOs/EDs provides a compelling real-world example.

### 1.7 PenCom Corporate Governance Guidelines for Licensed Pension Fund Operators

- **Full Title:** Guidelines on Corporate Governance for Pension Fund Operators (2021); Revised Guideline on Corporate Governance for Licensed Pension Fund Operators (September 2025)
- **Issuing Body:** National Pension Commission (PenCom)
- **2021 Version:** https://www.pencom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/Guidelines-on-Corporate-Governance-for-Pension-Fund-Operators-February-2021.pdf
- **2025 Revision:** https://www.pencom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Revised-Guideline-on-Corporate-Governance-for-LPFOs-22-September-2025.pdf
- **Applicable to:** Licensed Pension Fund Administrators (PFAs) and Pension Fund Custodians (PFCs)

**Key Provisions (2021):**
- Chairman and CEO roles must be separated
- Minimum non-executive director representation equal to executive members
- At least one independent director with no company relationships
- Annual formal board evaluations; results submitted to PenCom
- Succession plans for key officers must be outlined as part of licensing requirements (Form APP/01/PFA)
- Mandatory Nomination and Governance Committee; must meet at least quarterly
- **NED Tenure:** Maximum **15 years** on a PFA board

**2025 Revision ("Pension Revolution 2.0") -- Key Additions:**
- Added formal requirement for **"Induction and Training of Directors"** -- structured onboarding and ongoing development now mandatory
- **Board diversity:** Boards must set measurable objectives on gender, age, experience, and culture
- **Principal Owner Provisions:** May remain on boards beyond normal tenure as NEDs, subject to shareholder re-election every 3 years, annual evaluations, committee certification, external review every 3 years, and PenCom oversight; barred from chairing Audit, Risk, Nomination, or Remuneration committees
- **Management Qualifications:** MDs require 15 years post-qualification experience (12 in financial sector, 8 in senior management); EDs require 12 years (10 in finance, 6 in senior management)
- CEO exit notice period remains only **2 months** -- critics note this is insufficient for large, systemically important pension operators; CBN's 6-month notice period for bank CEOs has been cited as a model PenCom could emulate

**Reference:** Nairametrics Analysis: https://nairametrics.com/2025/09/26/pencoms-new-guidelines-ambition-risks-and-the-fine-print/

**Teaching Relevance:** Pension sector-specific succession planning requirements. The contrast between PenCom's 2-month notice and CBN's 6-month notice creates excellent teaching material on sector-specific approaches.

### 1.8 CBN Guidelines on Fitness and Propriety of Directors

- **Full Title:** Assessment Criteria for Approved Persons' Regime / Fit and Proper Persons' Regime
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria, Banking Supervision Department
- **Revised Version:** October 2015
- **Official PDF:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2015/fprd/revised%20fit%20and%20proper%20-%20combined-final%20oct%202015.pdf

**Key Requirements:**
- **Fitness Tests:** Assess competence of candidates for board, top management, and critical operational positions and their capacity to fulfil responsibilities
- **Propriety Tests:** Assess integrity and suitability of candidates
- **MD/CEO Requirements:** Minimum first degree; minimum 15 years post-qualification experience, of which at least 10 must be in management and leadership positions
- Track record covering both integrity and past performance required
- Must meet specific requirements of the CBN Competency Framework for the Nigerian Banking Industry

**Teaching Relevance:** Succession plans must account for CBN fit-and-proper requirements. A successor who lacks the required qualifications or experience will be rejected by CBN, regardless of internal grooming.

## 4. NIGERIAN CASE STUDIES

### 4.1 First Bank of Nigeria -- CEO Succession Crisis (2021-2025)

- **Organisation:** First Bank of Nigeria / FBN Holdings Plc
- **Key Actors:** Adesola Adeduntan (MD/CEO), Femi Otedola (major shareholder/Chairman), Tunde Hassan-Odukale (former Chairman, First Bank Limited), Olusegun Alebiosu (current MD/CEO), CBN Governor Godwin Emefiele (historical involvement)
- **Timeline:** 2021-ongoing

**What Happened:**
- 2021: FBN Holdings board attempted to replace CEO Adesola Adeduntan; CBN intervened and reversed the board's decision, reinstating Adeduntan -- unprecedented regulatory intervention in a bank CEO succession
- Former CBN Governor Emefiele allegedly influenced Otedola's acquisition of significant FBN Holdings shares, enabling his emergence as Chairman
- After consolidating control, Otedola targeted Adeduntan for removal and displaced Hassan-Odukale as First Bank Chairman
- 2024: Adeduntan resigned under controversial circumstances; Olusegun Alebiosu appointed as new MD/CEO
- January 2025: Shareholders demanded EGM to remove Otedola as Chairman, citing governance concerns and a controversial N350 billion private placement

**Succession Planning Lessons:**
- Regulatory intervention can override internal succession decisions
- Major shareholder interests can distort succession processes
- Importance of transparent, governance-driven succession vs. personality-driven succession
- Shareholder activism as a check on board-level succession manipulation

**Sources:** Nairametrics, BusinessDay, The ICIR, Gazettengr, ThisDay Live

### 4.2 Access Bank -- Unexpected Founder Death and Emergency Succession (2024)

- **Organisation:** Access Holdings Plc / Access Bank Plc
- **Key Actors:** Dr. Herbert Wigwe (Group CEO, co-founder), Bolaji Agbede (Acting Group CEO), Roosevelt Ogbonna (MD/CEO, Access Bank subsidiary)
- **Timeline:** February 2024-ongoing

**What Happened:**
- February 9, 2024: Herbert Wigwe, co-founder and Group CEO of Access Holdings, died in a helicopter crash in California along with his wife, son, and the former President of the Nigerian Stock Exchange
- Access Holdings swiftly appointed **Bolaji Agbede** as Acting Group CEO -- she was the most senior founding executive director
- Roosevelt Ogbonna continued as MD/CEO of the banking subsidiary (appointed May 2022, before Wigwe's death)
- The transition demonstrated the value of having a strong leadership bench and clear organisational structure

**Succession Planning Lessons:**
- Emergency succession preparedness is critical -- even visionary founders are mortal
- The holdco/subsidiary structure (Access Holdings vs. Access Bank) provided leadership depth
- Having a deputy/acting successor pre-identified enabled rapid transition
- Emotional and reputational management during crisis succession

**Sources:** Semafor, The Africa Report, Bloomberg, TechCabal, Wikipedia

### 4.3 GTBank/GTCO -- Planned Holding Company Restructuring (2021)

- **Organisation:** Guaranty Trust Bank Plc / GTCO Plc
- **Key Actors:** Segun Agbaje (Group CEO), Miriam Olusanya (MD, GTBank subsidiary)
- **Timeline:** 2021

**What Happened:**
- June 28, 2021: GTBank completed reorganisation into Guaranty Trust Holding Company (GTCO) Plc, listed on NGX
- Segun Agbaje (CEO since 2011) transitioned from MD/CEO of the bank to Group CEO of the holding company
- Miriam Olusanya appointed as new MD of the banking subsidiary -- first female MD of GTBank
- Reorganisation involved significant management changes: **clean sweep of top management above age 45**; four General Managers out of nine asked to exit
- Younger executives (mostly under 45) promoted to take charge

**Succession Planning Lessons:**
- Corporate restructuring as a strategic succession planning tool
- Age-based management refresh -- bold but controversial approach
- Planned CEO transition: moving founder/long-serving CEO "up" to holdco level
- Creating new leadership opportunities through organisational redesign
- First female CEO appointment as part of succession diversity

**Sources:** Independent.ng, Nairametrics, ThisDay Live, African Business

### 4.4 Zenith Bank -- Model Internal Succession (Founder to 4th-Generation CEO)

- **Organisation:** Zenith Bank Plc
- **Key Actors:** Jim Ovia (Founder), Godwin Emefiele (2nd CEO), Peter Amangbo (3rd CEO), Ebenezer Onyeagwu (4th CEO), Dame Adaora Umeoji (5th CEO, current)

### 4.5 UBA -- Founder Transition to Strategic Chairman

- **Organisation:** United Bank for Africa (UBA) Plc / Heirs Holdings
- **Key Actor:** Tony Elumelu (Founder, Chairman)

- Tony Elumelu consolidated UBA through merger with Standard Trust Bank (2005), built it into a pan-African banking group

**Succession Planning Lessons:**
- Founder-to-Chairman as a structured transition model
- Building a portfolio conglomerate allows founders to "move up" rather than "move out"
- Professional management pipeline enables founder transition without value destruction
- Alignment of founder's equity interest with governance role

### 4.7 Ecobank Transnational -- CEO Crisis and Governance Failure

- Multi-jurisdictional governance complexity (Togo-headquartered, Nigeria-listed, Ivory Coast court)

## 6. PROFESSIONAL BODY REQUIREMENTS

### 6.1 CIPM (Chartered Institute of Personnel Management of Nigeria)

- **Establishment:** 1968; Chartered by Act No. 58 of 1992; CIPM Act 2019 made registration mandatory for all HR practitioners in Nigeria
- **Status:** Apex regulatory body for Human Resource Management practice in Nigeria
- **Website:** https://cipmnigeria.org/
- **Total Membership:** ~27,000 (2025/2026)
- **Mutual Recognition:** CIPD UK (signed September 17, 2024)

**Certification Levels:**
| Level | Key Requirements |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Associate (ACIPM)** | Pass all professional exams (Foundation + Intermediate + Professional), OR HR Practitioners' Route: Bachelor's/HND + 7 years experience + competence in 5+ HR areas. Application fee: N25,000 |
| **Member (MCIPM)** | 6 years as Associate + MCPE compliance + HR Practitioners' Licence, OR Executive Route: 12 years experience + Senior HR Manager level. Application fee: N50,000 |
| **Fellow (FCIPM)** | 7 years as Full Member + documented contributions to the profession |

**Examination Structure (3 levels, 12 papers):**
| Level | Papers | Fee/Paper |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| **Foundation** (4 papers) | Intro to HRM; Business Management & Economics; Intro to Organizational Behavior; Business Communication | N22,500 |
| **Intermediate** (4 papers) | Learning & Development; HR Administration; Industrial Relations & Labour Law; Performance Management | N25,000 |
| **Professional** (4 papers) | Strategic HRM; Compensation & Reward Management; HR Information Systems; Organizational Development & Change Management | N27,500 |

- Pass mark: 50% per paper; exams held 3 times/year (April, August, December)
- Succession planning falls under **Strategic HRM** and **Organizational Development & Change Management** at the Professional level

**CPD/MCPE Requirements:**
- CIPM operates a **Mandatory Continuing Professional Education (MCPE)** programme
- MCPE participation is mandatory for membership upgrades
- Members must document attendance at MCPE events, conferences, forums, and knowledge-sharing activities
- Valid **HR Practitioners' Licence (HRPL)** must be maintained
- Exact annual credit-hour requirement not publicly documented; confirmation from CIPM recommended
- Registration costs: N125,000 entry; N35,000-90,000 annual subscription depending on grade

**SABIficate Alignment:** Succession planning courses can be positioned as contributing to CIPM MCPE requirements (aligning with Professional Level syllabus: Strategic HRM, OD & Change Management). Partnership with CIPM for formal MCPE activity recognition would significantly enhance course value. The CIPM Advanced HRM I study pack covers succession planning -- SABIficate content should align with this syllabus.

### 6.2 CIoD Nigeria (Chartered Institute of Directors Nigeria)

- **Establishment:** 1983; Chartered by Act of Parliament in 2023

### 6.4 FITC (Financial Institutions Training Centre)

- **Establishment:** 1981, by CBN in collaboration with all licensed banks under the Bankers' Committee
- **Status:** Nonprofit; funded through annual subscriptions from CBN, NDIC, and all licensed banks
- **Website:** https://fitc-ng.com/
- **Role:** Primary delivery vehicle for CBN's Competency Framework for the Nigerian Banking Industry (issued November 2012)

**Key Programmes:**
- **Continuous Education Programme (CEP)** for directors of banks and financial institutions -- running since 1985; over **6,638 directors trained** to date
- **IFC-FITC Corporate Governance Centre:** Selected by World Bank Group/IFC to establish Corporate Governance Centre for training bank directors in Nigeria and Sub-Saharan Africa
- **IFC-GCGF Corporate Governance Board Leadership Programme** (since 2011) using globally standardised and localised curriculum
- **Future of Work Academy (FOWA):** Launched 2025 with nine specialised schools

**How FITC Courses Count:**
- FITC courses are **automatically recognised** for CIBN MCPD credits (FITC is a prominent approved provider)
- FITC training is the **de facto standard** for meeting CBN's competency framework requirements
- Completion of FITC governance programmes is recognised during **CBN supervisory reviews** of banks' governance posture
- Bank staff can count FITC programmes toward **CBN regulatory training requirements** and **CIBN MCPD credits** simultaneously

**SABIficate Alignment:** FITC is both a competitor and a potential partner. Its classroom/hybrid model does not offer microlearning. SABIficate could position as complementary -- microlearning reinforcement of FITC executive education. Understanding FITC's pricing and programme structure is a key gap requiring SME input.
