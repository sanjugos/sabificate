## Corpus Summary

This corpus covers AML/CFT/CPF compliance obligations for Designated Non-Financial Businesses and Professions (DNFBPs) in Nigeria -- law firms, accounting firms, real estate agents, hotels, jewellers, car dealers, dealers in precious metals and stones, supermarkets, casinos, tax consultants, and clearing/settlement companies. It is anchored in the Money Laundering (Prevention and Prohibition) Act 2022 (MLPPA), the EFCC (AML/CFT/CPF for DNFBPs) Regulations 2024, SCUML registration and supervision requirements, FATF Recommendations 22-23 on DNFBPs, NFIU suspicious transaction reporting guidelines, the Terrorism (Prevention and Prohibition) Act 2022, and Nigeria's Proceeds of Crime (Recovery and Management) Act 2022. The corpus addresses a massively underserved market -- most AML training in Nigeria targets banks and financial institutions; DNFBPs have minimal structured compliance education despite facing identical criminal penalties. In 2024, SCUML registered 26,047 DNFBPs and sensitized only 15,947 entities, while DNFBPs filed a mere 38 Suspicious Activity Reports versus 19,873 from banks. Nigeria was grey-listed by FATF in February 2023 partly due to weak DNFBP supervision, and while it exited in October 2025 after completing a 19-item action plan, enforcement is now intensifying with personal criminal liability for directors, partners, and compliance officers. The corpus includes 13 primary regulatory sources, 7 textbooks/reference works, 7 existing courses, 8 Nigerian case studies, 12 key facts and figures, professional body requirements for lawyers and accountants, 8 gaps requiring SME input, and a proposed 3-module/10-lesson structure.

## Primary Regulatory Sources

### 1. Money Laundering (Prevention and Prohibition) Act, 2022 (MLPPA)
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2022-05-13
- **Relevance:** Primary AML legislation. Repeals Money Laundering (Prohibition) Act 2011. Section 30 defines DNFBP categories: dealers in jewellery, cars and luxury goods, precious stones and metals, chartered accountants, audit firms, tax consultants, clearing and settlement companies, legal practitioners, hotels, casinos, supermarkets, dealers in real estate, and others designated by regulatory authorities. Section 18(2) defines money laundering. Establishes cash payment limits (N5M individuals, N10M corporates), 24-hour STR filing, CDD obligations, 5-year record retention. Personal liability for directors, partners, and compliance officers.
- **Key DNFBP Provisions:**
  - Section 30: DNFBP categories and definitions
  - Cash-Based Transaction Reports (CBTRs): transactions exceeding US$1,000 equivalent, filed within 7 days
  - Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs): N5,000,000 (natural persons), N10,000,000 (legal entities), filed within 7 days
  - International fund transfers exceeding US$10,000 reported within 1 day to NFIU
  - STRs filed within 24 hours, no minimum threshold
  - Compliance officer appointment mandatory with management-level authority
  - Record keeping: minimum 5 years after transaction completion
- **Penalties:**
  - DNFBPs: N1,000,000 minimum administrative fine
  - Mandatory disclosure non-compliance: N250,000 to N1,000,000 per day
  - Directors/employees: N10,000,000 minimum fine or 3 years imprisonment
  - Body corporate violations: N25,000,000 fine
- **Reference:** https://placng.org/i/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Money-Laundering-Prevention-and-Prohibition-Act-2022.pdf

### 2. EFCC (AML/CFT/CPF for DNFBPs) Regulations, 2024
- **Issuing Body:** Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC)
- **Date:** 2024 (Gazetted, published September 2024)
- **Relevance:** Comprehensive implementing regulations for DNFBPs under the MLPPA 2022. Designates SCUML as the body responsible for registration, monitoring, and supervision of DNFBPs. Covers obligations to establish AML/CFT/CPF programmes, identification and management of ML/TF/PF risks, designation of compliance officers, sanctions screening, cash-based transaction reports, STR filing, internal controls, and independent testing.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Mandatory registration with SCUML
  - Risk-based AML/CFT/CPF programmes proportionate to business operations
  - Designation and duties of compliance officers
  - Customer due diligence requirements
  - Independent testing and controls
  - Risk assessment framework for ML/TF/PF
  - Sanctions screening obligations
  - Record-keeping and reporting obligations
- **Reference:** https://scuml.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/EFCC-AML-CFT-CPF-REGULATIONS-2024-pdf.pdf

### 3. SCUML Registration Requirements and Guidelines
- **Issuing Body:** Special Control Unit Against Money Laundering (SCUML) / EFCC
- **Date:** Ongoing (updated 2024-2025)
- **Relevance:** Mandatory registration for all DNFBPs within 3 months of commencing operations. Registration is free. From 1 January 2026, all CTR/CBTR submissions must be via the SCUML portal (email submissions discontinued after 31 December 2025).
- **Registration Process:**
  - Submit application via SCUML portal (portal1.scuml.net)
  - Provide company registration documents, TIN certificate, beneficial ownership information
  - SCUML issues registration certificate (free of charge)
  - Must be completed within 3 months of commencing operations
- **SCUML Categories:** Automotive dealers, hospitality businesses, casinos, clearing and settlement companies, consultants and consulting companies, dealers in jewellery, mechanized farming equipment, precious metals and precious stones, real estate, chartered accountants, audit firms, tax consultants, legal practitioners, supermarkets, NGOs, and others as designated
- **Reference:** https://scuml.efcc.gov.ng/ | https://portal1.scuml.net/registrationguideline.php

### 4. SCUML AML/CFT Compliance Guidelines for DNFBPs
- **Issuing Body:** Special Control Unit Against Money Laundering (SCUML) / EFCC
- **Date:** 2019 (foundational), supplemented by 2024 Regulations
- **Relevance:** Operational compliance guidelines covering CDD obligations, STR filing requirements, record keeping, and internal controls for DNFBPs.
- **Reference:** https://www.scuml.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/08/AML-CFT-COMPLIANCE-GUIDELINES-FOR-DNFIS-1.pdf

### 5. FATF Recommendations 22 and 23: DNFBPs
- **Issuing Body:** Financial Action Task Force (FATF)
- **Date:** 2012 (Recommendation 23 amended October 2021)
- **Relevance:** International standards requiring customer due diligence (CDD), record-keeping, and suspicious transaction reporting (STR) for DNFBPs globally. Recommendation 22 covers CDD and record-keeping for casinos, real estate agents, dealers in precious metals/stones, lawyers, notaries, accountants, and TCSPs. Recommendation 23 extends other AML measures (internal controls, compliance officers, STR filing) to DNFBPs. The Interpretive Note to Recommendation 22 specifies when CDD is required for each DNFBP sector.
- **Key Requirements:**
  - CDD, internal controls, audit and compliance officers for DNFBPs
  - STR filing obligations for all DNFBP categories
  - Group-wide AML programmes (Recommendation 18 extended to DNFBPs via Recommendation 23, amended October 2021)
  - Risk-based approach for supervision of DNFBPs (Recommendation 28)
- **Reference:** https://www.fatf-gafi.org/content/dam/fatf-gafi/recommendations/FATF%20Recommendations%202012.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf

### 6. FATF Explanatory Materials for Recommendations 18 and 23
- **Issuing Body:** FATF
- **Date:** October 2021
- **Relevance:** Clarifies how group-wide AML programme requirements apply to DNFBPs, particularly for law firms, accounting firms, and TCSPs that operate across jurisdictions.
- **Reference:** https://www.fatf-gafi.org/content/dam/fatf-gafi/recommendations/Explanatory-Materials-R18-R23.pdf

### 7. Nigerian Financial Intelligence Unit (NFIU) Act, 2018
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2018-07-12
- **Relevance:** Established NFIU as autonomous body. All DNFBPs must file STRs directly to NFIU. NFIU receives, analyses, and disseminates financial intelligence. Section 25(2) empowers NFIU to impose penalties for non-compliance.
- **Reference:** https://www.nfiu.gov.ng/index.php/nfiu

### 8. NFIU Guidelines for Identification, Verification and Reporting of Suspicious Transactions (December 2024)
- **Issuing Body:** Nigerian Financial Intelligence Unit (NFIU)
- **Date:** 2024-12-13
- **Relevance:** Latest STR filing guidelines applicable to FIs and DNFBPs. Defines 72-hour investigation timeline and 24-hour reporting deadline. DNFBPs must report to NFIU when they detect transactions linked to ML, TF, or other financial crimes within 24 hours.
- **Reference:** https://thegovernanceplatform.org/highlights-of-the-nigerian-financial-intelligence-unit-suspicious-transactions-reporting-guidelines/

### 9. Terrorism (Prevention and Prohibition) Act, 2022
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2022-05-12
- **Relevance:** Companion legislation to MLPPA 2022. DNFBPs must implement internal controls against terrorism financing, report suspicious transactions (whether executed or not), conduct ML/TF risk assessments before launching new products/services, comply with asset freezing obligations under Section 54.
- **Reference:** https://nigsac.gov.ng/assets/lawsdoc/terrorism%20prevention%20publication.pdf

### 10. Proceeds of Crime (Recovery and Management) Act, 2022
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2022-05-12
- **Relevance:** Third pillar of 2022 legislative overhaul. Provides for seizure, confiscation, forfeiture, and management of proceeds of crime. Conviction-based and non-conviction-based forfeiture proceedings. Directly relevant to DNFBPs that facilitate property transactions.
- **Reference:** https://scuml.efcc.gov.ng/laws-regulations/

### 11. Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020 -- Beneficial Ownership Provisions
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** 2020-08
- **Relevance:** Sections 119-123 require disclosure of Persons with Significant Control (PSC) holding over 25% shares/voting rights. CAC Beneficial Ownership Register operationalised. DNFBPs must identify and verify beneficial owners of customer entities as part of CDD. Critical intersection with real estate and legal services CDD.
- **Reference:** https://www.openownership.org/en/publications/implementation-of-cosp-resolution-10-6/nigeria-leveraging-beneficial-ownership-transparency-for-enhanced-asset-recovery/

### 12. SCUML Guidance Note on Beneficial Ownership Identification and Verification for DNFBPs
- **Issuing Body:** SCUML
- **Date:** 2022
- **Relevance:** Specific guidance for DNFBPs on identifying and verifying beneficial owners of customer entities. Aligns with CAMA 2020 and FATF Recommendation 24/25.
- **Reference:** https://www.scuml.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/Guidance-Note-on-Beneficial-Ownership-Identification-and-Verification-for-Designated-Non-Financial-Business-and-Profession.pdf

### 13. Rules of Professional Conduct for Legal Practitioners, 2023 (Articles 55-66: AML/CFT)
- **Issuing Body:** Nigerian Bar Association (NBA) / Body of Benchers
- **Date:** 2023
- **Relevance:** Articles 55-66 establish AML/CFT/CPF obligations for lawyers. Requires designation of a compliance officer in every law firm (regardless of size), risk assessments, client profiling, record keeping, and filing of STRs. Implemented via the NBA Anti-Money Laundering Committee (NBA-AMLC) portal launched April 2025.
- **Key Requirements:**
  - Every law firm must designate a trained AML compliance officer
  - Mandatory risk assessment and client profiling
  - Record-keeping obligations
  - STR filing for suspected ML/TF
  - Compliance officer must be trained on AML/CFT/CPF frameworks
- **Reference:** https://blog.nigerianbar.org.ng/2024/10/11/notice-to-all-lawyers-in-nigeria-on-compliance-with-anti-money-laundering-and-terrorism-financing-provisions-of-the-rules-of-professional-conduct-for-legal-practitioners-2023/

## Nigerian Case Studies

### 1. Abubakar Malami (Former Attorney General) -- Real Estate Money Laundering (2025-2026)
- **Parties:** EFCC, former AGF Abubakar Malami (SAN), Abubakar Abdulaziz Malami (son), Bashir Asabe (associate), Metropolitan Auto Tech Limited
- **Summary:** EFCC filed 16-count charge (FHC/ABJ/CR/700/2025) alleging conspiracy to launder over N8.7 billion between 2015-2025. Key allegations: Used Metropolitan Auto Tech Limited (an auto company) to conceal N1.014 billion in a Sterling Bank account; disguised N500 million to purchase luxury duplex in Maitama, Abuja; additional N600.01 million concealed through bank deposits. EFCC sought interim forfeiture of 57 properties linked to Malami. Arraigned 30 December 2025.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Demonstrates how automobile dealers and real estate agents can be instrumentalized for ML; luxury property as ML conduit; corporate vehicles to disguise ownership; why auto dealers and real estate agents need robust CDD; personal liability of professionals who facilitate transactions
- **Source:** https://guardian.ng/news/efcc-files-16-money-laundering-charges-against-malami-son-associate/

### 2. Diezani Alison-Madueke -- Cross-Border Real Estate and Luxury Goods ML
- **Parties:** EFCC, former Minister of Petroleum Resources Diezani Alison-Madueke, business associates
- **Summary:** Alison-Madueke and co-conspirators acquired over US$1.5 billion in illicit profits laundered into real estate in Manhattan, California, and London, plus yachts, aircraft, and jewellery. Nigerian politicians own properties worth $400 million in Dubai alone. Case demonstrates cross-border ML through luxury goods and real estate.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Real estate agents as gatekeepers; jewellers and luxury goods dealers as ML channels; cross-border property purchases; why enhanced CDD is critical for high-value transactions; PEP identification obligations
- **Source:** https://hedang.org/nigeria-ranked-5th-in-property-and-money-laundering-as-diezani-leads-the-ring/

### 3. Abdulrasheed Maina -- Pension Fund Property ML
- **Parties:** EFCC, former pension reform official Abdulrasheed Maina
- **Summary:** Convicted of money laundering. At least 20 properties seized and forfeited to Nigerian government (final court ruling 2024). Purchased mansion for US$2 million in cash. Bought properties in US and UAE before conviction. Demonstrates how real estate is used to integrate illicit pension funds.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Cash property purchases as red flag; international property purchases; forfeiture consequences; why real estate agents must verify source of funds; CDD failure enables large-scale property-based ML
- **Source:** https://www.occrp.org/en/scoop/nigerian-pension-reformer-bought-us-and-uae-properties-before-money-laundering-conviction

### 4. Diepreye Alamieyeseigha -- London Real Estate ML and International Recovery
- **Parties:** EFCC, former Bayelsa State Governor Diepreye Alamieyeseigha, UK and South African authorities
- **Summary:** Pleaded guilty to corruption and ML in July 2007. Nigeria recovered US$17.7 million. London real estate worth estimated US$15 million discovered. Cross-border cooperation between EFCC and UK/South African authorities essential for recovery.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** International real estate as ML vehicle; cross-border cooperation in asset recovery; PEP property portfolios; why foreign property agents dealing with Nigerian clients need CDD
- **Source:** https://star.worldbank.org/sites/default/files/2023-10/Alamieyeseigha_StAR_Case_Study_0.pdf

### 5. Imo State High Court: Lawyers Are Not Exempt from DNFBP Obligations (November 2025)
- **Case:** Ikedi Agunkwu, Esq. v. First Bank of Nig Ltd (Suit No. HOW/571/2023), High Court of Imo State
- **Date:** 25 November 2025
- **Summary:** Court held that lawyers are NOT exempted from DNFBPs under the MLPPA 2022. This reversed the position in FRN v. Chief Mike Ozekhome (SAN) (2021) 9 NWLR (Pt. 1782) 448, where the Court of Appeal had held legal practitioners excluded from designated non-financial institutions under the previous 2011 Act. The 2025 ruling affirms that the MLPPA 2022 definitively includes lawyers as DNFBPs.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Legal practitioners are now unambiguously covered; historical exemption argument is dead; all law firms must comply with SCUML registration, CDD, STR filing; compliance officer designation mandatory
- **Source:** https://www.templars-law.com/app/uploads/2025/12/Client-Alert_Nigeria-Commences-Enforcement-Under-Anti-Money-Laundering-Act.pdf

### 6. NBA v. FGN & CBN -- Historical Lawyer Exemption Challenge
- **Case:** Registered Trustees of NBA v. AGF & CBN (Suit No: HC/BS/173/2014); Appeal: CBN v. NBA & AGF (Appeal No: CA/A/202/2015)
- **Summary:** The Nigerian Bar Association challenged the application of AML obligations to lawyers. Federal High Court upheld NBA's argument that lawyer bank accounts cannot be supervised to protect client-attorney privilege. Court of Appeal in CBN v. NBA & AGF ruled in favour of NBA. This position was effectively superseded by the MLPPA 2022 (which explicitly includes legal practitioners in Section 30) and the 2025 Imo State ruling.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Evolution of lawyer AML obligations in Nigeria; tension between privilege and compliance; legislative override of judicial interpretation; importance of understanding current law vs. historical positions
- **Source:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/310756672_Applicability_of_anti-money_laundering_laws_to_legal_practitioners_in_Nigeria_NBA_v_FGN_CBN

### 7. EFCC Axe Over 100 Lawyers / 17 LPDC Sanctions (2026)
- **Parties:** EFCC, Legal Practitioners Disciplinary Committee (LPDC), approximately 100 lawyers
- **Summary:** EFCC investigating over 100 lawyers for alleged involvement in financial crimes. 17 lawyers received sanctions from the Legal Practitioners Disciplinary Committee. Signals intensified enforcement focus on legal professionals as gatekeepers.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Lawyers face both criminal prosecution and professional discipline; dual liability (EFCC + LPDC); compliance is a career preservation issue; gatekeeper role creates unique vulnerability
- **Source:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2026/04/judicial-cleansing-efccs-axe-dangles-over-100-lawyers/

### 8. EFCC Asset Recovery: N248 Billion, $105 Million, 753 Properties (2024)
- **Parties:** EFCC, SCUML, various defendants
- **Summary:** In 2024 alone, EFCC recovered over N248 billion, $105 million, and 753 duplexes. Cash forfeitures included N10.9 billion and $7.3 million. Cryptocurrencies including 13.37 Bitcoin and 5.97 Ethereum were also seized. SCUML registered 26,047 DNFBPs, sensitized 15,947 entities, and processed 46,405 CTRs from high-risk sectors.
- **DNFBP Teaching Points:** Scale of enforcement; real estate as primary asset category; SCUML registration as enforcement priority; CTR processing volume; compliance protects against asset seizure
- **Source:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2025/03/crackdown-on-economic-financial-crimes-efccs-landmark-cases-recoveries/

## Professional Body Requirements

### Nigerian Bar Association (NBA) -- AML Obligations for Lawyers
- **Regulatory Framework:** Rules of Professional Conduct for Legal Practitioners, 2023 (Articles 55-66)
- **Key Obligations:**
  - All law firms must designate a trained AML compliance officer (regardless of firm size)
  - Compliance officer must have AML/CFT/CPF training
  - Mandatory risk assessments and client profiling
  - Record-keeping obligations
  - Filing of STRs for suspected ML/TF
- **NBA Anti-Money Laundering Committee (NBA-AMLC):**
  - Created to educate NBA members on AML regulations
  - Provides compliance guidelines
  - Promotes awareness of financial crimes
  - Fosters cooperation with NFIU and regulatory bodies
  - AML portal launched April 2025 (nbaaml.com) with resources library, compliance tools
- **Training Activities:** Webinars (e.g., March 2026 sanctions compliance), in-person workshops (Asaba 2024), sensitisation sessions
- **Historical Context:** NBA initially challenged AML obligations (NBA v. FGN & CBN); Court of Appeal ruled in favour of exemption under 2011 Act. MLPPA 2022 Section 30 explicitly includes legal practitioners. November 2025 Imo State High Court ruling confirmed lawyers not exempt.
- **Reference:** https://blog.nigerianbar.org.ng/2024/10/11/notice-to-all-lawyers-in-nigeria-on-compliance-with-anti-money-laundering-and-terrorism-financing-provisions-of-the-rules-of-professional-conduct-for-legal-practitioners-2023/

### Institute of Chartered Accountants of Nigeria (ICAN) and ANAN
- **Regulatory Framework:** International Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants (IESBA), adopted by ICAN since 2018, including NOCLAR provisions (Responding to Non-Compliance with Laws and Regulations)
- **AML Obligations:**
  - SCUML partners with ICAN and ANAN to ensure accountants adhere to AML/CFT standards
  - SCUML organises training programmes and workshops for accountants
  - Accountants must register with SCUML as DNFBPs
  - CDD, record-keeping, and STR filing obligations
- **Sectoral Risk Assessment:** SCUML published a Sectoral Risk Assessment of Accountants in Nigeria (2025), analysing STR filing patterns and ML/TF risks specific to the accounting profession
- **Enforcement:** SCUML collaborates with EFCC to investigate and sanction non-compliant accounting firms. Sanctions include fines, business restrictions, or criminal prosecution.
- **Reference:** https://scuml.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Accounting-Sector-Risk-Assesment-Final-Final.pdf

### FATF Risk-Based Approach Guidance for Sector Self-Regulatory Bodies
- **Accountants:** FATF Guidance on the Risk-Based Approach for Accountants (https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Fatfrecommendations/Fatfguidanceontherisk-basedapproachforaccountants.html)
- **TCSPs:** FATF Guidance on the Risk-Based Approach for Trust and Company Service Providers (https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Fatfrecommendations/Rba-tcsps.html)
- **Real Estate:** FATF Risk-Based Approach Guidance for the Real Estate Sector, July 2022 (https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Fatfrecommendations/Guidance-rba-real-estate-sector.html)
- **Supervision:** FATF Guidance on Risk-Based Supervision (includes DNFBP and VASP supervision strategies)

## Recommended Module Structure

### Module 1: The DNFBP Compliance Imperative -- Why AML/CFT Applies to Your Business (3 lessons)
**Lessons:**
1. What Are DNFBPs and Why Are They Targeted? Money Laundering Through Non-Financial Channels
2. Nigeria's AML Legal Framework for DNFBPs -- The MLPPA 2022, EFCC Regulations 2024, and SCUML
3. The Cost of Non-Compliance -- Personal Criminal Liability, N10M Fines, 3-Year Imprisonment, and 57 Seized Properties

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define DNFBPs and identify all 12+ categories under MLPPA Section 30
- Explain the three stages of money laundering using Nigerian DNFBP-specific examples (real estate, car dealers, jewellers)
- Identify the roles of SCUML, EFCC, NFIU, and NBA-AMLC in DNFBP supervision
- Calculate personal financial and criminal exposure using real 2024-2025 penalty data
- Explain why DNFBPs filed only 38 SARs in 2024 vs. 19,873 from banks and what this means for enforcement risk

**Source Basis:** MLPPA 2022, EFCC AML/CFT/CPF Regulations 2024, SCUML Registration Guidelines, FATF Recommendations 22-23, Malami case, EFCC 2024 enforcement data, GIABA MER

**Key Case Studies:** Malami N8.7B real estate ML; EFCC 2024 recoveries (N248B, 753 properties); Imo State Court ruling on lawyers

---

### Module 2: Know Your Customer, Know Your Risk -- CDD, Reporting, and Record-Keeping for DNFBPs (4 lessons)
**Lessons:**
1. SCUML Registration -- Process, Categories, and the January 2026 Portal Transition
2. Customer Due Diligence for DNFBPs -- Identification, Verification, Beneficial Ownership, and PEPs
3. Suspicious Transaction Reporting -- The 24-Hour Requirement, Red Flags, and Filing to NFIU
4. Currency Transaction Reports, Cash-Based Reports, and the 5-Year Record-Keeping Rule

**Learning Objectives:**
- Complete a SCUML registration application with correct documentation
- Conduct CDD appropriate to each DNFBP sector (real estate transaction, legal retainer, accounting engagement, jewellery sale, hotel guest, car purchase)
- Identify beneficial owners using CAMA 2020 PSC requirements and SCUML Guidance Note
- Apply enhanced CDD for PEPs using Nigerian case study examples
- Recognise sector-specific AML red flags:
  - **Real estate:** Rapid buying/selling, price divergence from market, all-cash purchases, unclear beneficial ownership
  - **Lawyers:** Unusual confidentiality requests, complex trusts/shell companies without commercial rationale, transactions lacking economic purpose
  - **Accountants:** Shell company creation for undisclosed parties, multi-layered ownership structures, obscured source of funds
  - **Jewellers/luxury goods:** Large unexplained cash payments for high-value items
  - **All sectors:** Client refusal to provide beneficial ownership information, unnecessary use of intermediaries
- File an STR within the 24-hour deadline
- Prepare CTRs (N5M/N10M thresholds) and CBTRs (US$1,000+ threshold) within 7-day filing window
- Implement a 5-year record retention system

**Source Basis:** SCUML Registration Guidelines, MLPPA 2022, EFCC Regulations 2024, NFIU December 2024 STR Guidelines, SCUML BO Guidance Note, CAMA 2020, FATF RBA Real Estate Guidance 2022, NFIU DNFBP STR Presentation

**Key Case Studies:** Diezani Alison-Madueke (US$1.5B in real estate/jewellery); Maina pension property ML; Alamieyeseigha London real estate

---

### Module 3: Building a Compliance Programme -- Internal Controls, Sector-Specific Risks, and Professional Obligations (3 lessons)
**Lessons:**
1. Establishing Your AML/CFT/CPF Programme -- Risk Assessment, Compliance Officer, Internal Controls, and Independent Testing
2. Sector-Specific Compliance -- Lawyers (NBA Rules 55-66), Accountants (ICAN/ANAN), Real Estate, Jewellers, Hotels, and Car Dealers
3. Nigeria's AML Future -- FATF Exit, Enforcement Escalation, Beneficial Ownership Transparency, and Career Development

**Learning Objectives:**
- Design a risk-based AML/CFT/CPF programme compliant with EFCC 2024 Regulations
- Designate and empower a compliance officer with management-level authority
- Implement internal controls including employee training, independent testing, and ongoing monitoring
- Apply sector-specific compliance requirements:
  - **Lawyers:** NBA Rules of Professional Conduct Articles 55-66, NBA-AML portal, client-attorney privilege boundaries
  - **Accountants:** ICAN/ANAN requirements, IESBA NOCLAR provisions, SCUML sectoral risk assessment findings
  - **Real estate:** FATF RBA Guidance 2022, property CDD, all-cash transaction protocols
  - **Hotels:** Guest identification, high-value transaction monitoring
  - **Jewellers/precious metals:** SCUML Risk-Based Approach guidance, cash payment protocols
  - **Car dealers:** Vehicle purchase CDD, source of funds verification
- Conduct an institutional ML/TF/PF risk assessment before launching new products or services
- Implement sanctions screening obligations
- Understand Nigeria's post-grey-list enforcement landscape and prepare for intensified SCUML examinations

**Source Basis:** EFCC AML/CFT/CPF Regulations 2024, Rules of Professional Conduct 2023, ICAN/ANAN framework, SCUML Sectoral Risk Assessment (Accountants), FATF RBA Guidance (Real Estate, Accountants, TCSPs), GIABA Typologies Report, FATF MER Follow-Up Reports, Templars Client Alert on enforcement commencement

**Key Case Studies:** EFCC axe over 100 lawyers; NBA v. FGN (historical); Imo State 2025 ruling; SCUML 2024 compliance data
