## 1. PRIMARY REGULATORY SOURCES

### 1.1 Labour Act (Cap L1, Laws of the Federation of Nigeria 2004)

- **Full Title:** Labour Act, Chapter L1, Laws of the Federation of Nigeria, 2004
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Original Enactment:** Labour Act 1974 (re-enacted 1990, compiled 2004)
- **Official Source:** https://placng.org/lawsofnigeria/print.php?sn=205
- **LawGlobal Hub:** https://www.lawglobalhub.com/section-1-6-of-the-nigerian-labour-act-2004/

**Scope of Application:**
- Applies to employees engaged under a contract of manual labour or clerical work
- Does NOT apply to:
  - Persons exercising administrative, executive, technical, or professional functions
  - Members of the employer's family
  - Representatives, agents, or commercial travellers (where employment is not of a manual nature)
  - Persons in the armed forces, police, or government security services
  - Domestic servants in private households

**Key Provisions:**

**Contracts of Employment (Part I):**
- Written particulars of employment must be provided within 3 months (S.7)
- Contracts must specify: name of employer, employee's name, nature of employment, remuneration, hours of work, holiday entitlement, notice period, and other terms (S.7(1))
- Oral contracts recognized but discouraged for periods exceeding 3 months

**Wages (Part II):**
- Wages payable in legal tender only (S.1)
- Prohibition on truck system (payment in kind)
- Deductions permitted only with prior written consent of authorized labour officer (S.4)
- No deduction for absence from work on grounds of civic obligations
- Wages must be paid on time and in full

**Terms and Conditions (Part III):**
- Normal working hours: 8 hours per day, 40 hours per week for most workers
- Rest period: minimum 1 hour per 6 consecutive working hours
- Annual leave: minimum 6 working days per year (12 days for workers under 16)
- Sick leave: minimum 12 working days per year with medical certificate
- Maternity: 12 weeks leave (6 before, 6 after delivery) with at least 50% pay
- Public holidays: national gazette holidays

**Termination (Part IV):**
- Notice period varies by period of service:
  - Less than 3 months service: 1 day notice
  - 3 months to 2 years: 1 week notice
  - 2 to 5 years: 2 weeks notice
  - 5+ years: 1 month notice
- Payment in lieu of notice permitted
- Summary dismissal permitted for gross misconduct (willful disobedience, breach of duty, dishonesty)
- No mandatory redundancy pay under the Labour Act (common law may differ)

**Recruitment and Foreign Workers (Parts V-VI):**
- Restrictions on recruitment of workers for employment outside Nigeria
- Requirements for permits for foreign workers
- Ministerial approval needed for certain categories

### 1.2 National Minimum Wage (Amendment) Act 2024

- **Full Title:** National Minimum Wage (Amendment) Act, 2024
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria (signed by President Bola Tinubu)
- **Date:** July 2024
- **Effective Date:** May 1, 2024 (retroactive)
- **Pavestones Legal Analysis:** https://pavestoneslegal.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/The-National-Minimum-Wage-Amendment-Act-2024.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- New national minimum wage: N70,000 per month (up from N30,000)
- Applies to organizations with 25+ employees
- Employers with fewer than 25 employees may apply for exemption
- State governments and private sector employers must comply
- Retroactive to May 1, 2024 -- arrears calculation required
- Review frequency: minimum every 3 years (previously 5 years)

**State Implementation (as of 2025):**
- Rivers State: N85,000 (highest)
- Bayelsa, Niger, Akwa Ibom: N80,000
- 20 states: N70,000
- Several states still negotiating above-minimum payments

### 1.3 Employee Compensation Act 2010 (ECA)

- **Full Title:** Employees' Compensation Act, 2010
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Official Text:** https://admin.theiguides.org/Media/Documents/Employee%20Compensation%20Act.pdf
- **Mondaq Analysis:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/employee-benefits-compensation/1418622/employee-compensation-act-2010-legal-considerations-and-realities

**Key Provisions:**
- No-fault compensation scheme -- compensation regardless of who caused the workplace incident
- Establishes the Nigeria Social Insurance Trust Fund (NSITF) as administering body
- Employer contribution: 1% of total monthly payroll to the Employee Compensation Fund
- Covers: death, permanent total/partial disability, temporary disability, occupational diseases
- Compensation amounts based on assessed income (monthly earnings)
- Death benefit: 90% of assessed income for surviving spouse/dependents
- Permanent total disability: weekly payments for lifetime plus N100,000 lump sum
- Temporary disability: full wages for first 12 months, then 66.67% thereafter
- Employer must report workplace accidents within 7 days
- Employee must report injury/disease within 14 days
- Penalties for non-compliance: fines up to N250,000 and/or 1 year imprisonment

### 1.4 Pension Reform Act 2014 (PRA)

- **Full Title:** Pension Reform Act, 2014
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Official Text:** https://placng.org/lawsofnigeria/laws/P4.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- Contributory Pension Scheme (CPS) mandatory for organizations with 3+ employees (amended from 15+ under 2004 Act)
- Employer contribution: minimum 10% of employee's monthly emoluments
- Employee contribution: minimum 8% of monthly emoluments
- Voluntary additional contributions permitted (tax-advantaged)
- Retirement savings held in individual Retirement Savings Accounts (RSAs) managed by Pension Fund Administrators (PFAs)
- Pension Fund Custodians (PFCs) hold assets
- National Pension Commission (PenCom) regulates
- Minimum pension guarantee from Pension Transitional Arrangement Directorate (PTAD)
- Retirement age: as specified in employment contract or 50 years if not specified
- Voluntary retirement after 50 years of age

### 1.5 National Health Insurance Authority (NHIA) Act 2022

- **Full Title:** National Health Insurance Authority Act, 2022
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** May 2022
- **Replaces:** National Health Insurance Scheme (NHIS) Act 1999
- **KPMG Analysis:** https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/commentaries-on-the-national-health-insurance.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- Mandatory health insurance for every Nigerian resident
- Employers with 5+ staff must register and contribute to approved health insurance scheme
- Health insurance contribution shared between employer and employee
- Established NHIA to regulate health insurance, replacing NHIS
- State Health Insurance Agencies for state-level implementation
- Vulnerable Groups Fund for indigent populations
- Penalties for non-compliance by employers

### 1.6 Trade Unions Act (Cap T14, LFN 2004)

- **Full Title:** Trade Unions Act
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **NICN Reference:** https://nicnadr.gov.ng/Content/images/Labour-Laws-and-Trade-Unions-in-Nigeria.pdf

**Key Provisions:**
- Right of workers to form and join trade unions (subject to registration)
- Registration with Registrar of Trade Unions required
- Minimum 50 members for registration
- Check-off system: employer deducts union dues from wages with worker's written consent
- Prohibited for members of armed forces, police, and some government employees
- Industrial action: strike action permitted only after mediation and conciliation fail
- Collective bargaining agreements binding on all parties
- Trade Disputes Act governs dispute resolution mechanisms

### 1.7 Factories Act (Cap F1, LFN 2004)

- **Full Title:** Factories Act
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria

**Key Provisions:**
- Registration of factories with the Factories Inspectorate Division
- Health and safety standards: cleanliness, ventilation, lighting, sanitation
- Overcrowding prevention
- Fencing and guarding of dangerous machinery
- Fire precautions and emergency exits
- First aid facilities and personnel
- Hours of work for young persons (under 18)
- Reporting of occupational diseases and accidents
- Inspector powers: entry, examination, investigation
- Penalties for non-compliance

### 1.8 National Industrial Court Act 2006

- **Full Title:** National Industrial Court Act, 2006
- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **NICN Website:** https://nicnadr.gov.ng/
- **Jurisdiction FAQ:** https://nicn.gov.ng/jurisdiction-and-power

**Jurisdiction (enhanced by Constitution Third Alteration Act 2010):**
- Exclusive original jurisdiction over all labour and employment matters
- Trade union disputes
- Collective bargaining disputes
- Workplace discrimination claims
- Child labour matters
- International labour standards
- Unfair labour practices
- Appeals from Registrar of Trade Unions

**Procedure:**
- Pre-trial mediation mandatory
- Simplified procedures for unrepresented parties
- No limitation period for most employment claims (per recent NICN case law)
- Constitutional jurisdiction to apply ILO conventions and international best practices

### 1.9 Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023 -- Employment Provisions

- **Issuing Body:** Federal Republic of Nigeria
- **Date:** June 12, 2023
- **Relevance to Employment:**
  - Employee data is personal data under NDPA
  - Employer as data controller must comply with 7 data protection principles
  - Consent or legitimate interest as lawful basis for processing employee data
  - Employee right to access their personnel records
  - Data protection impact assessment required for employee monitoring systems
  - Cross-border transfer rules apply to sharing employee data with group companies abroad

### 1.10 Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) 2020 -- Employment Provisions

- **Relevant Sections:**
  - Section 87: Company secretary qualifications
  - Section 282-284: Directors' duties (duty of care creates employment-adjacent obligations)
  - Section 303: Directors' service contracts must be available for member inspection
  - Section 378: Voluntary winding up -- employee priority claims
  - Section 434-435: Preferential payments in winding up -- employee wages and salaries have priority

### 1.11 Immigration Act 2015 and Expatriate Quota Regulations

- **Relevance:** Employment of foreign nationals
- **Key Requirements:** Expatriate quota approval, Combined Expatriate Residence Permit and Aliens Card (CERPAC), Subject-to-Regularisation (STR) visa, obligation to understudy/train Nigerian replacement

### 1.12 Personal Income Tax Act (PITA) -- As Amended by Nigeria Tax Act 2025

- **Relevance:** Employer obligations as tax agent
- **Key Provisions:**
  - PAYE (Pay As You Earn) withholding obligations
  - Tax rates: graduated from 7% to maximum 25% (reduced from 24% for high earners under 2025 reform)
  - Exemption for earnings up to N800,000/year under 2025 reforms
  - Employer must remit PAYE deductions monthly
  - Annual returns filing obligation
  - New NRS replaces FIRS from January 1, 2026

### 1.13 Lagos State Employment Trust Fund Law

- **Issuing Body:** Lagos State Government
- **Relevance:** State-level employment programs, N25 billion fund for employment generation
- **Note:** Lagos has the most developed state employment law ecosystem in Nigeria

### 1.14 ILO Core Conventions Ratified by Nigeria

Nigeria has ratified the following core ILO conventions with employment law implications:
- C029 -- Forced Labour Convention (1960)
- C087 -- Freedom of Association and Protection of the Right to Organise (1960)
- C098 -- Right to Organise and Collective Bargaining Convention (1960)
- C100 -- Equal Remuneration Convention (1960)
- C105 -- Abolition of Forced Labour Convention (1960)
- C111 -- Discrimination (Employment and Occupation) Convention (2002)
- C138 -- Minimum Age Convention (2002)
- C182 -- Worst Forms of Child Labour Convention (2002)

**Relevance:** NICN has constitutional jurisdiction to apply ILO conventions in employment disputes under the Third Alteration Act 2010.

### 1.15 Child Rights Act 2003

- **Relevance:** Prohibition of child labour (under 12 years), restrictions on employment of young persons (12-16 years), prohibition of hazardous employment for persons under 18
- **Note:** Only adopted in 24 of 36 states as of 2025

### 1.16 HIV/AIDS Anti-Discrimination Act 2014

- **Relevance:** Prohibition on workplace discrimination based on HIV/AIDS status, prohibition on mandatory HIV testing as condition of employment, confidentiality of HIV status in workplace

## 4. NIGERIAN CASE STUDIES

### 4.1 National Minimum Wage Implementation Disputes (2024-2025)
- **Year:** 2024-2025
- **Organizations:** Federal Government, State Governments, NLC (Nigeria Labour Congress), TUC (Trade Union Congress)
- **Summary:** Following the National Minimum Wage (Amendment) Act 2024 raising minimum wage from N30,000 to N70,000, protracted negotiations between organized labour and government ensued. NLC threatened general strike. Implementation varied across states -- some paid above (Rivers: N85,000), many delayed implementation. Arrears calculation from May 2024 effective date created compliance challenges.
- **Teaching Points:** Minimum wage compliance obligations; arrears calculation; state-by-state implementation differences; collective bargaining dynamics; strike action legal framework

### 4.2 Mr Constantine Alimonos v Honeywell Flour Mills Plc (NICN, July 2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Organizations:** Honeywell Flour Mills Plc
- **Summary:** NICN confirmed that a fixed-term contract can end without stating a reason in the absence of a renewal agreement. However, failing to give required notice may still constitute wrongful termination requiring damages.
- **Source:** NICN/LA/161/2023, decided July 28, 2025
- **Teaching Points:** Fixed-term contracts; notice requirements; distinction between lawful non-renewal and wrongful termination

### 4.3 Chibuzor Albert Agulana v Dr Fabian Okonkwo (NICN, April 2024)
- **Year:** 2024
- **Organizations:** Private employer
- **Summary:** NICN held it had jurisdiction over defamation claims arising from employment. Found that unproven accusations of dishonesty and crimes in an employee's dismissal letter constituted defamation. Awarded damages.
- **Source:** NICN/EN/35/2021, delivered April 17, 2024
- **Teaching Points:** Employer liability for defamatory dismissal communications; importance of factual basis for dismissal reasons; NICN defamation jurisdiction

### 4.4 Ibrahim M. Gidado v National Space Research & Development Agency (NICN, January 2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Organizations:** National Space Research & Development Agency (NASRDA)
- **Summary:** Court frowned upon deprivation of claimant's salaries for over six years. Ordered immediate payment of all outstanding salaries plus general damages assessed at equivalent of one year's salary.
- **Source:** NICN, January 21, 2025
- **Teaching Points:** Employer obligation to pay salaries; consequences of salary withholding; general damages assessment; government employer obligations

### 4.5 Mr Yannet Joshua v DAAR Communication Plc (NICN, February 2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Organizations:** DAAR Communication Plc (AIT/RayPower)
- **Summary:** Seven-year salary deprivation. Court ordered immediate payment alongside general damages equivalent to a year's salary. Part of emerging NICN pattern of penalizing prolonged salary non-payment.
- **Source:** NICN, February 4, 2025
- **Teaching Points:** Media sector employment issues; prolonged salary non-payment consequences; NICN remedies

### 4.6 Abbey Iyobu Robert v Port Harcourt Electricity Distribution Company (NICN, July 2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Organizations:** Port Harcourt Electricity Distribution Company (PHED)
- **Summary:** NICN held that employer must provide a safe system of work, proper instructions, and reasonable supervision. Landmark decision on employer's duty of care and workplace safety obligations.
- **Source:** NICN/PHC/43/2024, decided July 1, 2025
- **Teaching Points:** Employer duty of care; safe system of work; workplace negligence; Employee Compensation Act interface

### 4.7 Windowstory Nig. Ltd v Mr. Peter J. Ayuba (NICN, December 2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Organizations:** Windowstory Nig. Ltd
- **Summary:** Court refused to restrain former employee from competing. Held that non-compete clauses seeking to restrain post-employment activities without properly proving existence or misuse of trade secrets were unreasonable and unenforceable.
- **Source:** NICN, December 10, 2025
- **Teaching Points:** Post-employment restrictive covenants; non-compete enforceability in Nigeria; trade secrets burden of proof; contractual drafting lessons

### 4.8 Adefisoye Stephen Abiodun v Safari Support West Limited (NICN, September 2024)
- **Year:** 2024
- **Organizations:** Safari Support West Limited
- **Summary:** Court reiterated that though termination for cause can occur during probation, the employer must present evidence on the plausibility of the termination when challenged. Probation is not a blanket shield for arbitrary dismissal.
- **Source:** NICN, September 17, 2024
- **Teaching Points:** Probationary employees' rights; due process even during probation; evidential requirements for termination for cause

### 4.9 NICN Limitation Period Evolution (2025)
- **Year:** 2025
- **Summary:** In March 2025, NICN declined to apply limitation statutes to employment disputes, reasoning that the Constitution's Third Alteration expanded NICN jurisdiction to apply international labour conventions. This creates uncertainty for employers about the time window for employment claims.
- **Teaching Points:** No clear limitation period for NICN claims; employers must maintain records indefinitely; constitutional basis of NICN jurisdiction

### 4.10 Tax Reform and NRS Transition Impact on Employment (2025-2026)
- **Year:** 2025-2026
- **Organizations:** FIRS (dissolved), Nigeria Revenue Service (NRS), employers
- **Summary:** Nigeria Tax Act 2025 and NRS Establishment Act 2025 overhauled the tax system. Key employment impacts: PAYE maximum rate adjusted, exemption for incomes up to N800,000/year, new contractor vs. employee classification rules, remote worker tax obligations, platform economy taxation. Takes effect January 1, 2026.
- **Teaching Points:** Employer tax agent obligations; contractor classification risks; remote worker considerations; compliance transition planning
