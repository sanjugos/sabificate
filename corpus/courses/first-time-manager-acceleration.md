## 1. Management Frameworks

### 1.1 Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard / Blanchard SLII)

**Source:** Ken Blanchard & Paul Hersey; updated as SLII by Blanchard (blanchard.com)

**Core Concept:** No single "best" leadership style. Leaders flex along two behavioral dimensions -- direction (task) and support (relationship) -- to match the follower's development level for a specific task.

**Development Levels:**
- D1: Enthusiastic Beginner (low competence, high commitment)
- D2: Disillusioned Learner (some competence, low commitment)
- D3: Capable but Cautious (moderate-to-high competence, variable commitment)
- D4: Self-Reliant Achiever (high competence, high commitment)

**Matching Leadership Styles:** Directing (S1), Coaching (S2), Supporting (S3), Delegating (S4)

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. Maps extremely well to Nigerian hierarchical culture where new employees expect directive leadership. Helps first-time managers calibrate when to shift from directive to supportive as team members gain competence -- countering the default Nigerian management tendency toward permanent top-down direction.

### 1.2 Radical Candor (Kim Scott)

**Source:** Kim Scott, "Radical Candor" (2017); radicalcandor.com

**Core Concept:** Balance Caring Personally with Challenging Directly. Framework creates four quadrants:
- **Radical Candor** (care + challenge): The goal
- **Ruinous Empathy** (care without challenge): Most common management failure
- **Obnoxious Aggression** (challenge without care): The "brilliant jerk"
- **Manipulative Insincerity** (neither care nor challenge): Political behavior

**Order of Operations:** Solicit feedback first, then offer specific praise, then give kind and clear criticism.

**Nigerian Relevance:** CRITICAL BUT REQUIRES ADAPTATION. Nigerian culture defaults to Ruinous Empathy (avoiding confrontation to save face) or Obnoxious Aggression (harsh criticism from "big boss" position). Radical Candor gives a framework to deliver feedback that respects hierarchy while being honest. Must be adapted to incorporate indirect communication norms and the saving-face principle -- feedback delivered privately, wrapped in care, with softened language.

### 1.3 The Coaching Habit (Michael Bungay Stanier)

**Source:** Michael Bungay Stanier, "The Coaching Habit" (2016)

**Seven Questions Framework:**
1. The Kickstart Question: "What's on your mind?"
2. The AWE Question: "And what else?"
3. The Focus Question: "What's the real challenge here for you?"
4. The Foundation Question: "What do you want?"
5. The Lazy Question: "How can I help?"
6. The Strategic Question: "If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"
7. The Learning Question: "What was most useful for you?"

**Philosophy:** Stay curious longer, rush to advice more slowly. Be more coach-like: ask questions instead of giving answers.

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. Directly counters the Nigerian management default of "the boss must have all the answers." Coaching questions empower subordinates to think independently while maintaining the relationship. Especially powerful for managing former peers.

### 1.4 Manager Tools Trinity

**Source:** Manager Tools podcast (manager-tools.com) by Mark Horstman & Mike Auzenne

**The Trinity (four components):**
1. **One-on-Ones** (Know Your People): Weekly 30-minute meetings, 10 min for direct, 10 min for manager, 10 min for future
2. **Feedback** (Talk About Performance): Specific, behavioral, timely praise and correction
3. **Coaching** (Ask For More): Helping directs improve skills
4. **Delegation** (Push Work Down): Systematically moving work to develop people

**Implementation:** Roll out one at a time. Most managers fail trying to implement everything at once.

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. One-on-ones are rare in Nigerian management culture. Introducing structured weekly check-ins represents a major differentiator. The "push work down" delegation model directly addresses the Nigerian tendency toward centralized decision-making.

### 1.5 Google Project Oxygen (10 Behaviors of Great Managers)

**Source:** Google re:Work, Project Oxygen (2008-2018); rework.withgoogle.com

**10 Behaviors (evolved from original 8):**
1. Be a good coach
2. Empower the team without micromanaging
3. Create a team environment that values everyone
4. Focus on productivity and results
5. Communicate effectively
6. Support career development and discuss performance
7. Implement a clear vision and strategy
8. Have key technical skills
9. Collaborate across the organization
10. Make strong decisions

**Research Basis:** Analyzed 10,000+ data points from performance reviews and feedback surveys, plus hundreds of pages of interview notes.

**Key Finding:** Teams with highly effective managers achieved better results, were happier, and experienced lower turnover.

**Nigerian Relevance:** MODERATE-HIGH. Behavior #2 (empowerment without micromanaging) is the hardest in Nigerian context. Behavior #3 (inclusive environment) maps to diversity management challenges. Behavior #1 (coaching) is the biggest development opportunity.

### 1.6 Gallup Q12 Employee Engagement Framework

**Source:** Gallup, Inc.; Q12 meta-analysis spanning 2.7M workers across 2.5M+ work units

**Key Statistic:** Managers account for at least 70% of the variance in employee engagement scores across business units (Gallup, 2015 -- "State of the American Manager" by Randall Beck and Jim Harter).

**12 Engagement Drivers:**
1. I know what is expected of me at work
2. I have the materials and equipment I need
3. I have the opportunity to do what I do best every day
4. In the last seven days, I have received recognition or praise
5. My supervisor, or someone at work, seems to care about me as a person
6. There is someone at work who encourages my development
7. At work, my opinions seem to count
8. The mission of my organization makes me feel my job is important
9. My associates are committed to quality work
10. I have a best friend at work
11. In the last six months, someone has talked to me about my progress
12. I have had opportunities to learn and grow

**Impact Data:**
- High-engagement organizations see 81% lower absenteeism, 18% lower turnover, 18% higher productivity
- Global engagement fell to 21% in 2024 (down from 23% in 2023)
- Manager engagement fell from 30% to 27%

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. Items #1 (expectations), #4 (recognition), #5 (care as person), and #7 (opinions count) are the biggest gaps in Nigerian management culture where hierarchy suppresses voice and recognition culture is underdeveloped.

### 1.7 The First 90 Days (Michael Watkins)

**Source:** Michael Watkins, "The First 90 Days" (2003, updated 2013); Harvard Business Review Press

**Core Framework:** Three phases -- Preparation, Immersion, Acceleration

**STARS Diagnostic:** Diagnose whether you are in a:
- **S**tart-up
- **T**urnaround
- **A**ccelerated growth
- **R**ealignment
- **S**ustaining success

**10 Transition Challenges:**
Promote yourself, accelerate learning, match strategy to situation, negotiate success, secure early wins, achieve alignment, build your team, create alliances, manage yourself, expedite everyone.

**Key Insight:** By applying the 90-day framework, leaders reach the "breakeven point" up to 40% faster.

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. Maps directly to the SABIficate course's "first 90 days" module. Securing early wins and creating alliances are especially important in Nigerian hierarchical organizations where new managers must establish credibility quickly.

### 1.8 Five Dysfunctions of a Team (Patrick Lencioni)

**Source:** Patrick Lencioni, "The Five Dysfunctions of a Team" (2002); ISBN 978-0-7879-6075-9

**The Pyramid of Dysfunctions (bottom to top):**
1. Absence of Trust (foundation)
2. Fear of Conflict
3. Lack of Commitment
4. Avoidance of Accountability
5. Inattention to Results

**Nigerian Relevance:** HIGH. Trust and conflict avoidance are the two most relevant dysfunctions in Nigerian teams. The collectivist culture discourages open disagreement, which leads to artificial harmony and lack of genuine commitment. First-time managers need to learn to build psychological safety within Nigerian cultural norms.

## 2. Nigerian Workplace Culture & Context

### 2.1 Hofstede Cultural Dimensions for Nigeria

**Source:** Geert Hofstede; multiple academic papers applying to Nigeria (ResearchGate, BusinessDay NG)

| Dimension | Score | Implication for Managers |
|-----------|-------|------------------------|
| Power Distance | 80 (High) | People accept hierarchical order. Boss decisions are final. |
| Individualism | 30 (Collectivist) | Group loyalty overrides rules. Hiring/promotion influenced by in-group. |
| Masculinity | 60 (Moderate-High) | Achievement and success orientation |
| Uncertainty Avoidance | 55 (Moderate) | Some tolerance for ambiguity |
| Long-Term Orientation | 13 (Short-term) | Focus on quick results, tradition |
| Indulgence | 84 (High) | Optimistic, value enjoyment |

### 2.2 Nigerian Management Style Characteristics

**Sources:** Commisceo Global Management Guide; World Business Culture; Navigating Nigeria blog

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Benevolent autocrat** is the preferred leadership style
- Managers provide explicit instructions and expect strict adherence
- Little delegation of authority; managers tend to be openly critical of performance
- Decisions develop slowly and should not be rushed
- "Getting-to-know-you conversations" are crucial before business
- Deadlines and timescales are viewed as flexible rather than rigid
- Managers show paternalistic concern beyond professional matters

### 2.3 Communication Norms

**Sources:** Rivermate cultural guide; Commisceo Global; Studocu impact analysis

- **Indirect, high-context communication** for sensitive topics
- "Yes" may mean compliance rather than agreement
- Criticism softened and wrapped in positives
- Raised voices during discussion indicate engagement, not anger
- Titles must be used: "Sir," "Ma," "Doctor," "Chief," "Boss" -- not optional
- Excessive directness is perceived as rude or aggressive
- Softening phrases recommended: "Would you be able to..." "Might we consider..."
- Real negotiations happen privately, not in formal meetings
- Feedback should be delivered privately to preserve dignity

### 2.4 Diversity Management

**Sources:** Multiple academic papers on ResearchGate (Olugbode et al.; Ugwueze et al.)

- Nigeria has 250-400 different ethno-linguistic groups
- Three dominant groups: Yoruba (Southwest), Igbo (Southeast), Hausa-Fulani (North)
- Regional cultural variations affect management:
  - **Lagos/Southwest (Yoruba):** Formal greetings, hierarchical, title-conscious
  - **Southeast (Igbo):** Achievement-oriented, entrepreneurial, title-conscious
  - **North (Hausa-Fulani):** Conservative, gender-separated, Friday prayer observance critical
  - **South-South:** Community-focused
- Friday prayers (Muslim) and religious holidays are normal workplace interruptions
- Faith-based conversations are standard workplace discourse
- Managing ethno-religious diversity is a key competency for Nigerian managers
- Proper diversity management correlates with improved corporate performance

### 2.5 Delegation in Nigerian Context

**Sources:** ResearchGate research; LinkedIn delegation challenges

- High power distance creates reluctance to delegate (fear of losing authority)
- Trust deficit between levels of hierarchy inhibits delegation
- "Control concerns" -- managers fear losing track of what is happening
- Skills gaps in subordinates cited as barrier to delegation
- Academic studies show positive relationship between delegation and innovation in Nigerian companies
- First-time managers must overcome cultural conditioning that says "the boss does everything important"

### 2.6 Performance Management in Nigeria

**Sources:** NotchHR; Mondaq; academic studies on MTN Nigeria, Ogun State civil servants

- Many Nigerian organizations lack effective performance appraisal systems
- Key dimensions often missing: goal setting, learning/development, effective communication, feedback systems
- Nigerian Labour Act and Employee's Compensation Act govern compliance framework
- Best practices include: objective appraisal, regular feedback, career development
- MTN Nigeria case study shows telecom sector as more advanced in performance management adoption

## 5. Nigerian Case Studies

### 5.1 Zenith Bank: Mass Promotion Wave (January 2025)

**What happened:** Over 4,000 employees (approximately 49% of 8,146 workforce) promoted across various grades, with 20-30% salary increases effective January 2025.

**Leadership Context:** First major personnel action under new MD/CEO Adaora Umeoji, who focused on building staff morale after an exodus of skilled staff seeking opportunities abroad, partly driven by IT disruptions from late-2024 software upgrades.

**Teaching Points:**
- Mass promotions create mass first-time managers overnight
- How do 2,000+ newly promoted supervisors/managers learn to lead?
- Retention strategy through compensation vs. development
- Challenge: Personnel expenses rose to N150.5B in 9 months of 2024 vs. N124B for all of 2023
- Risk: Promoted without training = "accidental managers"

**Sources:** Nairametrics (Jan 23, 2025); Business Post Nigeria; Investors King

### 5.2 Moniepoint: Scaling Judgment (2019-2025)

**What happened:** Pivoted from infrastructure provider TeamApt to small-business banking platform Moniepoint. Raised $110M at $1B valuation in late 2024. Processes 800M+ transactions monthly. Profitable.

**Leadership Evolution (CEO Tosin Eniolorunda):**
- Transitioned from coding to CEO duties -- "humbling and clarifying"
- Key insight: "Scaling a company hinges less on adding features or customers and more on scaling judgment"
- Management philosophy: Empowering specialists, building high-performing teams, investing heavily in culture, alignment, and communication
- Built dedicated women-in-tech pipeline: 5 cohorts, 95% retention rate

**Teaching Points:**
- Transition from technical contributor to manager/leader
- "Scaling judgment" as the core leadership challenge
- Building inclusive teams deliberately (women-in-tech pipeline)
- When to step in vs. step back as a leader

**Sources:** TechAndBiz (Nov 2025); TechCabal (Feb 2026); Maglazana (Jun 2025)

### 5.3 Paystack: Building Culture Through Acquisition (2015-2020)

**What happened:** Co-founded by Shola Akinlade and Ezra Olubi in 2015. Y Combinator 2015. Stripe led $8M Series A in 2018. Acquired by Stripe for $200M in 2020 -- largest Nigerian startup acquisition at the time.

**Teaching Points:**
- Building company culture from startup through acquisition
- Maintaining team identity within a larger organization (Stripe)
- Managing rapid growth (few employees to hundreds)
- Aligning mission and values through organizational change

**Sources:** Quartz Africa; The Flip podcast; Stripe newsroom

### 5.4 NRS (Nigeria Revenue Service): Institutional Transformation (2023-2026)

**What happened:** President Tinubu appointed Zacch Adedeji as FIRS chairman in September 2023. Led rebranding from FIRS to NRS. Launched Rev360 digital tax administration platform (April 2026). Completed NRS House after 22-year delay.

**Key Quote (Adedeji at 2026 NRS Leadership Retreat):** "Institutional transformation begins with personal transformation. Reform is not first a technical exercise; it is a human one."

**Teaching Points:**
- Leading organizational change in government/bureaucracy
- Personal transformation as prerequisite for institutional transformation
- Change management in hierarchical, traditional organizations
- Phased implementation strategy (medium taxpayers first, then larger entities)
- "Seamless transition" -- managing change so stakeholders pivot without struggling

**Sources:** Leadership.ng; BusinessDay; ThisDay Live

### 5.5 Andela: Remote Team Management at Scale

**What happened:** Founded 2014 in Lagos. Harvard Business School case study (Case 321-113, January 2021). Built engineering talent platform across Nigeria, Kenya, Rwanda, Uganda. Pivoted to full-remote model. Raised $200M Series D from SoftBank (2021) at unicorn valuation.

**Management Challenges:**
- Laid off 420 junior engineers in 2019 during restructuring
- Transitioned from hub-based to fully remote model
- 91% of African developers preferred remote/hybrid work
- Managing distributed teams across time zones and cultures

**Teaching Points:**
- Remote/hybrid team management
- Making difficult personnel decisions (layoffs)
- Pivoting business model while maintaining team morale
- Building trust in distributed teams

**Sources:** Harvard Business School (Tarun Khanna et al.); TechCrunch; TechBuild Africa

### 5.6 Flutterwave: Governance Failures as Management Lesson

**What happened:** Rapid growth exposed weaknesses in governance, compliance, risk management, and internal controls. From April 2022: allegations of mismanagement, sexual harassment, administrative errors, security breaches. Three senior finance officials departed November 2023. $27M cyber incident (February 2023). Kenya froze $52.3M (later released). CEO GB Agboola worked on rebuilding trust.

**Teaching Points:**
- What happens when a company scales faster than its management capabilities
- Corporate governance as a management responsibility, not just a board function
- The cost of ignoring people management during hypergrowth
- Rebuilding trust after management failures

**Sources:** BusinessDay; TechCrunch; CNN interview with Agboola; Contrary Research

### 5.7 First Bank of Nigeria: Leadership Acceleration Programme (LAP)

**What happened:** First Bank (via First Academy, its training division) developed a formal Leadership Acceleration Programme to build management pipeline. Academic case study (Journal of Education and Learning, 2016) documented four effective strategies.

**Training Strategies Found Effective:**
1. Multi-modal training (classroom + online + on-the-job)
2. Structured mentoring programs
3. Shadow management coaching
4. Personal development and work-life balance initiatives

**Teaching Points:**
- Formalized leadership development programs work in Nigerian banking
- Multi-modal training > single-channel training
- Mentoring as a cultural fit for Nigeria's relationship-oriented workplaces
- Research-backed model for replication

**Sources:** Lawal, Journal of Education and Learning, Vol. 5, No. 2 (2016)

### 5.8 Dangote Group: Management Styles Research

**What happened:** Academic research examined how different leadership styles (transformational, autocratic, transactional) affect employee performance at Dangote Nigeria Plc.

**Key Finding:** All three leadership styles significantly affect employee performance but at varying degrees. Transformational leadership showed the strongest positive correlation.

**Teaching Points:**
- Even in a strong-founder culture, leadership style matters
- Nigerian employees respond to different styles differently
- The "visionary leader" model (Dangote) requires managers who can translate vision into execution

**Sources:** ResearchGate (2023); University of Wales Trinity Saint David repository

## 7. Regulatory & Professional Body Requirements

### 7.1 Nigeria Labour Act (2004)

- Principal legislation governing employment relations
- Employer obligations: wages in legal tender, minimum wage compliance, health/safety, 21 days annual leave, 12 days sick leave
- Application limited to contracts of manual labour or clerical work
- ILO notes the Act is "insufficient for modern workforce protection"
- First-time managers must understand basic employment obligations

### 7.2 National Minimum Wage Act

- Employers must pay not less than national minimum wage
- Managers responsible for ensuring compliance in their teams
- Understanding of deduction rules

### 7.3 Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023

- Relevant to managers handling employee data
- Performance records, health information, personal details
- Cross-referenced with SABIficate regulatory corpus

### 7.4 CIPM Nigeria (Chartered Institute of Personnel Management)

- Apex HR professional body in Nigeria
- 5-stage examination process (Foundation through Professional II)
- 2026 learning calendar includes leadership, soft skills, public sector programmes
- CPD requirements for members
- SABIficate courses could potentially count toward CIPM CPD hours

### 7.5 ICAN (Institute of Chartered Accountants of Nigeria)

- 63,000+ members
- 40 CPD credit hours required annually
- Executive MCPD programmes include general management
- SABIficate management courses could target ICAN members needing CPD credits

### 7.6 ILO Decent Work Framework

- Nigeria member since 1960; ratified 44 ILO Conventions
- Decent Work Country Programme covers protection, employment promotion, gender equality, social protection
- National Employment Policy 2025 addresses youth unemployment, informality, gender inequality

## 8. Recommended Module Structure

### Module 1: The Transition Mindset (3 lessons)

**Source Basis:** First 90 Days (Watkins), Gallup 70% statistic, CMI accidental manager research, Google Project Oxygen

**Lesson 1.1: From Doer to Leader -- Why This Transition Matters**
- Learning Objectives:
  - Explain why management quality drives 70% of engagement variance
  - Identify the three biggest mindset shifts from individual contributor to manager
  - Recognize the "accidental manager" trap and how to avoid it
- Key Content: Gallup 70% stat, 60% failure rate, CMI 82% untrained stat, identity shift from "best performer" to "enabler of performance"

4. **Gender dynamics in management:** Nigerian gender dynamics for first-time female managers managing male subordinates (and vice versa). Hofstede masculinity score suggests challenges that need practical guidance.

5. **Salary/compensation conversations:** Nigerian-specific norms around discussing pay, handling requests for salary advances (common in Nigerian workplaces), and managing expectations around "egunje" (informal payments/tips).

6. **Managing in the informal sector:** Many SABIficate learners may manage in semi-formal or informal business contexts. Frameworks assume formal corporate structures.

7. **WhatsApp management dynamics:** Nigerian teams rely heavily on WhatsApp for work communication. Specific guidance on managing via WhatsApp (boundaries, professional communication, group management) is missing from all frameworks.

8. **"Big Man" syndrome:** How first-time managers avoid falling into the Nigerian "big man" trap where promotion leads to self-aggrandizement rather than servant leadership.

9. **NYSC corps member management:** Many Nigerian teams include National Youth Service Corps members. Specific management dynamics for overseeing NYSC members need documentation.

10. **Family/community obligations impact:** Nigerian collectivist culture means employees frequently have family obligations (funerals, marriages, naming ceremonies) that affect work. How first-time managers handle these requests.

11. **Power/infrastructure challenges:** Managing team productivity when NEPA/PHCN power is unreliable, internet goes down, or transportation issues affect attendance. Nigerian-specific productivity management.
