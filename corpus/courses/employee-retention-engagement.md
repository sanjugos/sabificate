### 1.1 Nigerian Labour Act (Cap L1, LFN 2004; as amended 2025)

**Key Provisions Relevant to Retention:**
- **Section 7(1)**: Employers must provide written contracts within 3 months of employment, specifying nature of service, tenure, remuneration, hours of work, notice periods, and grounds for dismissal
- **Section 11**: Termination notice periods graduated by tenure:
  - 3 months or less: 1 day notice
  - More than 3 months but less than 2 years: 1 week
  - 2-5 years: 2 weeks
  - 5+ years: 1 month
- Annual leave: 21 days after 12 months continuous service (12 days for workers under 16)
- Sick leave: Up to 12 days paid sick leave per year with medical certification
- Wages must be paid at least once per month

**Recent Amendments:**
- **National Minimum Wage Amendment Act 2024** (July 2024): Raised minimum wage from N30,000 to N70,000; reduced review cycle from 5 to 3 years. Some states paying above: Lagos N85,000-N104,000
- **Labour Laws (Amendments) Act, No. 4 of 2025** (assented 4 March 2025): Extends Labour Act scope to all sectors (previously excluded professionals/executives); improves worker protections and dispute resolution
- At current exchange rates (~N1,600/$), N70,000 = approximately $42/month. US State Department report noted naira devaluation has eroded the wage increase; N70,000 barely covers one week of living in some communities

### 1.2 Employees' Compensation Act (ECA) 2010

**Full Name:** Employees' Compensation Act, 2010 (No. 13 of 2010) -- replaced the Workmen's Compensation Act 1987.

**Key Provisions:**
- **Mandatory employer contribution**: 1% of total monthly payroll into the Employees' Compensation Fund, due before the 16th of the following month
- Coverage: All employees in public and private sectors (except armed forces)
- No-fault liability principle: employees entitled to compensation without proving employer negligence
- Compensable events: workplace injuries, occupational diseases, mental stress, hearing impairment, commute injuries, death
- Benefits: medical treatment, rehabilitation, periodic disability payments (up to 90% of wages for permanent disability), dependants' benefits
- **Penalties**: Imprisonment up to 1 year and/or fines (N100,000 individuals; N1,000,000 corporate) for non-compliance

**Administering Body:** Nigeria Social Insurance Trust Fund (NSITF)

### 1.3 Pension Reform Act (PRA) 2014

**Key Provisions:**
- **Section 4(1)**: Mandatory contributory pension for all employers with 3 or more employees
- **Contribution rates**: Employer minimum 10% + Employee minimum 8% of monthly emoluments = 18% total. Where employer bears full cost, minimum is 20%
- Contributions deposited into employee's personal Retirement Savings Account (RSA) with a licensed Pension Fund Administrator (PFA)
- Employers must also provide group life insurance coverage
- Both mandatory and voluntary contributions are tax-deductible
- RSAs are fully portable -- employees keep their account when changing jobs

**Recent Developments:**
- Q2 2024: 483,464 Nigerians withdrew N247.47 billion from RSAs due to job losses (25% withdrawal provision)
- 2025: PenCom launched "Pension Revolution 2.0" reform agenda
- New pension rules expected to unlock approximately $600 million for private equity investments
- House of Representatives called for comprehensive review, citing scheme's failure to protect retirees against inflation

**Retention Relevance:** Pension is one of the most powerful retention tools. Employers differentiate by contributing above the 10% minimum, offering voluntary contribution matching, and choosing PFAs with strong returns.

**Administering Body:** National Pension Commission (PenCom)

**Sources:**

### 1.4 National Housing Fund (NHF) Act (No. 3 of 1992; as amended 2023)

**Key Provisions:**
- Employee contribution: 2.5% of basic monthly salary to the NHF through the Federal Mortgage Bank of Nigeria (FMBN)
- **Critical 2023 Amendment (Business Facilitation Act 2022)**: NHF contributions are now VOLUNTARY for private sector employees. Public sector employees remain mandated
- Housing loan benefits: up to 90% of cost of house; interest fixed at 6% p.a. throughout mortgage life
- Eligibility requires NHF contributor status (minimum 6 months of contributions)

**Recent Performance:**
- FMBN collected N103 billion in NHF in 2024
- Record N152.4 billion collected in 2025 (48% increase YoY)
- 139,000+ new contributors registered in 2025; 178,619 in 2024
- Federal Government approved N10 billion housing loan scheme for civil servants

**Retention Relevance:** Even though NHF is now voluntary for private sector, offering NHF participation as an employee benefit is a strong retention tool. The 6% fixed-rate mortgage is well below market rates, making this a high-value benefit.

### 1.5 National Health Insurance Authority (NHIA) Act 2022

**Key Provisions:**
- **Section 14**: Every employer with 5 or more employees must enrol employees in an NHIA-approved health insurance scheme
- Coverage scope: Employee + 1 spouse + up to 4 biological children under 18
- Contribution rate: Employer contributes 10% of employee's basic monthly salary (with additional 5% employee contribution per some guidelines)
- Represents shift from voluntary to mandatory health insurance
- Replaces the NHIS Act of 1999

**Recent Developments:**
- July 2024: Lagos State issued executive order requiring all residents, employers, and workers to comply
- September 2025: President Tinubu directed all MDAs to enrol employees without further delay
- October 2025: House of Representatives mandated NHIA to compile defaulting private companies and impose sanctions
- June 2026: Enhanced enforcement and registration drives underway for workers nationwide

**Retention Relevance:** Health insurance is now a mandatory benefit. Employers differentiate by offering coverage exceeding NHIA minimum (more dependants, dental/optical), choosing premium HMOs, and adding wellness programs.

### 1.6 Industrial Training Fund (ITF) Act (Cap I9, LFN 2004)

**Key Provisions:**
- Employers with 25+ employees OR annual turnover of N50 million+ must contribute 1% of annual payroll to ITF
- Payroll includes total of all basic pay, allowances, and other entitlements
- Covers full-time, part-time, and temporary workers (30+ days)
- Payment due not later than 1 April of the following year
- Penalty: 5% per month on outstanding contributions

**Training Reimbursement (Key Retention Feature):**
- Compliant employers can claim reimbursement of up to 50% of qualifying training expenditure
- Requirements: At least 15% of workforce must be trained annually
- Qualifying expenditure: external trainer fees, training materials, internally delivered programs, facilitator costs, apprenticeship costs

**Retention Relevance:** Directly linked to retention through learning and development. Banks and fintechs can maximize the 50% reimbursement by structuring training programs to meet ITF criteria, positioning training investment as a retention benefit.

### 1.8 Employees' Housing Scheme / ESOP Framework

**Employees' Housing Scheme:**
- Operates through the NHF (see 1.4 above) -- no separate legislation exists
- FMBN administers the fund and provides mortgage products
- Increasingly used by employers as a retention benefit by facilitating NHF enrollment and supplementing with employer-assisted housing programs

**Employee Stock Option Plans (ESOPs) in Nigeria:**
- No specific ESOP legislation in Nigeria, but increasingly used by fintechs and startups
- Nigerian startups typically set aside 10-15% equity as an ESOP pool in early stages
- LIRS (Lagos State Internal Revenue Service) issued 2017 notice mandating tax payment on employee shares/stock options
- Tax treatment: gain = fair market value minus purchase price; dividends during vesting subject to personal income tax
- Nigerian Exchange Group introduced equity-based incentives in 2021

**Sources:**
