### 1.1 Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance 2018 (NCCG 2018)

- **Full Title:** Nigerian Code of Corporate Governance 2018
- **Issuing Body:** Financial Reporting Council of Nigeria (FRCN), issued by the Honourable Minister for Industry, Trade and Investment
- **Date Issued:** January 15, 2019
- **Effective Date:** January 1, 2020 (extended to July 31, 2020 due to COVID-19)
- **Official Source:** https://frcnigeria.gov.ng/publications/
- **ICSAN Mirror:** https://icsan.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance-2018.pdf
- **KPMG Analysis:** https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/advisory/2018-Nigerian-Code-of-Corporate-Governance.pdf
- **ECGI Listing:** https://www.ecgi.global/publications/codes/nigerian-code-of-corporate-governance-2018

**Structure:** 7 Parts containing 28 Principles with recommended practices for each principle

**Distribution of 28 Principles Across 7 Parts:**

| Part | Topic Area | Number of Principles |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Part 1 | Board of Directors and Officers of the Board | 16 principles |
| Part 2 | Assurance (Risk Management, Internal Audit, Whistleblowing) | 4 principles |
| Part 3 | Relationship with Shareholders | 3 principles |
| Part 4 | Business Conduct and Ethics | 2 principles |
| Part 5 | Sustainability | 1 principle |
| Part 6 | Transparency | 2 principles |
| Part 7 | Definitions | -- |

**Compliance Approach:** "Apply and Explain" -- assumes application of all principles; entities explain how principles are applied (distinct from UK "Comply or Explain")

**Key Provisions:**
- Board composition: appropriate mix of Executive Directors (EDs), Non-Executive Directors (NEDs), and Independent Non-Executive Directors (INEDs); majority must be NEDs
- Chairman must be NED, not involved in day-to-day operations; separation of Chairman and CEO roles
- Four mandatory board committees: Audit, Nomination/Governance, Remuneration, and Risk Management
- Board evaluation: annual assessments; external evaluation by independent consultant at least every 3 years
- External auditor rotation: maximum 10-year tenure
- Internal audit function required (or documented reasons for non-establishment)
- Whistleblowing framework required
- Sustainability policy and board committee overseeing it
- Annual corporate governance reports submitted via FRCN online portal

**Scope of Application:** All public listed entities, private holding companies of public entities, regulated private companies, concessioned/privatized companies. Other private companies encouraged to adopt.

**Monitoring:** FRC through sectoral regulators and registered exchanges; sanctions for deviations.

### 1.2 CBN Corporate Governance Guidelines for Banks 2023

- **Full Title:** Corporate Governance Guidelines for Commercial, Merchant, Non-Interest, Payment Services Banks and Financial Holding Companies in Nigeria
- **Issuing Body:** Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN)
- **Date Issued:** July 13, 2023
- **Effective Date:** August 1, 2023
- **Official PDF:** https://www.cbn.gov.ng/Out/2023/FPRD/Circular%20and%20Guidelines%20for%20Corporate%20Governance.pdf
- **PwC Analysis:** https://www.pwc.com/ng/en/assets/pdf/2023-cbn-governance-guidelines.pdf
- **Mondaq Analysis:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1437172/key-highlights-of-the-cbns-code-of-corporate-governance-guidelines-for-banks-and-financial-holding-companies-in-nigeria
- **Replaces:** CBN Code of Corporate Governance for Banks and Discount Houses 2014 (effective October 1, 2014)

**Board Composition:**
- Commercial/Merchant/Non-Interest Banks (CMNIBs): minimum 7, maximum 15 directors
- Payment Service Banks (PSBs): minimum 7, maximum 13 directors
- NEDs must outnumber EDs
- INEDs: minimum 3 for large commercial/merchant/national non-interest banks; minimum 2 for regional/PSBs
- At least 2 NEDs (one must be INED) with IT/fintech/cybersecurity expertise
- Maximum 2 members of same extended family on board
- Only 1 family member in MD/CEO, Chairman, or ED roles simultaneously

**Mandatory Board Committees (all chaired by INEDs):**
- Board Audit Committee (NEDs only)
- Board Nomination & Governance Committee
- Board Remuneration Committee
- Board Credit Committee (for CMNIBs)
- ICT & Cybersecurity Committee (for PSBs)
- Quarterly meetings minimum for board and all committees

**Tenure Limits:**
- MD/CEO and Executive Directors: maximum 12 cumulative years
- NEDs: maximum 12 years (3 terms of 4 years)
- INEDs: maximum 8 years (2 terms of 4 years)
- External auditors: 10 consecutive years maximum; engagement partner rotation every 5 years

**Cool-Off Periods:**
- ED to NED (same bank): 2 years
- NED to executive role (same bank): 2 years
- ED to Financial Holding Company board: 2 years

**Key Officer Requirements:**
- Executive Compliance Officer (ECO): one ED designated; cannot combine with income-generating activities
- Chief Compliance Officer: General Manager rank (major banks); AGM rank (regional/merchant)
- Company Secretary: outsourcing prohibited; direct reporting to Board
- Internal Shariah audit function for non-interest banks

**Ownership Rules:**
- No controlling interest in more than one bank without CBN approval
- 5%+ equity holdings require CBN prior approval
- Government holdings capped at 10%; must divest within 5 years

**Sanctions:** Monetary penalties, suspension (initial 6 months), board removal for directors/officers

### 1.4 Companies and Allied Matters Act 2020 (CAMA 2020)

- **Full Title:** Companies and Allied Matters Act 2020
- **Enacted:** August 7, 2020, signed by President Muhammadu Buhari
- **Replaces:** CAMA 1990 (as amended 2004)
- **Relevance:** Foundation statute for all corporate governance in Nigeria

**Key Governance Provisions:**

| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Section 275 | Every company (except small companies) must have at least 2 directors |
| Section 275(3) | Public companies must have at least one-third of total directors as independent directors |
| Section 286 | Chairman of public company cannot also serve as CEO |
| Section 307 | Directors restricted to maximum 5 boards of public entities simultaneously |
| Section 404 | Statutory Audit Committee requirements |
| Various | Directors' duties: honesty, good faith, best interests of company, reasonable care and skill |

**Audit Committee Under CAMA 2020 (Section 404):**
- Composition: 5 members -- 3 shareholders and 2 non-executive directors (changed from 6 under CAMA 2004)
- Members must be financially literate; at least one must be a member of a professional accounting body
- Elected annually at AGM
- Shareholders may nominate other shareholders via written nomination 21 days before AGM
- Powers: review external auditor reports, examine audited financial statements

**Directors' Duties:**
- Duty of care, diligence, and skill
- Duty to act honestly and in good faith in the best interests of the company
- Duty to disclose interests
- Duty not to make personal profit from position
- Prospective directors must disclose participation on other boards

**Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/shareholders/1024130/the-companies-and-allied-matters-act-2020-what-you-need-to-know--part-12-directors-under-the-cama-2020

### 1.5 Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act 2020 (BOFIA 2020)

- **Full Title:** Banks and Other Financial Institutions Act 2020
- **Signed:** November 13, 2020 by President Muhammadu Buhari
- **Replaces:** BOFIA 1991 (as amended)
- **Relevance:** Primary banking regulation statute; grants CBN expanded supervisory powers

**Key Governance Provisions:**
- Section 12: Power to revoke banking licenses (used in Heritage Bank case)
- Section 33: CBN authority to conduct special examinations/investigations of banks
- CBN examiners may attend bank management and board meetings in observatory capacity
- Expanded CBN powers over bank insolvency and restructuring
- Strengthened regulatory oversight framework

**Reference:** https://nairametrics.com/2020/11/18/bofia-2020-forward-or-backward-step-for-nigerian-banks/

### 1.6 SEC Corporate Governance Guidelines and Directives

- **Full Title:** SEC Corporate Governance Guidelines (SCGG) + SEC Code of Corporate Governance for Public Companies
- **Issuing Body:** Securities and Exchange Commission, Nigeria
- **SEC Guidelines Portal:** https://sec.gov.ng/our-mandate/regulation/guidelines/
- **Code PDF:** https://www.sec.gov.ng/files/CODE%20OF%20CORPORATE%20GOVERNANCE%20FOR%20PUBLIC%20COMPANIES.pdf

**SEC Corporate Governance Scorecard:**
- Hybrid structure: 75% mandatory provisions, 25% voluntary provisions
- Annual submission signed by board members and senior management
- Due by January 31 each year
- Assesses compliance with SEC code and NCCG 2018

**SEC 2025 Director Tenure Circular (June 19, 2025):**
- Directors: maximum 10 consecutive years in single company; 12 years within same group
- INEDs prohibited from being appointed as EDs (including CEO) within same company/group
- Former CEOs/EDs: 3-year cool-off period before eligible for Chairman appointment; then maximum 4-year chairmanship
- Guidance Note issued July 1, 2025 with practical application details

**Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1643034/regulatory-update-sec-issues-new-corporate-governance-directives-to-curb-boardroom-rotations-and-preserve-independence

### 1.8 PenCom Corporate Governance Guidelines for Pension Fund Operators 2021

- **Full Title:** Guidelines on Corporate Governance for Pension Fund Operators
- **Issuing Body:** National Pension Commission (PenCom)
- **Date:** February 2021
- **Official PDF:** https://www.pencom.gov.ng/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/Guidelines-on-Corporate-Governance-for-Pension-Fund-Operators-February-2021.pdf
- **Applicable to:** Licensed Pension Fund Administrators (PFAs) and Pension Fund Custodians (PFCs)
- **Replaces:** Code of Corporate Governance for Licensed Pension Operators 2008

**Key Provisions:**
- Chairman and CEO roles must be separated
- Minimum non-executive director representation equal to executive members
- At least one independent director with no company relationships
- Independent directors: no employment with company/related entities for 3 years; no 5%+ equity
- Annual formal board evaluations; results submitted to PenCom with governance reports
- Disclosure of PFA-PFC relationships where shared ownership/directorship
- Spouses of pension operators cannot work at PenCom
- Principles: accountability, responsibility, transparency, integrity

**Reference:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/shareholders/1471344/understanding-corporate-governance-for-licensed-pension-operators-in-nigeria

## 4. NIGERIAN CASE STUDIES

### 4.1 The 2009 CBN Banking Crisis -- CEO Sackings

- **Year:** 2009
- **Organizations:** Intercontinental Bank, Oceanic Bank, Union Bank, FinBank, Afribank (first 5); Bank PHB, Spring Bank (later additions); total of 8 banks
- **Key Actors:** CBN Governor Sanusi Lamido Sanusi
- **Source:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2009/08/cbn-and-the-sack-of-bank-managements/

**Summary:** On August 14, 2009, CBN Governor Sanusi sacked CEOs of 5 banks after examinations revealed insolvency. Banks had aggregate NPL ratio of 40.81%, held approximately 30% of Nigeria's total deposits, and received N620 billion bailout (2.5% of 2010 GDP). Sanusi declared "the era of one-man bank is over in Nigeria" and implemented maximum 10-year CEO tenure.

**Teaching Points:**
- How weak board oversight enabled CEO imperialism and self-dealing
- Importance of independent non-executive directors and board committees
- Role of CBN as systemic risk regulator with power to remove directors
- Link between corporate governance failures and financial system stability
- How regulatory intervention (Prompt Corrective Action) works in practice

### 4.2 Oceanic Bank / Cecilia Ibru Fraud Case

- **Year:** 2009 (removal), 2010 (conviction)
- **Organizations:** Oceanic Bank Plc
- **Key Actors:** Cecilia Ibru (MD/CEO), Sanusi Lamido Sanusi (CBN Governor)
- **Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cecilia_Ibru; https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2010/10/9/jail-term-for-ex-nigeria-bank-boss

**Summary:** Cecilia Ibru, Nigeria's first female bank CEO, was removed by CBN on August 14, 2009 for fraud and mismanagement. Convicted October 8, 2010 on three counts: loans beyond credit limits, falsified accounts, N20bn loans without due process. Sentenced to 18 months; forfeited assets worth N191.4 billion. Original 25 charges reduced to 3 via plea bargain.

**Teaching Points:**
- Directors' duty of care and fiduciary obligations
- Criminal liability for governance breaches
- Insider lending and related-party transaction risks
- Asset recovery and forfeiture mechanisms
- Importance of regulatory fit-and-proper assessments

### 4.3 Intercontinental Bank / Erastus Akingbola

- **Year:** 2009
- **Organizations:** Intercontinental Bank Plc (later merged into Access Bank)
- **Key Actors:** Erastus Akingbola (MD/CEO)
- **Source:** https://www.theafricareport.com/378683/erastus-akingbola-nigerias-longest-running-corruption-case/

**Summary:** Akingbola sacked by CBN for inadequate corporate governance, poor credit administration, and failure to adhere to credit risk management. Bank had N87.6 billion in non-performing loans. NPL ratios for rescued banks ranged from 19% to 48%. Akingbola fled to UK; charges were instituted. Intercontinental Bank ultimately sold to Access Bank.

**Teaching Points:**
- Consequences of CEO domination of board
- Credit risk governance failures
- Cross-border enforcement challenges
- Merger/acquisition as resolution mechanism
- How governance failures destroy shareholder value

### 4.4 Cadbury Nigeria Accounting Scandal

- **Year:** 2006 (discovered), 2006-2007 (investigation/sanctions)
- **Organizations:** Cadbury Nigeria Plc, Akintola Williams Deloitte (auditors)
- **Key Actors:** Bunmi Oni (CEO), Mr. Akadiri (Finance Director)
- **Source:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262300127_Corporate_Fraud_in_Nigeria-_A_Two_Case_Study

**Summary:** Cadbury Nigeria overstated accounts by N13 billion (approximately $85 million) over 2003-2006. SEC identified concerns: declining profitability, worsening leverage, deteriorating cash flow, inadequate disclosure, non-compliance with governance code, and obtaining loans to pay dividends. CEO and Finance Director sacked by Cadbury Schweppes UK and banned from Nigerian capital market, financial services employment, and public company directorships. Auditors (Akintola Williams Deloitte) fined $130,000.

**Teaching Points:**
- Board's responsibility for financial reporting integrity
- Role of audit committee in preventing creative accounting
- External auditor independence and accountability
- SEC enforcement powers and market sanctions
- Relationship between parent company governance and subsidiary oversight

### 4.5 Oando PLC / SEC Corporate Governance Dispute

- **Year:** 2017-2021
- **Organizations:** Oando Plc, Securities and Exchange Commission
- **Key Actors:** Adewale Tinubu (CEO), Omamofe Boyo (Director), SEC
- **Source:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2019/06/corporate-governance-lapses-sec-bars-oando-ceo-for-5-years/

**Summary:** In 2017, two shareholders petitioned SEC alleging governance lapses and mismanagement at Oando. SEC appointed Deloitte & Touche for forensic audit. In May 2019, SEC barred CEO Tinubu and Director Boyo for 5 years, suspended AGM indefinitely, and proposed interim management. Oando challenged orders in court. After 4 years, SEC and Oando reached settlement in the interest of shareholders.

**Teaching Points:**
- Shareholder activism and minority shareholder rights
- SEC investigation and enforcement powers
- Forensic audit as governance tool
- Tension between regulatory intervention and corporate autonomy
- Settlement vs. litigation in governance disputes

### 4.6 Heritage Bank License Revocation

- **Year:** 2024
- **Organizations:** Heritage Bank Plc, NDIC
- **Key Actors:** CBN Governor
- **Source:** https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/corporate-governance/1477538/avoiding-corporate-missteps-key-questions-answered-from-heritage-banks-license-revocation

**Summary:** On June 3, 2024, CBN revoked Heritage Bank's banking license under Section 12 of BOFIA 2020. Over 80% of loans were non-performing (approximately N490 billion by early 2024). Bank suffered chronic financial mismanagement, governance failures, and persistent liquidity challenges with no reasonable prospect of recovery. NDIC appointed as liquidator. Action taken to protect financial system stability and public confidence.

**Teaching Points:**
- Ultimate consequence of governance failure: license revocation
- BOFIA 2020 powers for license revocation
- Role of NDIC in bank resolution and depositor protection
- How non-performing loans signal governance breakdown
- Board's duty to maintain financial viability

### 4.7 Skye Bank to Polaris Bank (Bridge Bank)

- **Year:** 2016 (board removal), 2018 (license revocation and bridge bank)
- **Organizations:** Skye Bank Plc, Polaris Bank (bridge bank)
- **Source:** https://www.icirnigeria.org/skye-bank-takeover-a-journey-from-afribank-to-polaris-bank/

**Summary:** In July 2016, CBN forced resignation of Chairman, all NEDs, MD, DMD, and two longest-serving EDs due to corporate governance lapses and failure to meet prudential ratios. Despite intervention, shareholders could not recapitalize. On September 21, 2018, CBN revoked Skye Bank's license and established Polaris Bank as bridge bank to assume assets/liabilities. AMCON injected N786 billion and was directed to sell Polaris Bank.

**Teaching Points:**
- Progressive regulatory intervention: board removal before license revocation
- Bridge bank as resolution mechanism
- AMCON's role in financial system stability
- Board's failure to maintain capital adequacy
- Consequences of governance lapses: from board removal to liquidation

### 4.8 FBN Holdings Boardroom Power Struggle

- **Year:** 2021-present
- **Organizations:** FBN Holdings Plc, First Bank of Nigeria
- **Key Actors:** Oba Otudeko (former Chairman), Femi Otedola (current Chairman), Hassan-Odukale
- **Source:** https://www.premiumtimesng.com/business/business-news/766321-sacked-from-fbn-holdings-board-in-2021-oba-otudeko-plots-return.html

**Summary:** CBN dissolved FBN Holdings board in 2021 over governance concerns. Otudeko (chairman 2012-2021) removed. Otedola acquired 5%+ stake in October 2021, sparking power struggle with Hassan-Odukale. Otudeko returned July 2023 via Barbican Capital (13.3% stake), displacing Otedola as largest shareholder. CBN introduced 5% acquisition limit for financial holding companies in July 2023 guidelines.

**Teaching Points:**
- Shareholder activism and controlling interest battles
- CBN's power to dissolve boards for governance failures
- 5% acquisition threshold and regulatory approval requirements
- Intersection of ownership structure and governance quality
- How board disputes affect institutional stability

### 4.9 Titan Trust Bank / Union Bank Scandal

- **Year:** 2024
- **Organizations:** Titan Trust Bank, Union Bank, African Export-Import Bank (Afreximbank)
- **Key Actors:** Godwin Emefiele (former CBN Governor), Jim Obazee (investigator)
- **Source:** https://techcabal.com/2024/01/10/cbn-sacks-board-of-directors/

**Summary:** In January 2024, CBN dissolved boards of Titan Trust Bank, Union Bank, Keystone Bank, and Polaris Bank. Investigation alleged former CBN Governor Emefiele used Dubai-based proxies to establish Titan Trust Bank and orchestrate Union Bank acquisition. $300 million Afreximbank facility allegedly secured using Union Bank's own assets as collateral. Court later ruled CBN's dissolution of Union Bank board was ultra vires under BOFIA 2020.

**Teaching Points:**
- Conflict of interest at the highest regulatory level
- Related-party transactions and self-dealing
- Due diligence in bank acquisitions
- Limits of regulatory power: judicial review
- Proxy ownership and beneficial ownership transparency

### 4.10 NGX Delisting Cases

- **Year:** 2021-2025 (ongoing)
- **Organizations:** Capital Oil Plc, Goldlink Insurance, Medview Airline, Standard Alliance Insurance, Niger Insurance, Tourist Company of Nigeria, and others
- **Source:** https://www.thisdaylive.com/2024/07/22/in-major-clean-up-ngx-delists-14-companies-for-non-compliance/

**Summary:** Between 2021 and 2024, dozens of companies were delisted from NGX for governance and compliance failures. In July 2024 alone, 14 companies were delisted for non-compliance. Common failures: non-submission of audited financial statements (some for 6+ years), failure to meet post-listing requirements on financial disclosures and corporate governance, and inadequate trading activity. N18 billion in shareholder investments affected by some delistings.

**Teaching Points:**
- Consequences of governance failures for capital market access
- Importance of timely financial reporting and disclosure
- NGX listing requirements and post-listing compliance
- Impact on minority shareholders when governance fails
- Board responsibility for regulatory compliance

### 4.11 Insurance Sector Governance Failures

- **Year:** 2012, 2021-2024
- **Organizations:** Alliance and General Insurance, A&G Life Assurance, Niger Insurance, Standard Alliance Insurance
- **Source:** https://www.vanguardngr.com/2021/06/naicom-to-sanction-insurance-firms-for-non-implementation-of-corporate-governance-guidelines/

**Summary:** In 2012, NAICOM suspended Alliance and General Insurance and A&G Life for 6 months over governance abuses including non-rendition of accounts, misrepresentation, and non-disclosure. NAICOM later withdrew licenses of Niger Insurance and Standard Alliance Insurance. In 2021, NAICOM warned all insurers of sanctions for failure to implement corporate governance guidelines.

**Teaching Points:**
- Sector-specific governance requirements and enforcement
- NAICOM's supervisory approach
- Link between governance failures and insurance sector performance
- Progressive sanctions: warning, suspension, license withdrawal

### 4.12 Pension Sector Governance Issues

- **Year:** 2010-ongoing
- **Organizations:** Police Pension Fund, Pension Reform Task Team
- **Key Actors:** Abdulrasheed Maina (former Task Team head)
- **Source:** https://www.academia.edu/57526324/An_Analysis_of_Police_Pension_Fraud_and_the_Future_of_Pension_Administration_in_Nigeria

**Summary:** Over 258,000 fake pensioners discovered in police pension system, resulting in N5 billion monthly payouts against N1 billion actual need. Pension Reform Task Team under Maina initially saved N151 billion but faced accusations of mismanaging N21 billion. Unauthorized bank accounts and fraudulent payments discovered. The reform led to the Pension Reform Act 2014 and establishment of PenCom governance framework.

**Teaching Points:**
- Governance of public sector pension funds
- Internal controls and fraud prevention
- Regulatory response: from crisis to institutional reform
- PenCom's role in pension fund oversight
- Fit-and-proper requirements for pension operators

## 6. PROFESSIONAL BODY REQUIREMENTS

### 6.1 CIoD Nigeria (Chartered Institute of Directors)
- **Requirement Type:** Voluntary professional certification; director development
- **Membership Grades:** Associate Member, Full Member, Fellow, Life Member
- **CPD:** Director development programmes
- **Mandatory Topics:** Board dynamics, board effectiveness, governance principles, ethical leadership
- **Assessment:** Open programme participation; evaluation-based progression
- **Fees:** Regular membership N680,000; Life membership N2,180,000

### 6.2 ICSAN
- **Requirement Type:** Professional qualification (Chartered Secretary)
- **CPD:** Ongoing professional development required
- **Mandatory Topics:** Corporate governance, company law, compliance, administration
- **Assessment:** Professional examinations (only ICSAN-authorized exams in Nigeria)

### 6.3 CBN Fit-and-Proper Requirements
- **Requirement Type:** Mandatory for bank directors
- **Assessment:** CBN approval required for all bank board appointments
- **Mandatory Topics:** Must demonstrate competence; at least 2 NEDs must have IT/fintech/cybersecurity expertise
- **Ongoing:** Annual compliance returns; CBN can remove directors for cause

### 6.4 SEC Director Fitness Requirements
- **Requirement Type:** Mandatory for directors of public/listed companies
- **Key Requirements:** Maximum 10 consecutive years per company; disclosure of other directorships; independent director criteria

## 7. RECOMMENDED MODULE STRUCTURE

### Module 1: Governance Frameworks for Nigerian Financial Services (5 lessons)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Explain the multi-layered corporate governance regulatory framework in Nigeria
- Distinguish between the NCCG 2018, sector-specific codes, and enabling legislation
- Apply the "Apply and Explain" compliance philosophy
- Compare Nigerian governance standards with international benchmarks (OECD, King IV)

**Lesson Topics:**
1. **Why Corporate Governance Matters in Financial Services** -- Systemic risk, depositor protection, and the cost of governance failures (2009 banking crisis, Heritage Bank case)
2. **The Nigerian Corporate Governance Architecture** -- NCCG 2018 structure (7 parts, 28 principles); relationship between FRCN, CBN, SEC, NAICOM, PenCom codes; hierarchy and interaction
3. **CAMA 2020 and BOFIA 2020 -- The Legal Foundation** -- Directors' duties under CAMA; CBN supervisory powers under BOFIA; statutory audit committee requirements
4. **Sector-Specific Governance Codes** -- CBN 2023 Guidelines for banks; NAICOM CGGIRC 2021 for insurance; PenCom 2021 Guidelines for pension operators; MFB code
5. **International Standards and Comparisons** -- OECD Principles; King IV (South Africa); UK Corporate Governance Code; how Nigeria benchmarks globally

**Source Basis:** NCCG 2018, CAMA 2020, BOFIA 2020, CBN 2023 Guidelines, NAICOM CGGIRC 2021, PenCom Guidelines 2021, Chambers Partners Nigeria 2025, ACCA/KPMG rankings

### Module 2: Board Effectiveness and Composition (5 lessons)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Design a board structure that complies with Nigerian regulatory requirements
- Apply director fit-and-proper criteria and independence tests
- Evaluate board performance using established methodologies
- Manage board dynamics, succession planning, and diversity

**Lesson Topics:**
1. **Board Composition -- Getting the Mix Right** -- ED/NED/INED ratios by sector (CBN: majority NEDs, minimum 3 INEDs for large banks); chairman independence; family restrictions; IT expertise requirements
2. **Director Qualifications, Tenure, and Independence** -- CAMA 2020 limits (5 boards max); CBN tenure limits (12 years ED, 8 years INED); SEC 2025 directive (10 years per company); cool-off periods; independence criteria
3. **Director Duties, Liabilities, and Protections** -- Fiduciary duties; criminal liability (Ibru case); disqualification; D&O insurance; conflict of interest management
4. **Board Evaluation and Effectiveness** -- NCCG 2018 annual evaluation requirements; external evaluation every 3 years; CBN requirements; practical evaluation methodologies
5. **Board Dynamics, Diversity, and Succession** -- Chairman-CEO relationship; gender diversity; generational transition; induction programmes; continuous development; the FBN Holdings case study

**Source Basis:** CBN 2023 Guidelines, CAMA 2020, NCCG 2018, SEC 2025 Circular, Ajogwu textbook, case studies (Ibru, Akingbola, FBN Holdings)

### Module 3: Board Committees and Oversight Structures (4 lessons)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Establish and operate the four mandatory board committees
- Implement effective risk oversight, audit, and compliance frameworks
- Structure committee composition, terms of reference, and reporting lines
- Navigate the dual audit committee structure (statutory + board)

**Lesson Topics:**
1. **Audit Committee -- Statutory and Board Requirements** -- CAMA 2020 statutory audit committee (5 members: 3 shareholders, 2 NEDs); CBN board audit committee (NEDs only, chaired by INED); external auditor rotation (10 years); financial literacy requirements; the Cadbury Nigeria lesson
2. **Risk Management Committee and Enterprise Risk Framework** -- NCCG 2018 risk management principle; CBN requirement for Enterprise Risk Management framework; risk appetite and tolerance; operational risk, credit risk, market risk oversight
3. **Nomination, Governance, and Remuneration Committees** -- CBN requirements (all chaired by INEDs); director appointment procedures; remuneration disclosure; succession planning; CBN approval for bank director appointments
4. **Specialized Committees** -- Board Credit Committee (CMNIBs); ICT & Cybersecurity Committee (PSBs); Shariah committee (non-interest banks); whistleblowing framework; Executive Compliance Officer role

**Source Basis:** CBN 2023 Guidelines, CAMA 2020 Section 404, NCCG 2018 Part 2, Cadbury Nigeria case, CBN sanctions data

### Module 4: Risk Oversight and Regulatory Compliance (4 lessons)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Implement risk governance frameworks compliant with CBN and NCCG requirements
- Navigate capital adequacy and prudential compliance as board responsibilities
- Manage regulatory relationships and supervisory interactions
- Apply anti-money laundering and financial crime governance obligations

**Lesson Topics:**
1. **Capital Adequacy and Prudential Oversight** -- Board responsibility for capital management; CBN recapitalization 2024-2026 (N500bn international, N200bn national, N50bn regional); Basel alignment; NPL monitoring; Prompt Corrective Action framework
2. **AML/CFT and Financial Crime Governance** -- Board-approved AML policies; Executive Compliance Officer requirements; CBN sanctions for AML violations ($10.7M in fines in 2024); whistleblowing obligations; KYC governance
3. **Regulatory Engagement and Supervisory Response** -- CBN examination powers; observatory status at board meetings; quarterly compliance returns; SEC scorecard submissions; FRCN annual governance reports; regulatory correspondence management
4. **Crisis Management and Resolution** -- License revocation process (Heritage Bank); bridge bank mechanism (Skye/Polaris); AMCON role; NDIC liquidation; board's duty during financial distress; the Titan Trust/Union Bank judicial review

**Source Basis:** BOFIA 2020, CBN recapitalization circular 2024, Heritage Bank case, Skye/Polaris case, Titan Trust case, CBN sanctions data, NDIC publications

### Module 5: Stakeholder Management and Emerging Governance Issues (4 lessons)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Manage relationships with shareholders, regulators, employees, and other stakeholders
- Implement ESG and sustainability governance frameworks
- Navigate digital governance, cybersecurity oversight, and fintech regulation
- Build a culture of compliance and ethical conduct

**Lesson Topics:**
1. **Shareholder Rights and Engagement** -- NCCG 2018 Part 3 (3 principles on shareholders); minority shareholder protection; AGM conduct; related-party transactions; the Oando case; SEC governance scorecard
2. **ESG, Sustainability, and Climate Governance** -- NCCG 2018 sustainability principle; FRC Sustainability Reporting Roadmap 2024; IFRS S1 and S2 adoption timeline; Nigerian Sustainable Banking Principles; board sustainability committee; NGX disclosure requirements
3. **Digital Governance, Cybersecurity, and Technology Oversight** -- CBN ICT & Cybersecurity Committee requirement for PSBs; board IT expertise requirements; data protection (NDPA 2023 linkage); fintech governance; AI governance emerging issues
4. **Ethics, Compliance Culture, and Continuous Improvement** -- NCCG 2018 Part 4 (business conduct and ethics); building compliance culture; company secretary role; corporate governance reporting; continuous professional development for directors

**Source Basis:** NCCG 2018 Parts 3-6, FRC Sustainability Roadmap, SEC scorecard, CBN 2023 Guidelines, Oando case, NGX delisting data
